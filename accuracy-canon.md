## Core Technology by Era

For each book division, the specific circuits/physics to teach and the key numeric values/formulas to hold constant. Notation follows the **Notation & Units** section.

### Prologue — The Physics Before Radio (1864–1900)
- **Maxwell's equations** — electricity, magnetism, and light unified as one EM field; waves propagate at the speed of light.
- **The wave-speed relation, c = f·λ** — the single load-bearing formula of the whole book; keep visible whenever prose switches between "meters" (period) and "kHz/MHz" (modern). Working value **c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s**; exact **c = 299,792,458 m/s**.
- **Resonance and the LC tank circuit** — an inductor and capacitor exchanging energy at f₀ = 1/(2π·√(LC)); the building block of every tuned circuit that follows.
- **Hertz's apparatus as a damped LC oscillator** radiating via a dipole, received by a second resonant loop — the tuning/resonance principle a generation before it became engineering.
- **Coherer detector** (Branly) — metal filings that drop resistance under RF; a crude, non-linear, binary on/off detector needing mechanical reset. No gain, no fidelity.
- **Marconi's grounded antenna / earth return** — the ancestor of the ground-plane monopole.
- **No modulation yet** — everything is on/off keying of a damped carrier.

### Chapter 1 — The Spark Era (1900–1917)
- **Spark-gap transmitter / LC "tank"** — a charged capacitor dumps energy through a spark gap into a tuned circuit, radiating trains of exponentially decaying ("damped") oscillations, not a continuous wave.
- **Damped-wave spectrum is inherently broad** — each decaying pulse occupies wide bandwidth; a root *technical* cause of the era's interference (not just operator misbehaviour).
- **Crystal detector** — the galena "cat's-whisker" (Pickard, ~1906) supersedes the coherer: passive rectification, more sensitive, still no gain.
- **Q (quality factor)** sets selectivity; spark circuits were fundamentally low-Q — the technical logic behind eventually mandating narrower CW.
- **Ground-wave propagation** dominates at the long wavelengths favoured by commercial/military stations; shorter waves were (wrongly) believed useless for distance.
- **Numeric anchor: 200 m ⇔ 1500 kHz (1.5 MHz)**, via λ(m) = 300 / f(MHz). Hold this constant whenever "the 200-meter law" appears.

### Chapter 2 — The Shortwave Revolution (1919–1927)
- **Triode vacuum tube** (De Forest Audion, 1906) — the enabling active amplifying/oscillating device for the whole era.
- **Regeneration** — feeding amplified plate signal back to the grid in phase boosts gain and selectivity, and, pushed further, drives self-oscillation (sensitive receiver *or* CW oscillator).
- **CW vs. damped spark** — CW is a pure, un-decaying keyed sine carrier occupying far less bandwidth than spark; inaudible until heterodyned.
- **Heterodyne principle / BFO** — mixing incoming CW with a local oscillator yields an audible beat at the difference frequency (Fessenden, ~1901).
- **Superheterodyne** — mix incoming RF with an LO to a fixed IF, then amplify/filter/detect: f_IF = |f_RF − f_LO|. Decouples gain/selectivity from tuned frequency.
- **MOPA (master-oscillator power-amplifier)** — isolate a stable low-power oscillator from the PA to stop frequency "pulling" under load.
- **Hartley** (tapped inductor) and **Colpitts** (tapped capacitor) LC feedback oscillators — the two canonical 1920s topologies.
- **Ionospheric skip and the skip zone** — sky-wave refraction off the ionosphere (Kennelly–Heaviside 1902; Appleton 1924–26) carries shortwave far beyond ground-wave range, with a dead skip zone between. **Numeric anchor: 110 m ⇔ ~2.73 MHz** (first transatlantic two-way band).

### Chapter 3 — The Golden Age of Tubes (1928–1936)
- **Superheterodyne becomes dominant**, displacing TRF designs.
- **Image frequency** — the superhet's structural weakness: a spurious response at f_LO ± f_IF on the far side of the LO (equivalently f_image = f_RF ± 2·f_IF); front-end selectivity and IF choice govern image rejection.
- **IF (intermediate frequency)** — where most gain/selectivity concentrates; era IFs commonly **175–500 kHz**.
- **AGC (automatic gain control)** — detector/IF feedback that cuts gain on strong signals; standard by the mid-1930s.
- **AM via plate modulation** — audio varies the PA plate supply; output is carrier plus two sidebands, occupied bandwidth **≈ 2 × highest audio frequency** (~6 kHz total for 3 kHz audio).
- **Pentode tubes** (screen + suppressor grids, ~1927–1930) — much higher gain, reducing the need for neutralization.
- **Neutralization** — a capacitive-bridge feedback-cancelling circuit that nulls plate-to-grid capacitance to stop parasitic self-oscillation in RF power amplifiers.
- **Pierce crystal oscillator** — a quartz crystal replaces the LC inductor for far higher frequency stability (high effective Q); the era's gold standard for staying "on frequency."

### Chapter 4 — Eve of War (1937–1945)
- **Class-C amplifier** — biased well beyond cutoff, conducting under 180° per cycle; **~60–80%+ efficiency** at gross nonlinearity; ideal for constant-envelope CW/FM, workhorse PA of the era.
- **Pi-network (π-network) output matching** — matches a wide impedance range while filtering harmonics; displaces link-coupled tanks as the standard output stage.
- **Crystal (IF) filters** — quartz bandpass filters in the IF strip for very sharp, high-Q selectivity; forerunner of postwar mechanical/crystal-ladder filters.
- **Yagi–Uda beam** — parasitic director/reflector array (Uda/Yagi 1926); practical at VHF where element size is small.
- **HF skip vs. VHF line-of-sight** — the push to 5 m (56–60 MHz) and the WERS ~2½ m band (**112–116 MHz** and 219–225 MHz) forces a near-line-of-sight propagation model. (Do not project postwar troposcatter theory onto pre-1945 operators.)
- **FM fundamentals** — information carried by frequency deviation, not amplitude; Armstrong's wideband FM trades bandwidth for noise immunity and capture-effect behaviour. Through 1945 FM is overwhelmingly a *broadcast* and WERS-VHF technology — general amateur-band FM is a postwar development.

### Chapter 5 — Postwar (1945–1960)
- **SSB generation — filter vs. phasing method**, both starting from a **balanced modulator** producing double-sideband suppressed carrier (DSBSC).
  - *Filter method*: a sharp bandpass filter (crystal-lattice or Collins mechanical, at IFs from **455 kHz to ~9 MHz**) removes one sideband; suppression **40–60 dB**, independent of audio frequency, but the filter is expensive.
  - *Phasing method*: two balanced modulators fed carrier and audio in 90° quadrature; summing cancels one sideband. Cheaper, but suppression only **20–30 dB** without careful trimming.
- **Balanced modulator** — multiplies carrier × audio, passing both sidebands but cancelling the carrier by symmetry.
- **Product detector** — receive-side counterpart: mixes the suppressed-carrier IF against a local BFO to recover audio (an AM envelope detector cannot).
- **BJT common-emitter amplifier** — the workhorse small-signal stage enabled by the 1947 transistor: base-emitter input, collector-emitter output, moderate input impedance, inverted output, both voltage and current gain. Tubes still dominate high-power HF stages into the 1960s–70s.
- **Anchor rig:** Collins KWM-1 (1957), first purpose-built SSB/CW transceiver, ~100 W PEP.

### Chapter 6 — Space (1961–1976)
- **FM detail.** *Deviation* Δf: US wideband amateur FM traditionally **±5 kHz** (20 kHz channel spacing); post-2013 narrowbanding **±2.5 kHz** on 12.5 kHz spacing.
- **Carson's rule** — BW ≈ 2(Δf + f_m). For Δf = 5 kHz, f_m = 3 kHz → **BW ≈ 16 kHz** (consistent with 20 kHz VHF spacing).
- **Capture effect** — an FM receiver locks to the stronger co-channel signal once it leads by ~**2–3 dB**, a consequence of the limiter/discriminator stripping amplitude.
- **Discriminator** — the FM demodulator (classically Foster-Seeley or ratio detector; later PLL/DSP).
- **Repeater architecture** — receive on one frequency, retransmit on another; **standard 2 m offset ±600 kHz**. **CTCSS** sub-audible tone (**~67–254 Hz**) gates access and prevents mutual triggering.
- **Satellite linear transponders** (OSCAR 6/7 Mode A/B) relay a whole spectrum slice, allowing many simultaneous SSB/CW QSOs.
- **Doppler shift** — Δf ≈ f₀·(v/c), v the line-of-sight (range-rate) component; LEO speed ~**7.5 km/s**, giving several kHz of shift on a 2 m uplink (proportionally more at 70 cm) — hence "tune the downlink, not the uplink."
- **Numeric anchor:** OSCAR 1 mass **10.0 kg (22 lb)** (see Resolved Uncertainties for the 10-vs-5 kg discrepancy).

### Chapter 7 — Microprocessor (1977–1990)
- **PLL frequency synthesis** — a VCO generates the RF; a programmable ÷N divider (microprocessor-controlled) divides it down; a phase detector compares against a stable reference crystal and steers the VCO until locked. Changing N yields thousands of precise channels from one crystal: **f_VCO = N·f_ref**.
- **AX.25 packet protocol** — amateur derivative of X.25.
  - *Framing*: HDLC-style (flag, address, control, PID, information, FCS).
  - *Addressing*: callsign + **SSID** (0–15).
  - *Error detection*: 16-bit **CRC-CCITT** as the Frame Check Sequence.
  - *Error recovery*: **ARQ** (connected-mode acknowledge/retransmit).
  - *Modulation*: VHF **1200-baud AFSK, Bell 202** (1200/2200 Hz tones) over FM; HF ~**300-baud, Bell 103**.
- **Digipeaters** relay AX.25 frames hop-by-hop via a path listed in the frame's address field.
- **AMTOR** — a teleprinter mode (5-bit Baudot-derived, 4-of-7 error-detecting code) in FEC or ARQ mode; amateur adaptation of maritime SITOR (ITU-R 476). Bridges Ch6/Ch7.

### Chapter 8 — Digital / SDR (1991–Present)
- **SDR/DSP fundamentals.**
  - *I/Q sampling*: digitize two mixer outputs 90° apart (In-phase, Quadrature), preserving amplitude and phase/sign so all demodulation happens in software.
  - *Direct conversion*: mix RF straight to baseband (0 Hz) with an LO at the target frequency (SDR-1000, SoftRock class); simpler hardware, but DC-offset/LO-leakage must be DSP-corrected.
  - *FFT waterfall*: stack successive FFT spectra over time to locate weak/drifting signals visually across wide spectrum.
- **PSK31** — **31.25-baud** BPSK, **~31 Hz** bandwidth; **Varicode** variable-length characters (common letters shorter, like Morse) with self-synchronizing zero-run inter-character gaps.
- **FT8** — **8-FSK** (79 symbols, 3 bits each), **~50 Hz** bandwidth (8 × 6.25 Hz); payload 77 info + 14-bit CRC = 91 bits protected by a **(174,91) LDPC** code; 15-second timed cycles; decodes at roughly **−20 to −24 dB SNR** in 2500 Hz (range, not a single figure).
- **Digital voice vocoders** — D-STAR, DMR, and System Fusion digitize voice with a low-bit-rate vocoder (commonly proprietary **AMBE/AMBE+2**) then modulate: D-STAR **GMSK**; DMR **4-level FSK, 2-slot TDMA** (2 voice slots on 12.5 kHz); Fusion **C4FM**. Trades FM's graceful degradation for a sharp digital "cliff edge."
- **Modern references only** — no owned Handbook anchors this era; cite modern sources and the public-domain FCC Part 97 rules.

## Notation & Units

One consistent style for the whole book. Frequencies are in hertz (with kHz/MHz as convenient); wavelength in metres. Frequency is lowercase **f**; specific frequencies take subscripts (f_RF, f_LO, f_IF). Greek letters: λ (lambda) wavelength, Δ (delta) a change/deviation, β (beta) FM modulation index, π (pi) 3.14159…. Powers of ten use ×10ⁿ. Ohm's law is written **E = IR** (E for EMF/voltage).

## Glossary

Canonical one-sentence definitions. These are binding; a chapter may expand but must not contradict them.

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Spark gap | An air gap across which a high-voltage discharge dumps stored capacitor energy into a tank circuit, exciting a burst of damped oscillations. |
| Damped wave | A carrier that rings down (decays exponentially) after each excitation rather than persisting, inherently occupying broad bandwidth. |
| Coherer | An early binary RF detector of loose metal filings that cohere (drop resistance) under a signal and must be tapped to reset. |
| Crystal detector | A passive point-contact semiconductor rectifier (e.g. galena and a "cat's-whisker" wire) that demodulates RF without gain. |
| Continuous wave (CW) | A pure, un-decaying sinusoidal carrier switched on and off for Morse keying, occupying far less bandwidth than spark. |
| Regeneration | Positive feedback of amplified output back to a tube's grid in phase, raising gain and selectivity and, taken further, causing oscillation. |
| Heterodyne | Mixing two frequencies to produce sum and difference outputs; the basis of both CW reception and the superheterodyne. |
| BFO (beat-frequency oscillator) | A local oscillator whose output beats against an incoming CW/SSB signal to produce an audible or intelligible tone. |
| Superheterodyne | A receiver that mixes the incoming signal with a local oscillator to a fixed intermediate frequency where most gain and selectivity live. |
| Intermediate frequency (IF) | The fixed frequency, after the mixer, at which a superheterodyne concentrates its amplification and filtering. |
| Image frequency | A spurious superhet response at the LO's opposite side (f_LO ± f_IF) that a poorly selective front end lets through. |
| MOPA | Master-Oscillator Power-Amplifier: a stable low-power oscillator buffered from the PA so antenna loading cannot pull its frequency. |
| Hartley oscillator | An LC feedback oscillator using a tapped inductor to set the feedback ratio. |
| Colpitts oscillator | An LC feedback oscillator using a tapped (divided) capacitor to set the feedback ratio. |
| Pierce oscillator | A Colpitts-derived oscillator with a quartz crystal replacing the inductor, giving very high frequency stability. |
| Crystal control | Using a quartz crystal's mechanical resonance to fix a transmitter's frequency far more stably than an LC tank. |
| Q (quality factor) | The dimensionless sharpness of a resonant circuit, Q = f₀ / BW, governing selectivity and losses. |
| Neutralization | A feedback-cancelling network that nulls a tube's plate-to-grid capacitance to prevent parasitic self-oscillation in an RF amplifier. |
| AGC (automatic gain control) | Feedback that reduces receiver gain on strong signals to level output and prevent overload. |
| AM (amplitude modulation) | Impressing audio on a carrier by varying its amplitude, producing a carrier plus two sidebands. |
| Plate modulation | AM produced by varying the high-voltage plate supply of the RF power-amplifier stage. |
| Pentode | A five-electrode tube adding screen and suppressor grids to a triode for higher gain and reduced neutralization need. |
| Class-C amplifier | An amplifier biased beyond cutoff, conducting under half a cycle, offering high efficiency but only for constant-envelope modes. |
| Pi-network | A π-shaped LC output-matching network that matches a wide impedance range while filtering harmonics. |
| Crystal (IF) filter | A quartz-based bandpass filter in the IF strip giving very sharp, high-Q selectivity. |
| Yagi–Uda antenna | A directional beam using parasitic reflector and director elements alongside a driven element. |
| Ionospheric skip | Sky-wave propagation in which shortwave signals refract off the ionosphere back to earth far beyond the horizon. |
| Skip zone | The dead annulus between a signal's ground-wave limit and its first sky-wave return, where no reception occurs. |
| FM (frequency modulation) | Impressing information on a carrier by varying its instantaneous frequency rather than amplitude. |
| Deviation | The peak amount an FM carrier's frequency swings above and below centre in response to modulation. |
| Carson's rule | An estimate of FM occupied bandwidth, BW = 2(Δf + f_m), capturing ~98% of the signal's power. |
| Capture effect | An FM receiver's tendency to reproduce only the stronger of two co-channel signals once it leads by a few dB. |
| Discriminator | An FM demodulator that converts instantaneous frequency deviation back into an audio voltage. |
| SSB (single sideband) | A bandwidth- and power-efficient mode transmitting one sideband of an AM signal with the carrier and other sideband suppressed. |
| Balanced modulator | A circuit that multiplies carrier and audio to output both sidebands while cancelling the carrier by symmetry (DSBSC). |
| Product detector | A receive-side mixer that demodulates a suppressed-carrier SSB/CW signal against a local carrier/BFO. |
| Filter method | SSB generation removing the unwanted sideband with a sharp bandpass (crystal or mechanical) filter. |
| Phasing method | SSB generation cancelling the unwanted sideband by combining two 90°-quadrature balanced-modulator outputs. |
| BJT common-emitter | The workhorse transistor amplifier stage giving both voltage and current gain with inverted output. |
| Repeater | An automatic station that receives on one frequency and simultaneously retransmits on another to extend range. |
| CTCSS | A sub-audible continuous tone (~67–254 Hz) carried on the signal to control repeater access and prevent co-channel triggering. |
| Transponder | A satellite payload that receives an uplink band and retransmits it, shifted, on a downlink band (linear transponders relay a whole slice). |
| Doppler shift | The frequency offset Δf = f·v/c caused by a satellite's line-of-sight motion relative to the ground station. |
| PLL (phase-locked loop) | A control loop that locks a VCO to a divided reference, enabling programmable frequency synthesis. |
| VCO | A voltage-controlled oscillator whose output frequency is steered by a control voltage, the tunable element of a PLL. |
| AX.25 | The amateur packet-radio link-layer protocol, an HDLC-framed derivative of X.25 addressed by callsign + SSID. |
| Digipeater | A digital repeater that relays AX.25 packet frames hop-by-hop along a path listed in the frame. |
| SSID | The 0–15 Secondary Station Identifier suffix distinguishing multiple logical stations under one callsign. |
| AMTOR | An amateur teleprinter mode with 4-of-7 error-detecting coding, run in FEC or ARQ, adapted from maritime SITOR. |
| ARQ | Automatic Repeat reQuest: an error-recovery scheme that acknowledges good data and retransmits what was not acknowledged. |
| TNC (Terminal Node Controller) | The modem-plus-controller that assembles and disassembles AX.25 packets between a computer and a radio. |
| I/Q | The In-phase and Quadrature (90°-apart) baseband pair that jointly preserves a signal's amplitude and phase for DSP. |
| Direct conversion | An SDR/receiver architecture mixing RF straight to 0 Hz baseband in one step rather than via an IF. |
| FFT waterfall | A scrolling time-vs-frequency display built from successive Fast Fourier Transforms of sampled data. |
| SDR (software-defined radio) | A radio in which demodulation, filtering, and mode decoding are done in software/DSP on digitized I/Q rather than fixed analog hardware. |
| PSK31 | A 31.25-baud BPSK keyboard mode ~31 Hz wide using Varicode, designed for efficient live HF conversation. |
| Varicode | A variable-length character code (common letters shorter) with self-synchronizing gaps, used by PSK31. |
| FT8 | A weak-signal 8-FSK mode on timed 15-second cycles with LDPC coding, decodable well below the noise floor. |
| LDPC | Low-Density Parity-Check: a powerful forward-error-correction code (FT8 uses a (174,91) codeword). |
| Vocoder | A low-bit-rate voice codec (e.g. AMBE/AMBE+2) that compresses speech for digital-voice modes before framing and modulation. |

**Glossary term count: 60 terms.**

## Resolved Uncertainties

The four research-flagged facts, each closed to the value the book will use, with its source. No open uncertainty markers remain in this canon; where a source conflict could not be fully resolved, the conservative reading is stated and used.

### 1. Restoration of US amateur transmitting after the WWI blackout — **1 October 1919**
Receiving privileges were restored first, by Navy Department order effective **15 April 1919**; transmitting privileges were restored separately, **1 October 1919** (the October 1919 *QST* "BAN OFF" supplement). A looser ARRL centennial reference to "November 1919" is a rounding of the same fall-1919 reopening; the book uses **1 October 1919** for transmitting and 15 April 1919 for receiving.
Sources: earlyradiohistory.us ("Amateur Radio After World War One"; "Amateur Radio Restored, 1919"); ARRL centennial (looser "November 1919" reference).

### 2. US availability dates of the three WARC-79 bands — **30 m: 28 Oct 1982; 12 m: 1985; 17 m: 31 Jan 1989**
WARC-79 created the allocations internationally (in force 1 Jan 1982); US access came on three separate later dates. **30 m** (10.100–10.150 MHz): FCC-authorized **28 October 1982** (US access is secondary, CW/digital only). **12 m** (24.890–24.990 MHz): the year **1985** is corroborated across sources; the exact month/day is not pinned, so the book cites the year alone. **17 m** (18.068–18.168 MHz): FCC 89-18, Docket 88-467, R&O adopted 27 Jan and released **31 January 1989**; the book cites the document release date (some secondary sources say "30 January"), so use **31 January 1989**.
Sources: Wikipedia (WARC bands); FCC (Docket 88-467 / FCC 89-18); aggregated ham-history summaries for the 30 m/12 m dates.

### 3. Incentive Licensing — FCC Docket 15928; R&O released 29 Aug 1967; effective 22 Nov 1968; ARRL co-originated the concept
The binding regulatory act was purely the FCC's: **Docket No. 15928**, R&O **released 29 August 1967** (FCC 67-978; 9 FCC 2d 814), phased in over ~two years, with **22 November 1968** the most-cited effective date for the core subband reallocation. Origin is not clean-cut: ARRL proposed a version of incentive licensing in an October 1963 FCC filing and helped develop the program with the FCC from 1964 — so the flat framing "FCC-initiated, not an ARRL program" is an oversimplification. The book states it precisely: only the FCC could and did promulgate the rule (Docket 15928), but ARRL originated and advocated the concept, which is why community anger fell on ARRL as well as the FCC. The program was strongly contentious. (A single secondary source's "Phase II, 22 Nov 1969" could not be corroborated and is treated as an anniversary artifact; the book does not assert a distinct 1969 phase as fact.)
Sources: Eastern Massachusetts ARRL ("A History of Amateur Radio License Changes"; 9 FCC 2d 814); ac6v.com (effective 22 Nov 1968); Wikipedia ("Amateur radio licensing in the United States"); OneTubeRadio.com.

### 4. IARU founding — **17 April 1925, Paris; Hiram Percy Maxim first International President**
The International Amateur Radio Union was formed when delegates of 23 nations met at the Faculté des Sciences in Paris for the First International Amateur Radio Congress; the constitution was adopted **17 April 1925** (congress 14–18 April; closing plenary 18 April, now marked as World Amateur Radio Day). Preliminary planning occurred in March 1924. Maxim (u1AW) was elected first International President.
Sources: ARRL ("World Amateur Radio Day: IARU Formed 100 Years Ago"); ETHW ("International Amateur Radio Union").
