## 8. The Digital, Networked, and Software-Defined Era (1991–present)

*"Encouragement and improvement of the amateur service through rules which provide for advancing skills in both the communication and technical phases of the art."*
*— 47 CFR § 97.1(c), Basis and Purpose of the Amateur Radio Service*

### The Story

By 1990 the amateur transceiver had already absorbed the microprocessor: synthesizers were digital, memories were digital, even the S-meter was often a digital approximation of an analog needle. But the radio itself — the mixers, filters, and detectors that touched the actual RF — was still built from discrete analog stages, the same MOPA-to-product-detector lineage that ran back to the 1950s. The next quarter-century would dissolve that boundary entirely. By the 2010s a serious HF station might contain a single wideband analog-to-digital converter, a USB cable, and a laptop doing everything else — mixing, filtering, demodulating, decoding — in software. The radio didn't just gain a computer. In the most literal engineering sense, the computer became the radio.

The era opens, fittingly, not with a circuit but with a regulatory door swinging open on 14 February 1991.
**FACT:** FCC eliminates the Morse requirement for the Technician class — the "no-code Technician."
For sixty-plus years, a five-, thirteen-, or twenty-word-per-minute code test had stood between an aspiring ham and every privilege above the entry-level Novice license. The no-code Technician kept the code requirement for HF operating privileges but let a new licensee reach the VHF/UHF bands — repeaters, packet, satellites — on a written exam alone. The hobby's demographics shifted almost immediately: license counts, which had been flat or declining through the late 1980s, began climbing again as the barrier to entry dropped from "learn a language spoken only in dots and dashes" to "pass a multiple-choice test on rules and basic theory." Technician quickly became, and has remained since, the single largest license class in the United States.

That surge showed up most visibly on 2 meters and 70 centimeters — the VHF/UHF FM repeater bands the no-code Technician license had opened outright — and the hardware market followed the demand. Handheld FM transceivers, which had been a premium accessory through the 1980s, became commodity items over the following two decades; by the 2010s, low-cost dual-band handhelds imported from Chinese manufacturers (Baofeng's UV-5R being the best-known example) sold for a small fraction of what an equivalent Japanese-made handheld had cost a generation earlier, putting a working 2 m/70 cm radio within reach of anyone who had just passed the Technician written exam. The economics ran in both directions at once: a lower licensing barrier created a larger entry-level market, and a larger entry-level market pulled hardware prices down further still, each reinforcing the other in a way the code-test era, with its slower, more selective pipeline of new operators, had never produced.

The code requirement itself did not disappear all at once, and its final removal was as much an international story as a domestic one. The ITU's Radio Regulations had long required Morse proficiency for any amateur license granting HF privileges — a treaty-level mandate, not merely a national one, embedded in International Radio Regulation 25.5. That changed in 2003, when the World Radiocommunication Conference in Geneva (WRC-03) amended RR 25.5 to make Morse testing optional at each national administration's discretion, handing every ITU member the choice of whether to keep testing code at all. Nations moved at their own pace after that — some (the UK among the earliest) dropped code testing within months; others held on for years out of a mix of tradition and genuine belief in Morse's operational value in poor conditions. The FCC took its time, running a full notice-and-comment rulemaking rather than acting unilaterally, and the American change did not take effect until the very end of the decade that had opened it.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** The code question exposed a real fault line inside the hobby, not just between generations but between propagation environments. In regions and eras where SSB and FM ruled uncontested — good antennas, reliable line power, benign QRN — code testing looked like a hazing ritual with no operational payoff. But CW's minimal bandwidth and a trained ear's ability to copy a signal buried well below the noise floor of any voice mode made Morse the mode of last resort for genuine DXers, emergency communicators, and QRP (low-power) operators working continents on a few watts. The WRC-03 decision didn't settle that argument; it just removed the treaty obligation to have it settled by regulation rather than by choice. CW traffic on the bands did not vanish after mandatory testing ended — if anything, once nobody had to learn it to get on HF, the people who still learned it did so because they wanted to, and CW activity on contest weekends into the 2020s remained as intense as ever.

**FACT:** FCC ends all Morse code testing for US amateur licenses (R&O in Fed. Reg. 24 Jan 2007).
The Report and Order closing out Morse testing in the United States was published in the Federal Register on 24 January 2007 and took effect on 23 February 2007. From that date, a US ham could hold an Amateur Extra-class license — the top of the license ladder, with access to every amateur HF subband — without ever having copied a single word of code. The old five-word-per-minute Element 1 exam, itself already reduced from the 13 and 20 wpm tests of the Incentive Licensing era, was simply gone. ARRL, which had internally been divided on the question for years, did not fight the change; by 2007 the argument that code testing filtered for operator quality had lost most of its constituency inside the League's own leadership. The rule change did not touch the bands, the power limits, or the emission privileges themselves — it touched only the door through which new hams walked in, and after 2007 that door was, for the first time since the 1920s, a door with no dots or dashes on it at all.

While the regulatory door was widening, a much quieter revolution was happening on the operating desk: the sound card. Every PC sold from the mid-1990s onward included, more or less for free, a stereo analog-to-digital and digital-to-analog converter running at 8 or 11 or 44.1 kHz — precisely the kind of general-purpose digitizing hardware that, a decade earlier, would have required a dedicated modem or TNC. Hams noticed almost immediately that a sound card, driven by the right software, could demodulate and generate arbitrary audio-frequency waveforms, and that an SSB transceiver — whose IF chain cares only about the audio passband it passes through, not what's encoded in it — was an ideal front end for whatever digital mode that software implemented. AMTOR and RTTY had proven the concept with dedicated TNCs in the 1970s and 1980s; the sound card made it a software problem, and software problems get solved fast by people who already know how to code.

Peter Martinez, G3PLX — the same operator who had built AMTOR's first amateur implementation on a Motorola 6800 microcomputer back in 1978 — took the sound-card idea and designed a mode purpose-built for it. Martinez introduced PSK31 to the amateur community in December 1998, with working soundcard software following in 1999. PSK31 was narrow, forgiving of modest signal strength, and — critically — fast enough for live, typed conversation, unlike the slow mechanical teleprinters of the RTTY era. Martinez published the mode's specification openly rather than patenting it, and within a year of the software reaching the community, PSK31 signals were audible in dense little clusters at the bottom of every HF digital subband, dozens of QSOs running in a bandwidth that would have held perhaps two or three RTTY signals. It became, for a while, the definitive proof that a soundcard and free software could out-innovate a decade of dedicated-hardware digital modes.

Parallel to the software-mode boom, the underlying receiver architecture itself was being rebuilt from first principles. Gerald Youngblood, AC5OG, founded FlexRadio Systems in the early 2000s around a radical premise: instead of a superheterodyne chain with an analog mixer, IF filter, and detector, build a receiver whose only analog stage is a quadrature (I/Q) mixer feeding a PC sound card, and do every remaining receiver function — filtering, AGC, demodulation, even the "dial" itself — in software running on the host computer. The result, in 2003, was the FlexRadio SDR-1000 — the first commercially available fully software-defined amateur transceiver. The SDR-1000 shipped with its control software, PowerSDR, released under the GPL — an open-source amateur transceiver control stack years before "open source" was a familiar phrase in ham circles. Hobbyist SDR spread fastest, though, through much cheaper kits: the SoftRock series of direct-conversion receiver (and later transceiver) kits, designed initially by Tony Parks, KB9YIG, sold for a fraction of a commercial rig's price and put the quadrature-sampling architecture within reach of anyone with a soldering iron and about forty dollars.

The final piece of the SDR-goes-mainstream story arrived from an unlikely direction: cheap DVB-T television tuner dongles built around the Realtek RTL2832U chip, sold for digital TV reception in Europe. Between roughly 2010 and 2012, RTL-SDR was popularized: Antti Palosaari and other Osmocom-project developers discovered that the RTL2832U chip, in one of its operating modes, could be coaxed into streaming raw 8-bit I/Q samples straight off its ADC rather than the demodulated TV signal it was designed to output — turning a nine-dollar TV stick into a wideband software-defined receiver covering roughly 24 MHz to 1.7 GHz. The `rtl-sdr` driver that resulted, and the flood of free SDR software that grew up around it, put a usable (if receive-only, and far from pristine) SDR front end in the hands of anyone who could order a dongle online. For the first time, the barrier to trying software-defined radio wasn't the cost of a transceiver — it was the cost of a USB stick.

None of this hardware would have mattered without a software ecosystem willing to treat radio as just another programmable signal-processing problem. GNU Radio, an open-source software toolkit begun in the early 2000s, gave anyone with a general-purpose computer a library of DSP building blocks — filters, mixers, modulators, demodulators — that could be wired together into a working receiver or transmitter as a graphical flowgraph, entirely independent of what hardware happened to be feeding it I/Q samples. The same openness showed up across the amateur SDR world: PowerSDR's GPL license on the FlexRadio side, the free `rtl-sdr` driver and its downstream applications (GQRX, SDR#, and dozens more) on the RTL-SDR side, and WSJT-X itself, released as open source, on the digital-mode side. A ham who wanted to understand exactly how a demodulator worked no longer needed a schematic and an oscilloscope; the demodulator was source code, readable, modifiable, and shareable at the speed of a `git clone` rather than the speed of ordering parts and winding coils.

Digital modes weren't confined to text and CW-adjacent signaling; voice went digital too, though far less uniformly than the industry hoped. In 2001 the Japan Amateur Radio League (JARL) published D-STAR, the first digital-voice standard designed specifically for amateur use rather than adapted from a commercial land-mobile system, and Icom brought it to market with the first commercial D-STAR radio a few years later. Two more digital-voice ecosystems followed on different technical foundations: DMR, imported wholesale from the commercial land-mobile-radio world (and thus benefiting from an enormous installed base of cheap surplus and new-production hardware), and Yaesu's proprietary System Fusion, introduced in 2013, using C4FM 4-level FSK. None of the three digital-voice systems was compatible with the others, an outcome familiar to anyone who had watched the 1950s "sideband wars" or the early-1980s TNC-format squabbles play out — new technology, same tribal fracturing along vendor lines.

That incompatibility spawned its own workaround culture. Because D-STAR, DMR, and Fusion all ultimately reduce to a compressed bitstream riding on RF, and because the internet doesn't care which vocoder produced the bits it's carrying, hobbyists built small, cheap "hotspot" devices — a low-power RF modem (commonly a DVMega or openSpot board) paired with a Raspberry Pi — that let a single ham, indoors, with no repeater in range at all, key up on DMR or D-STAR or Fusion from a handheld radio and have the hotspot relay the digitized voice over home Wi-Fi to a worldwide server network. DMR's server side coalesced around a free, volunteer-run backbone called Brandmeister, which by the mid-2010s linked thousands of DMR repeaters and hotspots into shared "talkgroups" — a single logical channel that might carry a regional net, a national conversation, or a worldwide roundtable, selectable from a radio's channel dial the way a broadcast listener picks a station. D-STAR's equivalent reflector network and Fusion's WIRES-X system did the same job on their own islands. The RF hop from handheld to hotspot might be a few meters; the "contact" that resulted could span the planet — digital voice's version of the packet-radio digipeater network from Chapter 7, except the backbone was now the public internet rather than a chain of amateur VHF hops.

Digital voice's real force multiplier, though, wasn't the vocoder — it was the internet. Dave Cameron, VE7LTD, launched the Internet Radio Linking Project (IRLP) in November 1997 with its first three nodes installed. IRLP let a repeater anywhere with an internet-connected node computer bridge its RF traffic to another repeater anywhere else in the world, turning a local VHF FM repeater into a global conversation venue over ordinary DTMF-controlled node selection. EchoLink, launched a few years later, did much the same thing but for individual stations rather than just repeater-to-repeater links, letting a licensed ham reach the network from a PC or smartphone app as well as from RF. Jonathan Taylor, K1RFD, launched EchoLink in 2002, adding PC-to-repeater VoIP linking to the mix. Digital voice standards like D-STAR and Fusion built internet reflectors and gateways into their protocols from the start, so a Fusion repeater in Ohio could link, via the internet backbone rather than any HF or VHF propagation, to a Fusion repeater in Japan. The distinction between "radio contact" and "internet-relayed radio contact" became, for many operators, a matter of personal ethics and contest rules rather than a technical wall — the RF link at each end was real amateur radio; the backbone joining them was not, and how much that mattered was a debate that ran through ham forums for two decades without ever fully resolving.

Two more networked-digital threads matured alongside voice linking, both descended directly from the AX.25 packet-radio work of Chapter 7 rather than from the sound-card revolution. Winlink, built around the ARQ-mode PACTOR modem protocol (itself a descendant of AMTOR's error-correcting logic) and later a software-only VARA mode, let a ham send and receive genuine internet email over HF or VHF radio alone — composing a message offline, connecting briefly to a Winlink gateway station over RF, and exchanging store-and-forward traffic with no internet connection of the sender's own. Emergency-communications volunteers adopted it as a lifeline precisely because it worked when the internet and phone networks it ultimately touched did not, so long as one gateway station somewhere still had a wire to the world. APRS (the Automatic Packet Reporting System), developed by Bob Bruninga, WB4APR, used the same AX.25 packet framing not for point-to-point messages but for a constant, low-rate broadcast of position, telemetry, and short text — a moving car's GPS fix, a weather station's temperature, a repeater's status — digipeated hop-by-hop across a metro area and, via internet-gateway stations, aggregated onto a public worldwide map. Neither Winlink nor APRS needed a soundcard trick or an SDR; both were proof that the AX.25 packet infrastructure built in the 1980s still had headroom for entirely new applications the TAPR founders had never specifically designed it for.

The single most disruptive development of the era, though, arrived almost without warning in the summer of 2017, and it had been twenty years in the making. Joseph Taylor, K1JT, a Princeton astrophysicist who shared the 1993 Nobel Prize in Physics for the discovery (with Russell Hulse) of the first binary pulsar, had spent much of his amateur-radio career after that discovery applying the same statistical, weak-signal-extraction instincts that had found a pulsar's timing signature buried in radio-telescope noise to the much smaller problem of finding a fellow ham's callsign buried in HF noise. His WSJT software suite — "Weak Signal communication by K1JT" — had already produced a string of specialized modes years before FT8: WSPR (Weak Signal Propagation Reporter), a slow, automated beacon mode that let low-power stations map propagation openings without any human at the keyboard at all; and JT65 and JT9, aimed at extreme weak-signal work — JT65 at moonbounce (Earth-Moon-Earth), where a signal arrives back from the Moon attenuated by an almost unbelievable path loss, and JT9 at pulling readable text out of the crowded, low-signal conditions of the LF, MF, and HF bands. Each of those modes established the same basic pattern FT8 would later popularize on ordinary HF: fixed timed intervals, a small structured payload, and a decoder willing to trade operating spontaneity for raw sensitivity. FT8 was the point where that lineage of specialist, edge-case weak-signal tools met the everyday HF operating desk. Taylor, working with Steven Franke, K9AN, released it on 29 June 2017.
**FACT:** FT8 released in WSJT-X by Joe Taylor (K1JT) and Steve Franke (K9AN); becomes the most-used digital mode within ~2 years.
FT8 was designed for one specific, narrow job: making a minimal, verifiable contact — callsigns, grid squares or signal reports, a confirmation — under conditions too marginal for a human ear to copy anything at all, and doing it fast enough that a single QSO fit inside a fixed fifteen-second time slot. Within months of release it dominated the HF digital subbands so completely that longtime operators of PSK31, RTTY, and even SSB itself began complaining, half-seriously, that FT8 had "killed" conversational HF operating — a QSO that once might have run five minutes of actual back-and-forth chat now completed in under a minute of scripted, computer-generated exchanges, with the operator often doing little more than clicking to confirm a proposed contact. Whether that was a loss of the hobby's soul or simply an evolution of it — most-used digital mode within roughly two years of release, by any reasonable count — became one of the defining arguments of HF operating in the following decade.

FT8's sensitivity found a particularly natural audience in the amateur QRP (low-power) community, whose whole operating philosophy — working the world on a few watts rather than a legal-limit kilowatt — had always been fundamentally a fight against the noise floor. A QRP station running five watts into a modest antenna had, for a century, been at a structural disadvantage against any mode requiring a human ear to copy a readable signal; FT8's roughly −20 to −24 dB SNR decode threshold changed that calculus substantially, because the software's sensitivity advantage over a human ear was worth far more effective watts than any realistic antenna or power upgrade could have bought. Operators who had spent decades treating five watts as a self-imposed handicap found themselves working the same DX, at the same distances, that a hundred-watt SSB station worked — not because the physics of propagation had changed, but because the demodulator had.

Award programs and contests, which had spent a century adapting their rules to each new mode from spark to SSB to RTTY, absorbed FT8 the same way: awkwardly at first, then structurally. DXCC (the ARRL's premier country-counting award) had already long since accepted digital modes generally, so an FT8 contact counted the same as a CW or SSB one; contest sponsors had a harder time, since FT8's own fixed exchange format didn't map cleanly onto a contest's usual signal-report-plus-serial-number exchange, and the mode's automation raised uncomfortable questions about how much of the "contact" a human operator had actually made versus how much the software had made on their behalf. The community's own answer, a few years after FT8's release, was a faster, contest-oriented sibling mode built on the same LDPC and timed-slot foundations but with shorter cycles and a leaner exchange — evidence that the FT8 architecture itself, not just the specific mode, had become a template other modes were built on top of rather than a one-time novelty.

### How It Works

Everything in this chapter traces back to one idea: instead of building a chain of analog stages — mixer, IF filter, detector, each a physical circuit with its own tolerances and drift — digitize the RF (or an intermediate frequency derived from it) as early as possible and do the rest in software. Understanding how that's done, and why it works, requires understanding **I/Q sampling**, the **direct-conversion architecture**, the **FFT waterfall**, and then the two weak-signal digital modes — PSK31 and FT8 — that made the software radio's real payoff visible to every operator on the bands.

**I/Q sampling.** A conventional superheterodyne mixer, as taught since Chapter 2, takes the incoming RF and a local-oscillator signal and produces sum and difference frequencies: $f_{IF} = |f_{RF} - f_{LO}|$. But a single real-valued mixer output cannot tell you the *sign* of that difference — whether the incoming signal sat above or below the LO — nor can it preserve the instantaneous phase of the signal, only its magnitude at each mixed frequency. Both of those pieces of information (magnitude and phase, or equivalently amplitude and sign) turn out to be exactly what you need to demodulate anything more sophisticated than plain CW or AM. The SDR answer is to build *two* mixers fed the same RF but with local oscillators 90° apart in phase — one called the **in-phase (I)** channel, the other the **quadrature (Q)** channel — and digitize both simultaneously. In one sentence, this is I/Q sampling:
**FACT:** digitize two mixer outputs 90° apart (In-phase, Quadrature), preserving amplitude and phase/sign so all demodulation happens in software.

Think of the RF signal at each instant as a rotating vector (a phasor) with some amplitude and some phase angle. The I mixer, multiplying by a cosine LO, samples the projection of that phasor onto one axis; the Q mixer, multiplying by a sine LO (90° shifted), samples its projection onto the perpendicular axis. Together, the pair $(I, Q)$ at every sample instant is a complete, lossless snapshot of the signal's instantaneous amplitude and phase — not just its magnitude, as a single real mixer would give, but its full complex representation. Once you have that pair as a stream of numbers in a computer, everything downstream — filtering, demodulating AM or SSB or FM or any digital mode, even resolving which sideband of the LO a signal came from — is pure arithmetic on two number streams. No further analog circuitry is required. This is the load-bearing idea of the entire chapter: **I/Q is what lets amplitude and phase both survive digitization**, and once both survive, software can reconstruct anything the original signal contained.

I/Q sampling also buys something more mundane but just as important: sampling efficiency. A real-valued (single-channel) digitizer must, by the Nyquist sampling theorem, sample at more than twice the highest frequency present in the signal or it aliases — folds higher frequencies down on top of lower ones, corrupting the spectrum irrecoverably. But when the mixer has already brought a signal of some occupied bandwidth $BW$ down to baseband as a *complex* $(I, Q)$ pair rather than a single real waveform, the same information can be captured with a sample rate only a little above $BW$ itself, not $2 \times BW$ — because the complex representation already separates positive and negative frequencies that a real signal would otherwise fold on top of each other. A modest sound card sampling I and Q at 44.1 kHz each can therefore cover a receive passband nearly 44 kHz wide, not the roughly 22 kHz a single real channel at the same sample rate would allow. That factor-of-two efficiency is a large part of why an ordinary stereo sound card — one real channel for I, one for Q — turned out to be exactly the right piece of consumer hardware for direct-conversion SDR.

**Direct conversion versus direct sampling.** The simplest SDR receiver architecture skips the intermediate frequency altogether and mixes the incoming RF straight down to baseband — to 0 Hz — with a local oscillator set right at the frequency of interest. This is exactly the direct-conversion approach used by the FlexRadio SDR-1000 and the SoftRock kits: an RF amplifier (or sometimes no amplifier at all) feeds a quadrature mixer pair, the I and Q outputs are low-pass filtered to remove anything above the audio range, and a stereo sound card (or a dedicated ADC) digitizes both channels. It is about as simple as a receiver front end can be — no IF filter to design, no image-frequency problem in the classical superheterodyne sense, because there is no IF for a spurious response to land on.

The tradeoff is that direct conversion inherits its own weaknesses. Any DC offset in the mixer or amplifier chain shows up as a spurious signal sitting exactly at 0 Hz — right in the middle of the receive passband, where a real signal is most likely to be tuned — and LO energy that leaks back into the RF input (LO leakage) produces the same kind of self-interference. Both problems are fixable, but not in analog hardware the way a superhet's image problem is fixed with front-end selectivity; they are fixed with DSP: measuring and subtracting the DC offset, and calibrating out the leakage term, entirely in software, exactly because the offending signal is now just a number in a buffer rather than a voltage on a wire. This is the general shape of the SDR bargain throughout the era — architectures get radically simpler in hardware and radically more capable in software, and the burden of correcting the hardware's remaining imperfections moves from copper and ferrite to code.

Direct conversion isn't the only way to build an SDR front end, and by the later part of the era it wasn't even the dominant one in higher-end gear. **Direct (RF) sampling** skips the analog mixer altogether: a single very fast analog-to-digital converter — running at tens or hundreds of megasamples per second, well above the Nyquist rate for the entire HF spectrum at once — digitizes the raw antenna voltage directly, with no frequency translation done in analog hardware at all. Everything a mixer would have done — selecting a particular slice of that spectrum, translating it to baseband, producing the I and Q pair — is instead done numerically, after the fact, by a digital down-converter: a piece of DSP logic (commonly running in an FPGA for speed) that multiplies the raw sample stream by a numerically generated cosine and sine at the frequency of interest and then filters and decimates the result down to a manageable rate. Where direct conversion trades one analog mixer stage for two, direct sampling removes the analog mixer from the receive chain entirely — the wideband ADC is the only frequency-sensitive analog component left, and its own imperfections (clock jitter, quantization noise, spurious responses) become the new limiting factor in place of the DC-offset and LO-leakage problems that plague direct conversion. Direct-sampling architectures need considerably more raw ADC performance and DSP horsepower than direct conversion — the price of moving the frequency-selection job entirely into the digital domain — but by the mid-2010s that hardware had become cheap enough that direct-sampling transceivers reached ordinary amateur price points rather than remaining a laboratory-grade luxury.

**The FFT waterfall.** A single spectrum snapshot — amplitude versus frequency at one instant, computed by a Fast Fourier Transform of a block of I/Q samples — tells you what's on the band right now. Stack successive snapshots vertically, newest at the top (or bottom, by convention), and color each amplitude value by intensity, and you get a scrolling two-dimensional image with frequency across one axis and time down the other: the waterfall display. A steady carrier becomes a bright, unbroken vertical line drifting slowly if the transmitting station's oscillator drifts; a PSK31 signal becomes a narrow, textured trace with visible phase transitions; an FT8 transmission becomes a train of short, evenly-spaced tone bursts. The waterfall turned "finding a weak signal" from a slow manual dial-scan — the operator's ear doing the FFT, one narrow slice of spectrum at a time — into something the eye could do across an entire subband in a glance, spotting a trace far too weak or too briefly present to ever have been found by ear-and-dial searching.

An FFT computed over $N$ samples taken at sample rate $f_s$ divides that sample rate into $N$ equally spaced frequency bins, so each bin is $f_s / N$ hertz wide — the finer the desired frequency resolution, the more samples (and so the more time) each FFT block must span. This is an unavoidable tradeoff, not a software limitation: a longer block resolves closely spaced signals better but blurs together anything that changes within that block's time span, while a shorter block tracks fast changes but smears nearby signals into the same bin. WSJT-X's own choices make the tradeoff concrete: PSK31 software typically runs a relatively short FFT to keep up with its fast 31.25-baud symbol rate and a comparably wide per-bin tolerance, while FT8's decoder analyzes the entire 15-second, fixed-duration receive interval as essentially one long block, buying very fine frequency resolution — well under the mode's own 6.25 Hz tone spacing — precisely because FT8 has no fast keyboard-typing timing constraint to keep up with.

**PSK31: BPSK and Varicode.** PSK31 encodes each bit of information not as a frequency shift (as AFSK and RTTY do) but as a 180° reversal of the carrier's phase — binary phase-shift keying (BPSK). At the mode's fixed **31.25 baud** symbol rate, phase reversals occur (or don't, for a repeated bit) exactly 31.25 times per second, and because a BPSK signal's occupied bandwidth is set essentially by its symbol rate, PSK31 occupies only about **31 Hz** — narrow enough that dozens of simultaneous PSK31 conversations fit in the same spectrum a single voice signal would need.

PSK31, in sum:
**FACT:** A 31.25-baud BPSK keyboard mode ~31 Hz wide using Varicode, designed for efficient live HF conversation.
But a fixed 31.25-baud bit stream needs a character encoding, and here Martinez borrowed the oldest idea in the book — literally the same idea behind the Morse code the FCC had just stopped requiring. Rather than encode every character with the same number of bits (as ASCII does, 8 bits regardless of frequency of use), PSK31 uses **Varicode**: common letters like E and T get short bit patterns, rare letters and punctuation get long ones, exactly mirroring Morse's own dot-heavy codes for E and T. Varicode has no fixed character length, so it needs a way to mark where one character ends and the next begins without a separate clock signal; it solves this by guaranteeing that no valid Varicode character contains two consecutive zero bits internally, and by using a run of two zero bits as the inter-character gap. A receiver decoding the bit stream can therefore find character boundaries by watching for that double-zero run — a self-synchronizing scheme, needing no separate timing channel, using the statistical structure of the code itself as the synchronization signal.

{{fig:psk31}}

> **Worked example:** How many simultaneous PSK31 QSOs fit in the space of one 2.8 kHz SSB voice channel? PSK31's occupied bandwidth is about 31 Hz per signal (per the canon's PSK31 bandwidth figure). Divide: $2800 \text{ Hz} / 31 \text{ Hz} \approx 90$. In principle, roughly ninety non-overlapping PSK31 signals could be packed into the spectrum a single SSB voice conversation occupies — though in practice operators leave guard space between signals and don't pack a band edge-to-edge, so real-world PSK31 subbands hold a few dozen simultaneous QSOs rather than the theoretical ninety. The comparison nonetheless captures why PSK31 became, within a year of release, the densest conversational activity anywhere on the HF digital subbands.

**FT8: structured slots, 8-FSK, and LDPC.** Where PSK31 optimizes for live typed conversation, FT8 optimizes for something almost opposite: reliably completing the *minimum* exchange needed to log a valid contact, under signal conditions where a human operator — or even PSK31's BPSK decoder — would hear nothing but noise. Every FT8 transmission is locked to a fixed, GPS- or network-time-synchronized **15-second** slot; stations alternate transmitting and receiving in strict lock-step, and a full exchange (callsigns, grid locators or signal reports, acknowledgment, 73) typically takes four to six of those slots — roughly a minute, largely automated, with the operator often only approving a proposed exchange rather than typing free text.

In the canon's shorthand, FT8 is 8-FSK, 79 symbols carrying 3 bits each, occupying BW ≈ 8 × 6.25 Hz ≈ 50 Hz, protected by a (174,91) LDPC code, run in 15-second cycles. Within each 15-second slot, FT8 sends 79 symbols using 8-tone frequency-shift keying: eight discrete, evenly-spaced audio tones, each symbol choosing one of the eight, so each symbol carries $\log_2 8 = 3$ bits. The tone spacing is 6.25 Hz, and with eight tones the whole signal occupies about $8 \times 6.25\text{ Hz} \approx 50\text{ Hz}$ — roughly the same order of narrowness as PSK31, but built for a completely different job. The payload those 79 symbols carry is small and rigidly structured: 77 information bits (enough for two callsigns, a grid square or report, and a handful of protocol flags) plus a 14-bit CRC for error detection, totaling 91 payload bits. Those 91 bits are then protected by a **(174,91) LDPC** (low-density parity-check) code — a forward error correction scheme that expands the 91 information bits into a 174-bit codeword by adding 83 redundant parity bits, chosen so that a receiver, even after a substantial fraction of the received bits are corrupted by noise, can iteratively solve a system of parity equations and recover the original 91 bits exactly. The code's rate — the fraction of each transmitted bit that carries genuine information rather than redundancy — is $91/174 \approx 0.523$: a little over half the codeword is payload, the rest is protection. That's a heavy price in raw throughput, but FT8 was never designed to move data quickly; it was designed to move a minimal, fixed-size message reliably, and a near-half-rate code buys a large amount of error-correcting margin in exchange for a payload the timed 15-second slot has ample room for regardless.

> **Worked example:** How many separate FT8 signals can one receiver decode out of a single 2500 Hz SSB passband in one 15-second cycle? Each FT8 signal occupies about 50 Hz (per the canon's FT8 bandwidth figure). Divide: $2500\text{ Hz} / 50\text{ Hz} = 50$. In principle, therefore, roughly fifty non-overlapping FT8 signals can sit side by side in the same passband a single voice QSO would occupy one of — and because the whole point of software demodulation is that decoding one signal costs nothing extra toward decoding the next, WSJT-X genuinely does decode dozens of simultaneous FT8 transmissions out of one busy band segment in a single 15-second cycle, each one processed as an independent slice of the same FFT the waterfall already computed. A CW or SSB operator, by contrast, can copy exactly one signal at a time, on one frequency, with one pair of ears — the same fundamental limit that has held since Chapter 1's spark-gap operators. FT8 doesn't just decode weaker signals than a human ear can hear; it decodes many of them at once, another way in which moving demodulation into software changed what "operating" even means.

This is the mechanism, not just the marketing claim, behind why the mode spread so fast: it could decode reliably at signal levels no earlier amateur mode had ever worked at. LDPC decoding doesn't need every bit correct — it needs *enough* of the parity relationships to be satisfiable, and because the code spreads redundancy across the entire 174-bit codeword rather than concentrating it, the decoder can extract a valid 91-bit message even when individual demodulated symbols are wrong far more often than they're right. Combined with the tight 15-second timing structure (which lets the decoder integrate — average — over a known, fixed signal duration rather than guessing where a transmission starts and stops) and the narrow 50 Hz bandwidth (which limits the noise power competing with the signal), FT8 pushed the practical decode threshold down to roughly **−20 to −24 dB SNR** in a 2500 Hz reference bandwidth — meaning the signal's power can be one-hundredth to one-two-hundred-fiftieth of the noise power in that band and the software will still, reliably, recover the message.

> **Worked example:** What does "−24 dB SNR" actually mean as a power ratio? By the canon's decibel relation, $\text{dB} = 10 \cdot \log_{10}(P_2/P_1)$. Setting $\text{dB} = -24$ and solving for the ratio: $P_2/P_1 = 10^{-24/10} = 10^{-2.4} \approx 0.004$. So a signal at −24 dB SNR carries roughly 1/250th the power of the noise measured in the same 2500 Hz reference bandwidth — a signal an operator listening by ear would describe not as "weak" but as "not there." The LDPC decoder doesn't hear it as speech or as a copyable tone sequence at all; it accumulates statistical evidence across the whole 15-second, 79-symbol block and solves for the most probable 91-bit message consistent with that evidence and the parity constraints. That gap between what an FT8 decoder can pull out of the noise and what even a skilled CW operator can copy by ear is the whole reason the mode reshaped HF operating within months of its release rather than years.

**Digital voice and the vocoder.** Digitizing an amateur voice signal at full telephone-quality bit rates — tens of kilobits per second — would occupy far more bandwidth than any amateur FM channel allows, so digital-voice systems don't digitize speech directly; they run it through a **vocoder**, a codec specifically designed to model the human vocal tract rather than to reproduce an arbitrary waveform. A vocoder analyzes short frames of speech, extracts a small set of parameters that describe the vocal tract's resonances and the pitch/voicing of that frame, transmits only those parameters (a few kilobits per second, sometimes under two), and reconstructs an approximation of the original speech at the receiver from a synthetic vocal-tract model driven by those parameters. D-STAR, DMR, and Fusion all lean on this same idea, most commonly with the proprietary **AMBE** or **AMBE+2** vocoder chips, though the three systems differ in how they modulate the resulting bit stream onto RF: D-STAR uses GMSK, DMR uses 4-level FSK with two time-division multiplexed voice slots sharing a single 12.5 kHz channel, and Fusion uses C4FM. DMR's two-slot time-division multiplexing (TDMA) is itself a small technical marvel worth pausing on: rather than give each of two simultaneous conversations its own separate RF channel, as analog FM repeaters do, a DMR repeater rapidly alternates — many times per second — between transmitting slot 1's data and slot 2's data on the *same* 12.5 kHz frequency, and every DMR radio listening simply ignores the timeslot that isn't its own. Two independent voice conversations ride one channel that, under the old FM rules of Chapter 6, could carry only one; it's the AX.25 packet frame's time-sharing logic, transplanted from data onto real-time voice.

In sum: D-STAR, DMR, and System Fusion digitize voice with a low-bit-rate vocoder, commonly proprietary AMBE or AMBE+2, then modulate the result onto RF each its own way — D-STAR GMSK, DMR 4-level FSK on a 2-slot TDMA scheme (two voice slots sharing 12.5 kHz), Fusion C4FM. The operational consequence of vocoding is a very different failure mode from analog FM. An analog FM signal degrades *gracefully* — as the signal weakens, noise rises smoothly with it, capture effect notwithstanding, and a listener can often still follow speech through significant hiss. A vocoded digital signal degrades almost not at all — right up until the error-correction and demodulation can no longer keep up with the bit-error rate, at which point the reconstructed speech doesn't get noisier, it gets *garbled* or drops out entirely, syllable by syllable, in the characteristic robotic stutter every digital-voice operator learns to recognize as "about to lose the signal." Hams call this the digital **"cliff edge"**: a system that sounds essentially perfect right up until it doesn't, trading FM's forgiving degradation for near-perfect clarity within its working range and near-total failure just outside it.

### The State of the Art in 2017

There is no owned Handbook edition anchoring this chapter — the book's last owned edition closes in 1983, and everything from 1991 onward is reconstructed from the public historical record and the public-domain FCC Part 97 rules rather than from a physical volume on the shelf. But it's possible to describe, concretely, what a serious HF operator's desk looked like in the weeks around FT8's release in June 2017, because that setup had by then become close to a de facto standard.

The transceiver itself, by 2017, was very often already an SDR in substance, and increasingly one built around direct RF sampling rather than analog mixing at all. Icom's IC-7300, introduced the year before FT8's release, put a direct-sampling architecture and a full-color built-in spectrum/waterfall display into a mid-priced HF transceiver aimed squarely at the ordinary ham rather than the SDR enthusiast — proof that the technology described earlier in this chapter had moved from kit-built experiment to mainstream retail product in barely a decade. At the high end, FlexRadio's 6000-series transceivers took the concept further still, exposing a wideband direct-sampling receiver's entire panadapter view as a first-class operating tool rather than a novelty. Even rigs that kept a conventional synthesizer-tuned front end, from Yaesu, Kenwood, and others, leaned on DSP heavily in their IF and audio chains, so the line between "an SDR" and "a normal modern transceiver" was, by 2017, more a matter of degree than of kind.

What made the desk distinctly a 2017 desk, though, wasn't the radio at all — it was the laptop beside it. A USB cable ran from the transceiver to that laptop, carrying rig-control commands (frequency, mode, PTT) over a CAT (computer-aided transceiver) protocol and audio in both directions through a USB sound-card interface — a dedicated unit like the SignaLink USB, or, on newer rigs, USB audio built directly into the radio itself. On the laptop, WSJT-X — Joe Taylor's software suite, open-source and free, running on Windows, macOS, or Linux — decoded the receive audio, displayed a waterfall of the last several minutes of the band, and, for FT8 QSOs specifically, largely automated the exchange: the operator selected a station shown as decoded on the waterfall, clicked to call it, and the software handled the precisely-timed 15-second transmit/receive alternation, composing and sending the scripted callsign/grid/report/acknowledgment sequence with a click at each stage rather than typed free text.

One detail of that setup mattered more than a newcomer might guess: the laptop's clock. Because every FT8 station on the air must begin transmitting within a small fraction of a second of the same 15-second slot boundary for the LDPC decoder's timing assumptions to hold, WSJT-X's documentation was blunt about the requirement — the operating computer's clock needed to be accurate to within about one second, and serious operators kept it far tighter than that using NTP (Network Time Protocol) synchronization against an internet time server, sometimes backed by a GPS receiver for stations wanting sub-second discipline independent of any internet connection at all. A ham whose PC clock had drifted by a few seconds — a common enough state of affairs on an old shack computer nobody had rebooted in months — would find WSJT-X still decoding other stations perfectly well while their own transmissions landed just outside the window everyone else's software was listening in, invisible on every other operator's waterfall despite going out at full power. It was a distinctly software-era failure mode: not a matter of watts or antenna gain, the concerns of every previous chapter, but of whether a general-purpose computer's internal clock agreed with the rest of the network to within a fraction of a second.

Around WSJT-X, a small supporting ecosystem had grown up by 2017. PSKReporter and similar automated-spotting networks aggregated decoded callsigns and grid squares from thousands of receiving stations worldwide in near-real time, turning any operator's FT8 decode log into a crowdsourced propagation map — a service with roots in the Reverse Beacon Network's earlier CW/RTTY spotting work, now extended to the much higher decode volume FT8 produced. Logging software (N1MM Logger+, Log4OM, and others) increasingly integrated directly with WSJT-X over the same local network sockets the mode software exposed, auto-logging each confirmed contact without the operator re-typing a single field. None of this — the waterfall, the clock discipline, the crowdsourced spotting, the auto-logging — existed in anything like this form a decade earlier; all of it existed, largely for free, by the time FT8 made it matter to the average HF operator rather than only to specialists doing moonbounce work.

Put concretely, a single exchange looked like this. The waterfall scrolled downward, a fresh column of decodes appearing every fifteen seconds along its bottom edge, each one a short text line: a callsign, a four-character Maidenhead grid square, a signal-to-noise estimate in dB. The operator, watching for a wanted prefix or a needed grid, clicked one decoded line; WSJT-X composed the first transmission — callsign pair and grid square — and, on the next slot boundary, sent it automatically at the pre-set power level. Fifteen seconds later, if the other station had decoded the call, its own automated reply carried a signal report instead of a grid; fifteen seconds after that, an acknowledgment; fifteen seconds after that, a final "73." Four exchanges, sixty seconds, and a logged contact — shorter, in elapsed time, than most operators would have spent identifying themselves and exchanging pleasantries on SSB, and achieved, not infrequently, at signal levels where SSB would have produced only static.

Behind that laptop sat everything the previous seven chapters had already built: an antenna that still obeyed the same wave-speed relation, $c = f\lambda$, that opened this book; a transceiver PA still very likely built around solid-state devices descended from the 1947 point-contact transistor, running Class-AB or Class-C depending on the mode; a station license that, since 2007, had required no Morse code at all to reach any HF band. What had changed wasn't the physics of the antenna or the electron flow in the final amplifier — it was that the layer of the station doing the actual demodulation, the layer that in 1957 was tubes and IF transformers and a mechanical filter, was by 2017 a few hundred lines of open-source signal-processing code running on a general-purpose CPU that the operator had also used, an hour earlier, to check email.

FT8's arrival in the summer of 2017 didn't require anyone to buy new hardware. Existing SSB transceivers, existing sound-card interfaces, existing antennas — all of it worked unmodified; the only new thing was a software download. Within months, band-scan surveys of the digital subbands showed FT8 activity outstripping every other digital mode combined, and DXpeditions that had spent decades chasing rare-country contacts primarily by CW and SSB began running FT8 as a first-choice mode specifically because its extreme sensitivity let a modest pileup station work a rare DX station through propagation too marginal for any other mode to close the circuit at all.

The trajectory of this whole chapter points in one direction: less and less of the "radio" lives in dedicated hardware, and more and more of it lives in general-purpose software running on a general-purpose processor, reachable and modifiable by anyone with the skill to write code rather than only by those with the skill to wind a coil or trim a filter. FT8's LDPC decoder, PSK31's Varicode, the SDR-1000's PowerSDR stack — every one of them is, in the end, an algorithm, not a circuit, and algorithms can be rewritten, forked, and improved at a pace no analog design cycle ever matched. That raises the question this book has been circling since Chapter 1, when a spark transmitter's damped wave-train was the only signal the art knew how to make: if the defining constraint of amateur radio was once *what physics and hardware would allow*, and that constraint has now largely dissolved into *what software can be written*, what is left to distinguish a ham radio operator from anyone else running a program that talks to an antenna? That is the question the epilogue takes up.
