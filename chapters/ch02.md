## 2. The Shortwave Revolution (1919–1927)

*"December 1921: at Ardrossan, Scotland, Paul Godley (2ZE) hears the American station 1BCG of Greenwich, Connecticut, logged at 2152 UTC — then goes on to copy more than twenty US amateur stations before the tests close."*
*— compiled from the ARRL Transatlantic Tests record, December 1921*

### The Story

On 6 April 1917 the Department of Commerce had ordered every amateur transmitting and receiving station in the country shut down and every antenna wire hauled off its mast. It was not a curfew or a power restriction; it was a blackout, total and indefinite, and it held for two and a half years while the country fought a war that had, almost as a side effect, matured radio technology faster than the previous two decades combined. Tens of thousands of young men who had never touched a wireless key before 1917 came out of the Army Signal Corps, the Navy, and the merchant marine as trained operators; vacuum tubes that had been expensive, fragile laboratory curiosities in 1914 were, by 1918, being manufactured by the hundreds of thousands for military sets. When the ban finally lifted, it did not lift all at once, and the two-stage manner of its lifting says something about how cautious the government still was about amateurs on the air. Listening came back first — receiving privileges were restored by Navy Department order effective 15 April 1919, amateurs free again to turn a dial even though every transmitting key in the country remained legally silent. Transmitting took another five and a half months. **FACT:** US amateur **transmitting** privileges restored ("BAN OFF," *QST* Oct 1919 supplement). The October 1919 issue of *QST* did not wait for its regular press run to carry the news; it went out as a supplement, two words filling most of a page — BAN OFF — to a readership that had waited two and a half years to read them.

What came back onto the air that autumn was not the same hobby that had gone quiet in the spring of 1917. The prewar amateur station had, more often than not, been a spark set: a charged capacitor, a gap, a tuned circuit ringing down in damped bursts, exactly the technology described in the previous chapter, its bandwidth so wide and its note so buzzing that a handful of transmitters sharing a city could jam each other into unreadability. The postwar amateur, rebuilding a station after two and a half idle years, increasingly reached instead for a vacuum tube. Military-surplus triodes — filament-type tubes designed for aircraft and trench sets, sold off cheaply as the War Department liquidated its wartime stockpiles — flooded the surplus catalogs just as thousands of newly trained operators were looking for a hobby to bring their skills home to. A tube cost more than a coil of wire and a spark gap, but it did something spark fundamentally could not: it amplified, and, wired correctly, it oscillated cleanly and continuously rather than in a decaying train of pulses. Within a few years the changeover was nearly complete. Continuous-wave, tube-generated Morse — CW — did not merely compete with spark on the amateur bands of the early 1920s; it strangled it, technically and then by regulation, until spark survived on ham frequencies mostly as a stubborn holdout's nostalgia rather than as a serious mode.

Part of what made the tube worth reaching for, rather than a mere curiosity as it had been for De Forest's original 1906 Audion, was that the tube itself had quietly gotten much better while amateurs were off the air. De Forest's earliest Audions held a soft, imperfect vacuum — enough residual gas inside the envelope that the tube's behavior was erratic, its gain inconsistent, its life short, closer to a laboratory novelty than a dependable amplifier. Wartime manufacturing pressure, chasing reliability for military sets that had to work the first time in a trench or a cockpit, pushed tube makers to pull a much harder vacuum and standardize their construction, and the tubes that came home from the war as surplus were, quietly, a different and far more trustworthy class of device than the Audion had been a decade earlier. Amateurs inherited that improvement for free, without needing to know or care why a postwar tube behaved so much more predictably than a prewar one had.

The organization that had gone dark with the rest of the hobby in 1917 came back too, and came back stronger. The American Radio Relay League, founded in Hartford in 1914 by Hiram Percy Maxim and Clarence Tuska explicitly to solve the problem of relaying messages farther than any single amateur station could reach, resumed publishing *QST* and rebuilding its relay network almost as soon as the ban lifted, and it did so into a hobby whose technical center of gravity had shifted underneath it. A relay network built around spark's short, unreliable range made a certain kind of sense when a hundred miles was a good night's work; a relay network built around tubes that were about to prove capable of spanning oceans on their own needed rethinking, and the League spent much of the early 1920s doing exactly that rethinking in public, in the pages of *QST*, station by station, as members reported results that kept outrunning what anyone had expected.

The single biggest technical fact that reshaped amateur radio in this decade was one nobody had planned and few had predicted: the wavelengths Congress had confined amateurs to in 1912, below 200 meters, precisely because the commercial and military users of the spectrum considered them worthless for anything but short-range work, turned out to be extraordinarily good at exactly what everyone had assumed they couldn't do. That discovery did not arrive all at once. It arrived in two dramatic steps, five years apart in genesis but only two years apart in the record, each one forcing the amateur community — and, more slowly, the engineering establishment that had written the 200-meter band off — to revise its picture of what a small station on a "useless" wavelength could actually do.

The first step was a listening test, not a two-way contact. By late 1921, sporadic reports had trickled in of American amateur signals being heard faintly by European listeners, and the ARRL organized a formal test to settle the question with something better than rumor: send its best operator across the Atlantic with the best receiving equipment available and see, systematically, what could actually be copied. Paul Godley, an ARRL technical man with a reputation as one of the sharpest ears in the hobby, sailed for Scotland with a specially assembled receiver and a directional long-wire antenna, and set up his listening post at Ardrossan, on the Scottish coast, pointed back across the Atlantic toward the stations he hoped to hear. **FACT:** ARRL Transatlantic Tests: Paul Godley (2ZE) at Ardrossan, Scotland hears US station 1BCG (Greenwich, CT), logged 2152 UTC; ultimately copies 20+ US stations. 1BCG was not an ordinary ham station; it was a specially built, unusually powerful transmitter assembled by a consortium of amateurs near Greenwich, Connecticut, explicitly for the tests, running on the wavelength then reserved for amateurs. But 1BCG was only the first entry in Godley's log. Before the tests closed he had copied more than twenty different American amateur stations, ordinary hams among them, proving something the commercial engineering establishment had not believed possible: that stations confined by law to spectrum nobody serious wanted could, with care and modest power, be heard three thousand miles away.

The choice of Ardrossan was not incidental. Godley needed a receiving location on the far side of the ocean, away from the electrical noise of a city, with room to lay out a long directional receiving antenna aimed back at North America — a wave antenna of the type Harold Beverage had recently developed, hundreds of feet of low wire strung close to the ground along the great-circle path to the signals it was meant to favor, working not by amplification but by the geometry of how quickly a wave traveling along the wire in the wanted direction reinforced itself compared to noise and signals arriving from elsewhere. Conditions on the Scottish coast in December were what December on the Scottish coast usually is, and Godley's task amounted to sitting for hours at a time with headphones clamped on, working a regenerative receiver by hand through static and weather to pull individual American callsigns out of a background that, to anyone without his trained ear, would have sounded like nothing at all. That the results were taken seriously back home mattered as much as the results themselves: RCA and the major commercial wireless interests had, by 1921, essentially written off anything below 200 meters as a serious communications band, and an ARRL-organized amateur test succeeding where informed commercial opinion expected failure did real work in forcing the engineering establishment to at least ask why.

The second step, less than two years later, went further and mattered more, because it did not merely prove that the 200-meter band could span an ocean — it proved that wavelengths shorter still, wavelengths hams had barely begun to experiment with, worked even better. Through 1922, encouraged by Godley's results, dozens of transatlantic amateur contacts were logged, still mostly close to the 200-meter wavelength the law specified. But a handful of experimenters on both sides of the Atlantic, chasing lower interference and cleaner tuning rather than any theory of propagation, had begun pushing their transmitters to progressively shorter wavelengths — 150 meters, then 130, then closer to 100 — and reporting, almost as an aside, signals that seemed to travel unreasonably well for the power behind them. Léon Deloy, a French amateur operating as 8AB from Nice, was one of them. In November 1923, Deloy arranged by cable with two American stations to attempt a coordinated two-way contact on a wavelength far shorter than anything the 1921 tests had used. **FACT:** First two-way transatlantic amateur contact: Léon Deloy (French 8AB) works Fred Schnell (1MO) and John Reinartz (1XAM/1XAL) on 110 m CW. This was not a one-way reception logged by a specialist with a superior receiver and an ocean of patience, as Godley's had been — it was a live, two-way conversation, both ends transmitting and receiving in real time, at 110 meters, a wavelength roughly half of what the "impossible" 1921 tests had needed. Word of it tore through the amateur community within days. Over the following months, hundreds of hams on both sides of the Atlantic, and then across the Pacific, dragged their transmitters down to 100 meters, then 80, then 40, chasing the same effect Deloy had stumbled into — and finding, again and again, that shorter consistently outperformed longer, that a fraction of the power that had struggled to cross an ocean at 200 meters now carried casually around a hemisphere at a wavelength a quarter that length. The wavelengths industry had handed to amateurs as worthless junk spectrum were, watt for watt, the best long-distance radio spectrum anyone had ever used.

The two years after Deloy's contact were, for the amateur bands, something close to a land rush. Stations that had spent 1922 proving the 200-meter band could cross an ocean spent 1924 through 1926 proving that almost every shorter wavelength worked better still, each new report in *QST* prompting another wave of builders to retune their transmitters lower and try it themselves. The League, which had spent the first years of the decade defending amateurs' right to keep any spectrum at all, spent the back half of it doing something closer to spectrum management on its own members' behalf — publishing recommended band segments for 80, 40, and 20 meters well before any of them had international treaty standing, imposing informal order on a rush that could easily have produced the same crowded, self-interfering chaos that had characterized the worst of the prewar spark years. New licensees kept arriving faster than the League's relay infrastructure had been designed for, drawn in as much by the newspaper-worthy novelty of hearing another continent on a homemade set as by any of the traffic-handling purpose that had founded the ARRL in the first place, and by mid-decade the hobby's center of gravity had shifted unmistakably from message relaying toward the pursuit of distance for its own sake — DX working, in the vocabulary the era cemented, as an end rather than a means.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** The shortwave discovery was, from the moment it became undeniable, an international story rather than a national one — a French station working two Americans was the founding contact — and the hobby's governance moved to catch up. In April 1925, delegates from twenty-three nations gathered at the Faculté des Sciences in Paris for the first International Amateur Radio Congress. **FACT:** International Amateur Radio Union (IARU) founded in Paris (Faculté des Sciences); Hiram Percy Maxim (u1AW) elected first International President. The League that had begun as a Hartford relay club nine years earlier now had a global counterpart, born directly out of the recognition that a hobby whose signals now routinely crossed oceans on a few dozen watts could no longer be governed, or even meaningfully understood, one country at a time. The date of that closing session is marked today, a century later, as World Amateur Radio Day — a retrospective label, not a period one, but a fair measure of how much weight the amateur community would come to place on that one week in Paris.

The discovery cut two ways for amateurs, and the second cut was regulatory. A band of spectrum that had been dismissed as worthless in 1912 was, by the mid-1920s, demonstrably the best real estate in the radio spectrum for long-distance communication — and everyone who had previously ignored it, from national governments to commercial telegraph companies, wanted a piece of it now that amateurs had proven what it could do. The domestic broadcast boom of the early 1920s had already produced its own regulatory crisis by mid-decade — hundreds of new stations crowding onto a handful of channels, with the existing 1912 Act's licensing framework legally unable to deny a station a license or assign it a clear channel, since the Act gave the Secretary of Commerce no discretion to refuse an applicant who met the technical requirements. A federal court ruling in 1926 made the problem acute almost overnight, stripping the Secretary of even the limited practical authority he had been exercising informally to steer stations toward unoccupied channels; for months afterward, broadcasters jumped frequency and raised power essentially at will, and the AM dial briefly became something close to the acoustic chaos this book's opening chapter described on the spark-crowded amateur bands of a decade earlier — proof, if anyone in Washington still needed it, that spectrum without an enforceable traffic cop was spectrum nobody could reliably use. Congress's answer arrived in early 1927. **FACT:** Radio Act of 1927 signed by President Coolidge; creates the Federal Radio Commission and the "public interest, convenience, or necessity" standard. For the first time, a federal body had explicit legal authority not merely to license radio stations but to deny licenses, assign frequencies, and revoke privileges in the name of a standard — "public interest, convenience, or necessity" — broad and vague enough to give the new Commission real regulatory teeth. Amateurs fell under the FRC's jurisdiction along with everyone else, though the amateur service's internal discipline — the ARRL's decade of self-organized band planning, traffic nets, and operating procedure — had already given regulators less to clean up on the amateur bands than on the chaotic broadcast dial.

The domestic reorganization was only half the picture; the shortwave discovery was a worldwide phenomenon, and it needed a worldwide settlement. That came at the end of the same year, when delegates from around the globe convened in Washington to redraw the treaty map of the radio spectrum in light of everything the previous six years had proven. The International Radiotelegraph Conference formally allocated the wavelengths amateurs around the world had already, informally, sorted themselves onto through years of trial: bands near 80, 40, 20, and 10 meters, carved out of what had recently been considered junk spectrum and now recognized, by treaty, as some of the most valuable real estate in the radio universe — plus the callsign-prefix blocks that would let an operator anywhere identify, from the first character of a station's call, roughly what country it belonged to. By the close of 1927, the argument amateurs had been having with the engineering establishment since before the war — that the "useless" short wavelengths were worth having at all — was formally, internationally settled, in the amateurs' favor, on paper signed by governments that six years earlier would not have taken the question seriously.

### How It Works

Six things made the 1919–1927 shortwave revolution possible, and understanding any one of them properly requires understanding all six together, because each solved a problem the others created. The **triode vacuum tube** gave amateurs, cheaply and for the first time, a device that could amplify a weak signal and generate a clean continuous one. **Regeneration** turned that same triode into a receiver dramatically more sensitive and selective than its raw gain alone would suggest. The **Hartley** and **Colpitts** oscillator circuits gave builders two reliable, well-understood ways to make a triode generate a stable continuous wave at a chosen frequency. The **MOPA** architecture addressed the signature failure mode of a self-excited transmitter — frequency instability under load. The **heterodyne principle** and its **beat-frequency oscillator** made the clean continuous wave those circuits produced audible at all, since a pure keyed carrier falls silent in an ordinary detector until it is beaten against a second local signal. And the physics of **ionospheric skip** explained, after the fact, why the wavelengths this whole toolkit was built to exploit worked so improbably well.

**The triode as amplifier and oscillator.** A triode vacuum tube — the direct descendant of Lee De Forest's 1906 Audion — is built around three elements sealed inside an evacuated glass envelope: a heated filament (or a separately heated cathode) that, by thermionic emission, boils electrons off its surface into the vacuum; a plate, held at a substantial positive voltage, that attracts those electrons across the gap and collects them, constituting a measurable plate current; and a grid, a fine wire mesh or spiral positioned between filament and plate, biased at some voltage of its own. Because the grid sits much closer to the electron stream than the distant plate does, a small change in grid voltage exerts an outsized, disproportionate influence on how many electrons make it through to the plate — geometry, not magic, is doing the work. A tiny alternating voltage applied to the grid produces a much larger alternating variation in plate current, and if that plate current is made to flow through a resistor or a transformer winding, it produces a correspondingly large alternating voltage swing at the output. That is amplification, and it is the property every circuit in this chapter ultimately depends on.

An amplifier becomes an oscillator by the addition of one idea: feed some of that amplified output back to the input, in the right phase and in sufficient amount, and the circuit no longer needs an external signal to have something to amplify — it amplifies its own feedback, again and again, building up a self-sustaining alternating signal from nothing but the random electrical noise present in any real circuit. Three conditions have to hold simultaneously for that buildup to settle into a stable, continuous oscillation rather than either dying out or growing without bound: the feedback has to arrive back at the grid in phase with the exact same signal that produced it (positive feedback, reinforcing rather than cancelling); the total gain around the feedback loop — tube gain times whatever fraction of the output the feedback path returns — has to be at least equal to the losses around that same loop, so the oscillation is sustained rather than damped out; and something in the loop has to be frequency-selective, so that only one frequency satisfies both the phase and the gain conditions strongly enough to build up while every other frequency present in the tube's noise is suppressed. In every practical 1920s oscillator, that frequency-selective element is an LC tank circuit, and its resonant frequency — $f_0 = \dfrac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$, the same relation that governs every tuned circuit in this book — sets the frequency at which the whole assembly settles into oscillation.

**Regeneration.** Armstrong's 1914 patent applied that same feedback idea to reception rather than generation, and the circuit built around it — the regenerative detector — was the single most important receiver of this entire era, standard equipment in the overwhelming majority of American amateur stations by the middle of the decade. A weak incoming RF signal is coupled into a tuned grid circuit (the "tank," coil L1 and its variable capacitor) set to the frequency of the desired station. The tube amplifies that signal at the plate as any triode amplifier would. But a second coil — the "tickler," conventionally called L2 — sits in the plate circuit, positioned physically close enough to L1 to be magnetically coupled to it, and wound so that the coupling returns energy to the grid tank in phase with the signal already there.

{{fig:regen-rx}}

The effect of that feedback, as the tickler coupling is increased, is not a simple linear boost in volume. Positive feedback into a tuned circuit behaves mathematically as though it were cancelling out some of the tank's own internal resistive losses — pumping energy back in to replace what the circuit's real resistance would otherwise dissipate as heat — which raises the tank's effective $Q = f_0 / \text{BW}_{3\text{dB}}$ well above what the physical coil and capacitor alone could deliver. A higher-Q tank rings more purely at its resonant frequency and rejects everything nearby far more sharply, so regeneration buys the operator both amplification — a single regenerative tube stage could equal several ordinary un-regenerated RF stages in sensitivity — and selectivity, simultaneously, from one tube and one extra coil. Pushed further still, the effective loss the feedback is cancelling reaches zero and then goes negative: loop gain exceeds unity, the phase and gain conditions for self-sustained oscillation described above are both satisfied, and the detector stops being merely a very good amplifier and becomes a small transmitter in its own right, radiating a faint signal of its own back out through the antenna it was supposed to be only listening on. A regenerative receiver run carelessly past that threshold was, and remained for years, one of the amateur bands' chief sources of interference — a "blooper" whose owner, absorbed in chasing a weak signal, had no idea the set was now squealing into a dozen neighbors' headphones.

The circuit's importance was not in dispute; its authorship was. Armstrong filed for his regeneration patent in 1913 and was granted it on 6 October 1914, but Lee De Forest — the Audion's own inventor — claimed to have discovered the same feedback effect first, and the two men, along with a scattering of other claimants, fought the question through the patent office and the federal courts for the better part of two decades, a dispute the engineering profession watched with an interest bordering on the personal, since almost every set on the amateur bands by mid-decade depended on the very circuit under dispute. Neither the timing nor the outcome of that fight changed how the circuit worked or how many hams built one; it simply meant that the single most important receiver of the entire shortwave-revolution era spent its formative years as one of the most contested inventions in the whole history of American technology, litigated in courtrooms while it was being soldered onto ten thousand kitchen tables.

Operators quickly learned to treat that threshold not as a hazard to avoid but as a dial to work right up against, and precisely which side of it to sit on depended on what they were trying to copy — a distinction that leads directly into the mechanics of hearing Morse at all.

**The Hartley and Colpitts oscillators.** A transmitter needs the mirror image of a regenerative receiver: not an amplifier nudged to the edge of oscillation, but a circuit built to oscillate cleanly and continuously, on a chosen frequency, as a matter of design rather than delicate adjustment. Two feedback-oscillator topologies, both well established by the early 1920s, did that job in the overwhelming majority of amateur transmitters throughout this era, and both work from the same three conditions already laid out for oscillation in general — they differ only in how they extract the feedback fraction from the tank circuit.

{{fig:hartley}}

The **Hartley oscillator** uses a single coil, tapped partway along its winding, in place of two separate coils. One end of the winding connects to the grid circuit, the other end to the plate circuit, the tap itself grounded (or returned to the cathode), and a single tuning capacitor sits across the whole coil to set resonance together with its total inductance. Because the two halves of the winding share one continuous magnetic structure, plate-side current induces a voltage on the grid-side turns automatically, with no separate coupling coil required; the position of the tap sets what fraction of the total winding's voltage swing is returned to the grid, which is the feedback fraction the oscillation depends on. A well-built Hartley can be tuned across a wide swath of a band with a single variable capacitor while the tap position, and hence the feedback fraction, stays fixed — a practical convenience that made it a favorite for simple, single-tuning-control transmitters.

The **Colpitts oscillator** achieves the identical result by the opposite means: instead of tapping the inductor, it splits the tank's capacitance into two capacitors wired in series across a single, untapped coil, and takes the feedback connection from the junction between them rather than from a coil tap. The two capacitors form a capacitive voltage divider, so a signal appearing across the full tank reappears at that junction scaled down by the divider ratio — exactly analogous to what the Hartley's tap does with inductance rather than capacitance. Hartley and Colpitts are, in this sense, electrical duals of one another: one topology splits the tank's inductance to derive feedback, the other splits its capacitance, and a 1920s builder chose between them largely on which was easier to wind from parts already on the bench rather than on any decisive difference in performance. Both circuits appear in dozens of dressed-up variants through the era's construction articles — a "tapped-coil" Hartley might use a single continuous winding with a sliding contact instead of a fixed tap, letting a builder trim the feedback fraction experimentally rather than committing to it at winding time, and both topologies show up equally often as the frequency-determining stage of a full transmitter and as the self-oscillating stage of a regenerative receiver, since an oscillator pushed just hard enough to sustain oscillation and a regenerative detector pushed just past the same threshold are, underneath the different job titles, the identical circuit.

> **Worked example:** A builder winds a Hartley tank coil of $L = 10\ \mu\text{H}$ and pairs it with a $C = 200\ \text{pF}$ variable capacitor. What frequency will the oscillator settle on? Using $f_0 = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{LC})$: $LC = (10 \times 10^{-6})(200 \times 10^{-12}) = 2.0 \times 10^{-15}$. The square root of that product is $\sqrt{2.0 \times 10^{-15}} \approx 4.47 \times 10^{-8}$, and $2\pi$ times that is $\approx 2.81 \times 10^{-7}$ seconds. Inverting gives $f_0 \approx 1 / (2.81 \times 10^{-7}) \approx 3.56 \times 10^{6}\ \text{Hz}$, or about 3.56 MHz — comfortably inside the shortwave range hams were, by the mid-1920s, learning was where the real distance lay. Halve either $L$ or $C$ and, because both sit under a square root, the resonant frequency rises by a factor of $\sqrt{2}$, not by a factor of two; double either and it falls by the same $\sqrt{2}$. That nonlinear relationship between component value and frequency is exactly why a 1920s station kept a rack of interchangeable plug-in coils, one wound for each band, rather than trying to cover the whole shortwave spectrum by turning a single tuning capacitor through an impractically large range.

**The MOPA transmitter.** A self-excited transmitter — a single Hartley or Colpitts oscillator tube coupled directly to the antenna, with no stage in between — was the simplest, cheapest possible amateur rig, and for that reason the most common one for most of this era. It was also, structurally, unstable in a specific and recognizable way. The antenna's impedance is never perfectly constant: it shifts with wind moving the wire, with nearby objects, and — critically — every single time the operator's key opens or closes, because keying changes the tube's own operating point and, with it, the tube's internal interelectrode capacitance, which is itself part of the effective tank capacitance the oscillator's frequency depends on. An oscillator whose tank is talking directly to a load that keeps shifting cannot hold a perfectly fixed frequency; it "pulls," and the signature of pulling, heard on a receiver, is chirp — the pitch of the beat note sliding audibly upward or downward at the start of every dot and dash, sometimes badly enough that a long transmission could visibly wander tens of kilocycles across the band and interfere with conversations that had nothing to do with it.

{{fig:mopa}}

The fix, understood in principle well before it became universal in practice, is architectural rather than incremental: decouple the function that sets the frequency from the function that delivers power to the antenna entirely. A small, lightly loaded oscillator — its tank talking only to the grid of a following amplifier stage, never to the antenna itself — sets the frequency and, because nothing downstream can load it enough to matter, holds that frequency essentially fixed no matter what the antenna, the key, or the weather is doing. One or more buffer and amplifier stages then take that stable, low-power signal and build it up to whatever power the final antenna coupling requires, with only the last stage in the chain actually seeing the antenna's shifting impedance — several tube-stages removed, by the time a load change propagates that far, from anything that could pull the oscillator's own tank. This is the master-oscillator power-amplifier architecture, MOPA: a name that describes a design philosophy — isolate the frequency-determining stage from the power-delivering stage — more than it describes any single fixed circuit. MOPA cost more than a self-excited rig — at minimum one more tube, one more power-supply drain, one more stage of tuning to keep aligned across bands — and through most of the 1920s the cheaper, chirpier self-excited transmitter remained the amateur norm, its operators developing a whole informal craft of "reading through" chirp much as an earlier generation had learned to read through the buzz of spark. The intermediate stage between oscillator and final amplifier, where one was fitted at all, is usually called a buffer: its job is not to add much power but simply to present the oscillator with a light, fixed, well-behaved load — the buffer's own grid circuit, not the antenna — so that whatever the final power-amplifier stage sees downstream never propagates back far enough to matter. A buffer amplifying only modestly, running comfortably within its own ratings, isolates the oscillator from load changes far more effectively than the same tube count spent trying to make the oscillator itself more powerful ever could; isolation, not raw output, was the whole point.

**The heterodyne principle and the beat-frequency oscillator.** Continuous-wave Morse solves the bandwidth and quality problems of spark completely, but it creates a listening problem of its own. A CW carrier, unlike a damped spark wave, does not vary in amplitude at all while the key is down — it is a pure, unchanging sine wave switched fully on and fully off, and fed into an ordinary rectifying detector, whether crystal or vacuum-tube, it produces only a silent shift in DC current while the key is down and silence while it's up. Dots and dashes you could watch on a meter, perhaps, but never hear directly. Making CW audible requires heterodyne action — the same mixing principle Reginald Fessenden had worked out around 1901 — combining the incoming, inaudible RF carrier with a second RF signal generated locally and tuned close to it, so that the *difference* between the two frequencies falls squarely in the range of human hearing. That difference tone, typically pitched by the operator somewhere around 500 to 1,000 Hz, switches on and off in exact lockstep with the incoming key, and at last CW has a sound. The local oscillator producing that second signal is the beat-frequency oscillator, or BFO — terminology that would outlive every other piece of 1920s receiver hardware by decades.

A dedicated, separate BFO tube is one way to generate that beat — the way every later receiver architecture, including the superheterodyne Armstrong had already patented in June 1920, would eventually adopt as standard. But the elegant trick that defined the typical amateur receiver of this era needed no extra tube at all: push the same regenerative detector already described past its threshold of self-oscillation, and it becomes its own local oscillator, beating directly against the incoming CW carrier using nothing but the one tube already doing the amplifying and detecting. A single triode simultaneously amplifying, detecting, and locally oscillating against the very signal it is receiving is called autodyne reception, and it explains why a 1920s regenerative receiver was really two different circuits sharing one set of parts: backed off just short of oscillation, for the sharpest and quietest copy of a continuous-carrier voice signal, and nudged a hair past it, self-generating its own beat note, for CW — the difference a matter of a fraction of a turn on the same regeneration knob, switched by feel several times a minute.

Armstrong's superheterodyne, patented in June 1920, solved the same beat-note problem in a more elaborate and more powerful way — mixing the incoming signal down to a fixed intermediate frequency where a separate, dedicated BFO could always beat against it under identical, predictable conditions regardless of what band the front end was tuned to — but through the 1920s it remained a minority architecture on the amateur bench. A superhet needed more tubes than a one- or two-tube regenerative set could match tube-for-tube, which meant more cost, more batteries or a bigger power supply, and more circuits to keep aligned; it also carried, in these same years, patent licensing entanglements through RCA that added friction of a different kind for manufacturers hoping to sell one commercially. The regenerative receiver's one-tube elegance — amplifier, detector, and BFO all in a single bottle — was not a stopgap waiting to be replaced; for most of this chapter's decade, it was simply the more practical machine, and it would take the tighter selectivity and interference-choked bands of the following era to tip amateur practice decisively toward the superheterodyne's greater complexity.

**Ionospheric refraction, skip distance, and the skip zone.** Everything above explains how 1920s amateurs generated and received a clean signal on a short wavelength; none of it explains why that signal, once radiated, traveled as astonishingly far as it did on so little power. That answer is atmospheric physics rather than circuit design, and it had been proposed in outline two decades before anyone tested it on purpose. In 1902, Kennelly and Heaviside independently suggested that the upper atmosphere contained a layer, ionized enough to reflect or refract radio waves, that could account for signals following the earth's curvature farther than a straight line ought to allow. The commercial and military engineering establishment absorbed that idea slowly and mostly as it applied to long wavelengths, where their transoceanic services already operated; nobody with real budget or real power had reason to test it on the short wavelengths amateurs had been confined to, because those wavelengths were, by the ground-wave physics everyone already understood, assumed to be useless for distance regardless of what any upper-atmosphere layer might or might not do.

Ground-wave propagation — a signal traveling along or near the earth's surface, diffracting slightly around the curvature rather than departing it — really does get worse, not better, as wavelength shortens; higher frequencies suffer sharply higher ground losses, and a short-wavelength ground-wave signal dies off within tens of miles where a long-wavelength one might carry for hundreds. That was the entire, correct basis for the prewar consensus that amateurs' below-200-meter spectrum was worthless junk. What the consensus missed was the other path a radiated wave can take: the sky wave, energy launched upward at some angle rather than along the ground, which — instead of simply escaping into space — can refract back down to earth if it meets a sufficiently ionized layer at a favorable angle, returning to the ground far beyond where the ground wave ever reached, sometimes on the far side of an ocean in a single hop, sometimes most of the way around the planet through several hops in succession, each one bouncing off the earth's surface and back up into the ionosphere again.

Between the outer edge of usable ground wave and the inner edge of the nearest sky-wave landing point lies the skip zone — a ring of near-total silence for a given transmitter and frequency, often the quietest patch on the map even though stations both much closer and much farther away come through clearly, because the near ring sits past where the ground wave can usefully reach and short of where the first sky-wave hop can land. How readily a given frequency refracts back down depends on the ionization density of the reflecting layer and the wave's angle of incidence: lower frequencies refract more readily, bending back at steeper angles over shorter hops and needing less ionization to return at all, while higher frequencies need either a shallower angle or heavier ionization to come back down rather than punching straight through into space. But refracting is only half the trip. A sky wave must also pass — twice, going up and coming back down — through the lower, less densely ionized reaches of the same atmosphere, and the free electrons there absorb a real share of a low-frequency wave's energy as heat along the way, an absorption loss that falls away sharply as frequency climbs. Edward Appleton's experiments through 1924 and 1926, which measured the height and behavior of the reflecting layer directly and confirmed the Kennelly-Heaviside hypothesis quantitatively for the first time, gave the physics establishment, after the fact, the rigorous explanation for what amateurs had already proven empirically: a wave in the low shortwave range that does refract successfully typically arrives at its landing point having shed far less energy along the way than a lower-frequency wave attempting the identical trip.

Amateurs had, in fact, been compiling their own crude version of that same physics for years before Appleton's instruments confirmed it, simply by keeping logs. Operators noticed, long before anyone could explain why, that a given band's behavior was not constant through the day: a wavelength that carried booming signals across a continent at midday might go dead by evening while a longer one came alive in its place, and the pattern repeated night after night closely enough that experienced operators began scheduling transatlantic attempts around the hours their logs said worked rather than around whenever it was convenient to sit at the key. A single hop off the ionosphere could carry a signal a long way, but operators working the greatest distances — spanning not just an ocean but the better part of the globe — were relying, whether they had the vocabulary for it or not, on multiple successive hops, the wave bouncing down off the earth's surface and back up into the ionosphere two or three times in succession before reaching a receiver on the far side of the planet, each hop's landing point becoming the next hop's launching point in a chain no single ground-wave or single-reflection model could account for.

> **Worked example:** Why would 100 watts at 20 meters routinely outperform 1 kilowatt at 200 meters — a tenfold disadvantage in power more than made up for by a change in wavelength alone? Using $\lambda(\text{m}) = 300 / f(\text{MHz})$: 200 meters corresponds to $f = 300/200 = 1.5\ \text{MHz}$, and 20 meters to $f = 300/20 = 15\ \text{MHz}$ — a tenfold jump in frequency to match the tenfold drop in wavelength. The 1 kW station at 1.5 MHz pushes a strong, reliable ground-wave signal a couple of hundred miles, and on a good night some of its remaining power reaches the ionosphere and returns as a faint, fading sky wave — but a large share of what reaches the lower ionosphere at that frequency is absorbed along the way, and what survives often refracts only weakly, delivering an inconsistent signal at kilowatt cost. The 100 W station at 15 MHz radiates a tenth the power, and its ground wave alone might carry only a few tens of miles before dying out entirely — but the energy that reaches the ionosphere at that higher frequency passes through the absorbing lower layers with comparatively little loss, refracts cleanly off the higher reaches of the ionosphere, and lands intact a thousand miles away or more, ready to hop again. Watt for watt, the "useless" wavelength wins by a margin no amount of added power on the "good" wavelength can close, because the two signals are not competing along the same path at different strengths — they are taking almost entirely different physical routes to get anywhere at all.

### The State of the Art in 1927

A ham assembling a station in 1927 — the year the treaty conference in Washington formalized the shortwave bands, and the year of the ARRL *Radio Amateur's Handbook* edition that anchors this chapter — would have every circuit described above laid out for them as the ordinary, expected way to build a set, not as advanced or experimental technique. On the receiving side, the regenerative detector was the default: a single-tube regenerative receiver, or, for the builder with a little more budget, two or three tubes — one or two stages of tuned radio-frequency amplification ahead of a regenerative detector, followed by an audio amplifier stage to drive headphones loud enough to copy through static. Loudspeakers existed and were spreading fast in broadcast parlors, but a serious DX operator working weak, fading signals near the noise floor still worked in headphones, where every last decibel of a marginal signal mattered and a speaker's added noise did not. Tube choice had largely standardized around a family of low-power, low-filament-current triodes — general-purpose types descended from the wartime surplus tubes that had started the whole tube-vs-spark changeover — cheap enough that a modest receiver cost a fraction of what an equivalent commercial broadcast set did.

On the transmitting side, the self-excited Hartley or Colpitts oscillator, sometimes dressed up with a single buffer stage in a simple approach to MOPA discipline, remained the typical amateur rig, chirp and all; full multi-stage MOPA transmitters were understood and increasingly recommended in the pages of *QST*, but the extra tube and extra tuning it demanded kept it a step up from what most stations actually built. Crystal control — a quartz crystal replacing the LC tank entirely, holding frequency to a precision no hand-wound coil could match — was, in 1927, a genuinely new idea rather than an established one: George Pierce had developed the underlying crystal oscillator circuit at Harvard only in 1923, and *QST* had published its first crystal-controlled-transmitter construction schematic in July 1924, barely three years earlier. A handful of technically ambitious hams had crystals on their benches by 1927; the overwhelming majority still tuned an LC tank by ear and by feel, nursing the regeneration knob and the tickler coil the way their receivers demanded, chirp accepted as the ordinary cost of doing business rather than a fault to be engineered away. Every serious station kept a set of plug-in coils, one wound for each band, swapped by hand between 80 meters for reliable evening contacts, 40 meters as it too proved itself, and 20 meters for the operators chasing the same daytime, long-hop DX that had made Deloy's contact famous four years earlier. The old 200-meter wavelength the 1912 Act had originally confined amateurs to was, by 1927, all but abandoned by the serious DX crowd — technically still legal ground, practically a museum piece, the entire community having voted with its dial for the shorter wavelengths that actually worked.

Outside the station itself, a 1927 ham's antenna was likely to be nothing more elaborate than a single wire — a hundred feet or so of copper strung between the house and a tree or a neighboring roofline, fed at one end and grounded or counterpoised at the other, tuned to resonance for whichever band the operator was working that evening by adjusting a loading coil or simply cutting the wire to a length the era's rule-of-thumb tables recommended for each band. Sophisticated directional arrays were still mostly a laboratory or commercial-station luxury; the parasitic beam antenna that would let a modest station concentrate its output toward one part of the world was still the better part of a year away, waiting on Uda and Yagi's 1926 work to reach print and then reach amateur construction articles. Operating procedure, though, was already unmistakably modern: the same abbreviations — CQ to call any station, 73 to sign off, QSL to confirm a contact — that a ham in a much later decade would still recognize instantly were already standard vocabulary, most of them inherited from commercial and Signal Corps practice and cemented into amateur use during the very traffic-handling years the ARRL had built its identity around. A contact confirmed by postcard, mailed to a station worked hundreds or thousands of miles away and kept as a paper trophy of the distance covered, was already a recognizable institution — the QSL card — years before any formal awards program existed to reward collecting them.

Even as the shortwave bands filled, a few restless experimenters were already pushing in the opposite direction along the spectrum. The Third National Radio Conference had opened a 5-meter band, 56 to 64 MHz, to domestic amateur use on 10 October 1924 — territory so far above anything ionospheric skip could reliably serve that operating there meant relearning propagation almost from nothing, mostly limited to whatever a signal could reach by straight-line, near-horizon paths. Few bothered with it seriously in the 1920s; the payoff of the shortwave bands below 30 meters was too immediate and too well proven to compete with an experimental band that, for most practical purposes, barely worked at all. But the 5-meter allocation sat quietly on the books through the rest of the decade, spectrum nobody yet knew what to do with, in exactly the position the below-200-meter band itself had occupied fifteen years earlier.

Power supplies were themselves mid-transition. Battery-powered stations — a bank of dry cells for the tube filaments, a stack of heavier "B" batteries for plate voltage — remained common, especially for receivers, but AC-operated supplies, running a transformer and a rectifier tube directly off house current to generate the needed plate voltage, were spreading fast on the transmitting side by the mid-1920s, freeing stations from the expense and inconvenience of replacing batteries and letting a ham run more power more conveniently than a battery bank of reasonable size ever could. The Radio Act of 1927 had, that February, handed a new Federal Radio Commission real regulatory authority over every one of these stations for the first time; the October Washington conference had, that same year, formally drawn the international band-edge lines amateurs had already sorted themselves onto by trial. The circuits on the bench had not changed because of either event — Hartley oscillators and regenerative detectors worked exactly the same the week before the Radio Act as the week after — but the legal and international framework around those circuits had, for the first time since 1912, caught up with what amateurs on those "useless" short wavelengths had already proven.

### Forward Hook

By the close of 1927 the argument over whether short waves were worth having at all was settled, on paper, by treaty. A harder, quieter argument was only just beginning, and it was entirely about the self-excited transmitters that still filled most of the newly recognized bands. A Hartley or Colpitts oscillator coupled straight to an antenna held its frequency only as well as its builder's coil, capacitor, and luck; the wavelength conference in Washington had drawn lines a few dozen kilocycles wide around each amateur band, and every chirping, drifting, hand-tuned rig now operating inside those lines was, station by station, testing exactly how precisely a hobby built on tickler coils and plug-in coils could be expected to stay inside a boundary it could not actually see. A crystal sat on a handful of advanced benches by 1927, promising a frequency stability no LC tank could match — but it was still, for most of the amateur world, a schematic in a three-year-old *QST* rather than a component in the parts box. The bands the treaty had just finished drawing were about to fill faster than anyone at the Washington conference table had planned for, and the era that discovered short waves would spend its final years explaining why, exactly, one operator's carrier kept sliding into his neighbor's.

Picture a single amateur band on a busy evening at the end of 1927: a dozen or more stations packed into a slice of spectrum only a few dozen kilocycles wide, each one a hand-wound Hartley or Colpitts tank a fraction of a turn of a dial knob from where its builder last left it, each carrier drifting a little with temperature, with antenna sway, with every dot and dash the key sends. Somewhere in that crowd a receiver operator, one hand on the tuning dial and the other nursing a tickler coil to the exact edge of oscillation, is trying to hold a single chirping signal in a headphone's narrow window of clarity while three more carriers wander past on either side. The treaty has drawn the lines. Nothing on the bench yet knows how to stay inside them.
