## 6. Reaching for Space (1961–1976)

*"OSCAR 1 launched from Vandenberg AFB on 12 December 1961 — the world's first amateur radio satellite, and the first non-governmental satellite of any kind."*
*— NASA/NSSDCA launch record; AMSAT*

### The Story

By 1960 the postwar transformation was essentially complete. SSB had won its argument with AM in the pages of *QST*; the Collins KWM-1 and its imitators had shown that a single cabinet could hold both transmitter and receiver; the transistor was ten years old and creeping, stage by stage, into circuits that tubes had owned outright since 1906. A ham of 1960 could sit down at a compact, largely solid-state-assisted station and work the world on 20 meters with a fraction of the power and bulk his father had needed. The hobby had finished digesting one revolution. It was about to be handed two more at once — and neither of them would come from a receiver's front end. One would come from orbit. The other would come from a repeater on a hilltop, filled with parts nobody in the amateur trade had designed.

The orbital half of the story starts with a group of Bay Area amateurs who called themselves, with a certain deadpan literalism, Project OSCAR — Orbiting Satellite Carrying Amateur Radio. Through personal contacts with engineers at the aerospace contractors that built America's early satellites, they secured something no amateur radio organization had ever had before: a ride. Not a dedicated launch — amateur radio had no budget and no standing to ask for one — but space as ballast. The US Air Force's Discoverer/Corona reconnaissance program, launching classified photographic satellites on Thor-Agena B boosters out of Vandenberg Air Force Base, sometimes had a little unused capacity, and a small nonmilitary payload that added negligible risk and cost was an acceptable rider. Project OSCAR built a satellite to fit that opportunity: a package of roughly 10 kg (22 lb), battery-powered, carrying no transponder, no camera, no scientific instrument — just a 2-meter beacon transmitter and a single, deliberately simple piece of telemetry. **FACT:** OSCAR 1 launched from Vandenberg AFB (secondary payload on a Thor-Agena B) — world's first amateur radio satellite and first non-governmental satellite.

What OSCAR 1 transmitted was almost a joke, and was meant partly as one: the beacon keyed out the Morse code group "HI" — the standard amateur shorthand for laughter — over and over, at a keying rate that varied with the satellite's internal temperature. The mechanism behind the joke was genuine engineering: a temperature-sensitive element, most simply a thermistor whose resistance changes predictably with heat, set the timing of the keyer's astable oscillator, so the interval between dots and dashes stretched or shrank as the spacecraft's interior warmed in sunlight or cooled in Earth's shadow. A ground station needed no decoder and no data sheet to read that telemetry — only a stopwatch and a table Project OSCAR had published in advance, converting keying rate to degrees. It was real telemetry disguised as a wink: by timing how fast "HI" repeated, a ground station could read off the temperature inside the spacecraft, and thousands of amateurs on every inhabited continent did exactly that during OSCAR 1's roughly three weeks of battery life, mailing their reception reports back to Project OSCAR for correlation. No transponder meant no two-way contact was possible through the satellite — this was a beacon experiment, not yet a relay — but the achievement was not really about the payload's sophistication. It was about who had built it, and about how little it had cost to do it. Where a national space agency's telemetry payload might carry a rack of transducers and a dedicated downlink data format, Project OSCAR's engineers got a working thermometer into orbit using a single component and the manual Morse-timing skill every licensed amateur already had. OSCAR 1 reached orbit about seven months before Bell Telephone Laboratories' Telstar 1, the demonstration satellite that is popularly credited with inaugurating the era of commercial space communication. The distinction is worth stating precisely: OSCAR 1 was the first *non-governmental* satellite of any kind, not merely the first amateur one, because by December 1961 every other object that had ever reached orbit had been put there by a national space program. A loosely organized volunteer group, working with a shoestring budget and a favor from an aerospace contractor, had put a working radio into space before the telephone company did.

OSCAR II followed in June 1962, a close cousin of the first. But the ballast-slot arrangement that had gotten both satellites into orbit was informal and fragile by nature — it depended on personal relationships and on classified launch programs having spare capacity at the right moment, neither of which was something Project OSCAR could plan around or scale up. Getting from "impressive stunt, twice" to "sustained program" needed an organization that could hold property, raise money in a legally accountable way, and negotiate with NASA and the Air Force as an institution rather than as a handshake between engineers. **FACT:** AMSAT (Radio Amateur Satellite Corporation) chartered as a 501(c)(3) in Washington, DC.

AMSAT's first flight, OSCAR 5, is itself a small lesson in how quickly the amateur satellite movement went international: the spacecraft was designed and built by students at the University of Melbourne, in Australia, with AMSAT providing engineering guidance from the United States, and it rode to orbit on a NASA Thor-Delta on 30 January 1970. Like its beacon-only predecessors, OSCAR 5 carried no transponder — but it proved that the amateur-satellite enterprise could now recruit talent and hardware from anywhere hams were licensed, not only from the group of California engineers who happened to have the right contacts. It also proved something more practical: that AMSAT, barely a year old, could act as a design-review and integration authority for a payload it had not built with its own hands, reviewing the Melbourne students' work against the same electrical, thermal, and structural requirements NASA imposed on any secondary payload sharing a launch vehicle. A newly chartered nonprofit had, within twelve months, gone from a filing cabinet and a checking account to a working relationship with a national space agency.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** A satellite orbits the whole planet, not just the country that launched it, and no single national amateur society can hear it for more than a few minutes at a time. From OSCAR 5 onward, amateur satellite work was structurally, not just sentimentally, international: a bird launched from a US range and built by Australian students needed listening posts on every continent to be tracked through a full orbit, and the pass schedules AMSAT published were of equal use to a ham in Melbourne, Manchester, or Minneapolis. Sister satellite organizations affiliated with AMSAT would go on to take root in the UK, in West Germany, and in Japan as the decade wore on — the same pattern IARU had established in 1925 for licensing and band planning, now repeating itself for orbital mechanics. A single ground station, however well built, can only see a satellite for a few minutes several times a day; a satellite's owners depend on a worldwide, cooperating network of independent amateur stations simply to know whether their spacecraft is still healthy between the passes their own home station can observe, which makes international cooperation a structural requirement of the enterprise rather than a courtesy extended to it.

Between OSCAR II and AMSAT's founding, a further handful of OSCARs flew across the mid-1960s, each testing incremental capability with mixed results — a normal, unglamorous attrition rate for a volunteer program building spacecraft on a hobbyist's budget rather than a national space agency's. The real turning point, the one that took amateur satellites from a beacon-listening curiosity to something an ordinary ham could actually use for two-way contacts, was AMSAT-OSCAR 6, launched 15 October 1972: AMSAT-OSCAR 6 carried a Mode A linear transponder plus a "Codestore" store-and-forward system, and its Mode A designation was the first entry in a lettering convention — Mode A, Mode B, and later letters as AMSAT's fleet grew — that named a transponder by its uplink/downlink band pair rather than by anything more descriptive, a shorthand satellite operators would use for the rest of the analog-transponder era. Where OSCAR 1 and its immediate successors carried only a beacon, AO-6 carried a working linear transponder — a payload that received an entire slice of the 2-meter band and retransmitted it, frequency-shifted, on 10 meters, so that dozens of ordinary hams could work each other through the satellite on SSB and CW at the same time, using nothing more exotic than the HF/VHF stations already in their shacks. A ham who had spent years calling CQ into the ordinary 10-meter noise floor could, for the first time, point a beam at the sky instead of the horizon and hear their own voice come back down clean, shifted onto a fresh piece of spectrum by a spacecraft they had never touched. "Codestore" added a second capability that reads, in retrospect, like an ancestor of the packet bulletin-board systems that Chapter 7 will describe: an onboard memory that could receive a message from one ground station and hold it for later retrieval by another, a primitive store-and-forward mailbox riding along with the transponder. AO-6 stayed in service for nearly five years, a remarkable run for a spacecraft built by volunteers, and it did more than any beacon-only OSCAR before it to convert "amateur satellites" from a curiosity a few hundred listeners tracked by ear into an operating discipline with its own literature, its own jargon, and its own growing population of regular practitioners.

Two years later, AMSAT-OSCAR 7 doubled the offer: launched 15 November 1974, it carried Mode A (2 m uplink → 10 m downlink) and Mode B (70 cm uplink → 2 m downlink) transponders side by side, giving operators a genuine choice for the first time. Where AO-6 gave operators one transponder, AO-7 gave them a choice of two, on different band pairs — a design decision that mattered because it let AMSAT's engineers, and the hams working through the bird, compare how a given amount of transponder power and bandwidth behaved at different frequencies, experience that would shape every satellite AMSAT built afterward. AO-7's eventual apparent battery failure in 1981, and its wholly unexpected return to life in 2002 — very likely because a permanently short-circuited battery cell finally opened, letting the solar panels power the spacecraft directly whenever they were illuminated — belongs to a later chapter of this book's story than the one being told here, but it is worth planting now: the amateur-built satellites of the early 1970s were engineered well enough, and simply enough, to keep doing useful work decades past when anyone expected them to.

While AMSAT's engineers were shrinking transponders into ten-kilogram boxes, the FCC was rewriting the license structure those same engineers, and everyone else in the hobby, operated under — and doing it in a way that generated more open anger inside amateur radio than almost anything before or since. The rulemaking is formally known as Docket 15928, and its stated purpose sounds almost bureaucratically mild: reward technical proficiency. In practice it meant carving the most desirable HF phone subbands away from General-class operators and reserving them for Advanced and Amateur Extra class licensees, who had passed harder written exams and, for Extra, a faster code test. The Commission had not invented this idea out of nowhere — the ARRL itself had proposed a version of "incentive licensing" in an October 1963 filing, and the League worked with the FCC on developing the concept from 1964 onward. But only the FCC could actually write and enforce the rule that resulted, and it did so as Report and Order FCC 67-978, released 29 August 1967, phased in over roughly two years. **FACT:** Incentive Licensing Phase I takes effect: HF subbands carved out exclusive to Advanced and Amateur Extra classes.

The reaction inside the hobby was immediate and, in a great many cases, furious. Thousands of General-class operators — some of them licensed for decades, competent, active, and in no sense the hobby's problem — woke up to find that privileges they had held and used for years were now walled off behind a subband boundary unless they sat for another exam: a harder written test, and for the top Amateur Extra class, code copy at 20 words per minute, four times the 5 wpm bar a Novice had to clear. A General-class phone operator who had spent a decade building friendships on 75-meter AM and SSB might find that his favorite slice of the band was now off-limits except to listen, unless he was willing to restudy theory he had last opened a decade earlier and prove Morse proficiency at a speed most working professionals never needed to touch again after their original exam. Because the ARRL had helped originate the underlying concept even though it had no authority to write the actual rule, resentment landed on the League's leadership almost as heavily as it landed on the Commission, an anger some corners of the hobby nursed for a very long time afterward — letters to *QST*'s editors in the years immediately following the rule's effective date make plain that a portion of the membership felt the League had traded away the interests of its ordinary dues-paying majority to reward a technically inclined minority, whatever the merits of the underlying goal. The FCC widened the affected HF phone subbands again in 1972, easing some of the operational friction, but the basic class-based subband structure — and the memory of how it arrived — persisted for a generation. It is one of the clean examples in this book's story of a regulatory decision landing at almost the same historical moment as a purely technical triumph, OSCAR 6's transponder going operational that same general period, with neither current paying the other much attention. Hams who were furious about losing 75-meter phone privileges that year could, the same evening, tune a 10-meter receiver and hear their own signal relayed back down from a spacecraft — the hobby's regulatory and technical stories running on separate tracks, as they often do in this book, meeting only in the shared licensee who lived through both at once.

The second technical revolution of the era made none of Washington's headlines, because no single agency ordered it. It happened because two unrelated trends collided: the FCC's tightening of channel spacing in the commercial land-mobile services, which left dealers and municipalities holding warehouses of suddenly obsolete VHF and UHF two-way radios, and a hobby that had 2-meter and 6-meter allocations it had barely used for voice because building a stable VHF FM transmitter and receiver from scratch was a nontrivial undertaking. Commercial gear solved the second problem by making the first problem someone else's inventory-clearance sale. **FACT:** 2 m FM repeater boom as hams retool surplus commercial land-mobile gear; CTCSS ("PL tone") adopted for repeater access control. A ham with a set of hand tools, a crystal-swapping habit, and a subscription to a surplus dealer's mailing list could turn a decommissioned police or taxi radio into a serviceable 2-meter FM rig for a fraction of the cost of anything built for the amateur market: pull the original crystal from its socket, order (or, for the more ambitious, hand-grind) a replacement ground for a new frequency inside the amateur allocation, retune the front-end and final-amplifier tank circuits to peak on the new frequency instead of the old commercial channel, and — because commercial gear was built to run for years unattended on a single crystal-controlled channel rather than to be casually retuned — accept that each channel change was a real bench session with a signal generator and a deviation meter, not the flip of a switch. Once enough hams had done that, the next obvious move was to take the best-sited one of those radios, put it on a hilltop or a tall building, and let it listen on one frequency and simultaneously repeat what it heard on another, so that low-power handhelds and car-mounted rigs down at street level could talk across a whole metropolitan area instead of just line-of-sight to each other.

That last sentence describes the amateur FM repeater, and through the 1970s repeaters multiplied faster than anyone was coordinating them. Two repeaters in the same region sharing an input/output pair, or a stray strong signal on someone's input frequency, could trigger a machine that had no way to tell a wanted user from noise or from a distant, unrelated station — a problem this chapter's How It Works section explains in detail, and one that pushed the hobby toward informal, then formal, regional frequency-coordination bodies as the decade went on. A club that wanted to put up a new machine increasingly had to ask a neighboring club, or a regional coordination committee, which input/output pairs were already spoken for within reach of its intended hilltop — the same kind of shared-resource bookkeeping the whole radio spectrum had needed since the first interference disputes of the spark era, now being reinvented from scratch, informally, by volunteers with no regulatory mandate to do it and no authority to enforce their decisions beyond the community's own goodwill.

Demand for the hardware to work these new machines was met, increasingly, not by American manufacturers but by Japanese ones — Icom, Yaesu, and Kenwood built inexpensive, compact, solid-state 2-meter FM equipment through the middle and late 1970s that made a repeater-capable mobile or handheld affordable to a much wider slice of the hobby than the surplus-conversion crowd alone, and by the back half of the decade a completely different kind of ham — one who had never touched a soldering iron in anger, who simply bought a small Japanese-built handheld and pushed a button — was walking around with a live connection to a citywide repeater network in a coat pocket. That last figure is worth pausing on, because it marks a real fork in what "being a ham" meant. The hobby's first six decades had rewarded, almost by definition, someone willing to build or at least deeply understand their own equipment; a repeater and a mass-produced handheld let a newly licensed Technician talk clearly across a metropolitan area on the first day of ownership, with no bench work, no crystal grinding, and only a passing need to understand what a discriminator or a duplexer actually did. Both kinds of ham — the one who had converted the surplus gear and sited the repeater, and the one who had simply bought a radio and pushed the button — were using exactly the same infrastructure, and needed each other to make it work at all.

### How It Works

Two technologies define this era's engineering: frequency modulation, in the specific, disciplined form the FCC's amateur rules and the era's equipment settled on for VHF/UHF voice, and the satellite transponder, which is best understood as a very large frequency converter sitting in orbit. Both rest on ideas the book has already introduced — modulation, bandwidth, mixing — pushed into new regimes. And both are, at bottom, applications of the same underlying question this book keeps returning to: given a fixed amount of spectrum, how much information can be carried through it, and how gracefully does a receiver behave when conditions are less than ideal? FM answers that question by trading bandwidth (a wideband FM signal occupies far more spectrum than an SSB signal carrying the same voice) for a specific, valuable kind of robustness against noise and co-channel interference; the satellite transponder answers a different but related question — how to make one shared, expensive, hard-to-replace piece of infrastructure serve as many simultaneous users as possible without demodulating and individually managing each one.

**Frequency modulation, rigorously.** Every modulation scheme this book has covered so far has impressed information onto a carrier's *amplitude* — CW's on/off keying is degenerate amplitude modulation, AM varies the envelope continuously, and SSB is amplitude modulation with the carrier and one sideband removed. FM abandons amplitude modulation entirely. The carrier's amplitude stays constant; information rides instead on the carrier's *instantaneous frequency*, which swings above and below its resting (center) value in direct proportion to the modulating audio's amplitude, and at a rate set by the modulating audio's own frequency. Where an AM transmitter's instantaneous frequency never moves at all — only its envelope amplitude changes — an FM transmitter's instantaneous frequency $f(t)$ tracks the modulating waveform $m(t)$ directly: $f(t) = f_c + \Delta f \cdot m(t)$, where $f_c$ is the resting center frequency and $m(t)$ is the normalized audio waveform swinging between $-1$ and $+1$. Everything about how FM is generated, received, and how much spectrum it occupies follows from that one relationship.

Generating that swing in period hardware meant modulating something in the transmitter's oscillator that directly sets its frequency — and the oscillator's frequency, this book's spine formula from the very first LC tank circuit onward, is $f_0 = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{LC})$. A *reactance modulator* is a stage (a vacuum tube or transistor biased to behave, from the tank circuit's point of view, like a variable capacitance) connected across the oscillator's tank and driven by the audio input; as audio swings the reactance modulator's effective capacitance up and down, it adds directly to the tank's own $C$, pulling $f_0$ up and down in step with the audio exactly as the deviation formula requires. Later designs did the same job with a varactor diode — a semiconductor junction whose capacitance varies with the reverse-bias voltage across it — wired directly across the tank, letting the audio voltage itself set the capacitance with no separate active stage at all. Either way, the technique is a direct descendant of the same resonant LC circuit this book introduced in its opening pages, now put to a new use: rather than simply setting a fixed operating frequency, the tank's capacitance is deliberately kept in motion.

That swing has a name and a standard size. The peak amount the carrier deviates from center is the deviation, written $\Delta f$. **FACT:** An estimate of FM occupied bandwidth, BW = 2(Δf + f_m), capturing ~98% of the signal's power. US amateur VHF/UHF FM through this era standardized on wideband deviation of $\Delta f = \pm 5\ \text{kHz}$, matched to 20 kHz channel spacing (decades later, "narrowbanding" would compress this to a fifth as much deviation on tighter channels, but that development belongs to a much later chapter). The ratio of that deviation to the highest audio frequency being sent defines the FM modulation index:

$$\beta = \frac{\Delta f}{f_m}$$

For voice-bandwidth FM with $f_m \approx 3\ \text{kHz}$ and $\Delta f = 5\ \text{kHz}$, $\beta \approx 1.67$ — comfortably above 1, which is what makes this "wideband" FM rather than the narrowband ($\beta < 1$) FM used in some other services. A modulation index above 1 means the carrier's frequency swing is large compared to the rate at which it's swinging, and that turns out to matter directly for how much spectrum the signal occupies.

Carson's rule gives the working answer to the bandwidth question, and it is the single most load-bearing formula an amateur working VHF FM needs:

$$BW \approx 2(\Delta f + f_m)$$

> **Worked example — Carson's-rule bandwidth of a standard 2-meter FM signal.** Take the era's standard values: $\Delta f = 5\ \text{kHz}$ peak deviation, $f_m = 3\ \text{kHz}$ maximum voice audio. Then $BW \approx 2(5\ \text{kHz} + 3\ \text{kHz}) = 2 \times 8\ \text{kHz} = 16\ \text{kHz}$. A 16 kHz-wide signal fits comfortably inside a 20 kHz channel with 2 kHz of guard band on either side — which is exactly why the era's 2-meter repeater and simplex channel spacing was set at 20 kHz in the first place. Carson's rule is not a precise cutoff; it is the bandwidth that captures roughly 98% of the signal's power, with a small residual spreading further in either direction at diminishing amplitude. But 98% is what a channel-planning engineer needs, and it is why the deviation standard and the channel-spacing standard were never independent numbers — one was engineered to fit the other.

FM's receiving side needs a fundamentally different demodulator from anything this book has described so far, because there is no envelope to detect and no beat frequency to mix down — the information is in the carrier's frequency, full stop, and amplitude carries nothing useful at all. That last fact is not a limitation; amateur and commercial FM receiver designers turned it into FM's decisive advantage.

An FM receiver's IF strip ends in a *limiter* — a deliberately overdriven amplifier stage that clips the IF signal hard, chopping off the top and bottom of the waveform so that whatever comes out has constant amplitude regardless of how the incoming signal's amplitude was varying. Anything that showed up as amplitude noise — atmospheric static, ignition noise, fading, a weaker co-channel signal riding underneath the wanted one — gets stripped away by the limiter before it ever reaches the next stage, because the limiter only passes the signal's *zero crossings*, its timing, not its height. What's left after limiting is a constant-amplitude signal whose frequency still faithfully carries the original modulation; only the amplitude-borne noise is gone.

That constant-amplitude signal then passes to the discriminator, the stage that actually converts instantaneous frequency back into an audio voltage.

{{fig:fm-detector}}

The classical circuits — the Foster-Seeley discriminator and its close relative the ratio detector — do this with a tuned transformer whose secondary is center-tapped and phase-shifted relative to the primary, arranged so that a signal exactly on the receiver's IF center frequency produces two equal, opposite half-wave-rectified voltages across the transformer's two halves that cancel to zero output. When the incoming signal's instantaneous frequency deviates above center, the phase relationship between primary and secondary shifts, unbalancing the two rectified halves and producing a net positive output; deviate below center and the imbalance reverses, producing a net negative output. The result is a demodulator whose output voltage tracks instantaneous frequency deviation directly and (over the useful range) linearly — exactly the audio waveform that was used to modulate the transmitter in the first place. The ratio detector rearranges the same basic tuned-transformer idea with an added capacitor that makes its output largely insensitive to any amplitude variation that slipped past the limiter, which made it popular in receivers built down to a price, where a marginal limiter stage could be tolerated if the discriminator itself didn't care about amplitude either. Practical FM receivers of the era commonly ran their IF strip at 10.7 MHz — high enough above the audio band and the deviation itself to leave the discriminator's tuned transformer comfortable room to operate linearly across the full ±5 kHz swing, and a frequency that would go on to become a near-universal FM-broadcast and amateur-VHF IF standard for decades after this era closed.

The limiter is also the direct physical cause of FM's most famous and most useful on-air behavior: the capture effect, an FM receiver's tendency to reproduce only the stronger of two co-channel signals once it leads by a few dB. Put two AM signals on the same frequency and a receiver reproduces some blend of both, typically an unpleasant heterodyne beat plus both audios fighting for the listener's attention — because an AM detector faithfully reproduces whatever composite amplitude envelope actually arrives at the antenna, and two signals of comparable strength genuinely do produce a composite envelope carrying both. Put two FM signals on the same frequency and the limiter changes the arithmetic entirely: it clips to constant amplitude regardless of what's underneath, and once one of the two signals is roughly 2–3 dB stronger than the other, the combined waveform's zero crossings — the thing the limiter actually preserves and passes on — are dominated almost completely by the stronger signal. In power terms, using the book's decibel relation $dB = 10\log_{10}(P_2/P_1)$, a 3 dB edge is only a 2:1 power ratio — a signal barely twice as strong as its rival, nowhere close to the 10:1 or 100:1 margins an AM or SSB operator would want before feeling confident of full quieting over an interferer. That a mere doubling of power is enough to swing the discriminator's output almost entirely to one signal is the whole of the capture effect in one number. The discriminator then reproduces essentially only that stronger signal; the weaker one is suppressed nearly to silence rather than blended in. This is why an FM receiver on a strong local signal sounds completely clean — "full quieting," in the era's own vocabulary — right up until the signal weakens past a threshold, at which point the noise doesn't creep in gradually the way it does on AM or SSB; it arrives abruptly, because the limiter has stopped having a clearly dominant signal to lock its output to. FM trades AM and SSB's graceful, linear degradation for a threshold behavior: superb above the threshold, rough below it. That tradeoff is precisely why FM became the era's VHF local/regional voice mode of choice — strong, line-of-sight, well-engineered repeater links stay comfortably above the threshold — while SSB remained the tool of choice for HF DX, where operators routinely work signals many dB into the noise and need every bit of that graceful degradation.

**Repeater duplex operation.** A hilltop repeater's entire value proposition is that it can hear a weak handheld signal that would never reach another handheld directly, and retransmit it with enough power and antenna height that every handheld and mobile in the coverage area hears the retransmission clearly. Doing that simultaneously — receiving and transmitting at the same moment, on the same shared antenna system, without the repeater's own powerful transmitted signal overwhelming its own much more sensitive receiver — is the specific engineering problem repeater duplex operation solves.

{{fig:repeater-duplex}}

The solution has two parts. First, the repeater receives on one frequency (its *input*) and transmits on a different frequency (its *output*), separated by a fixed offset — standard practice on 2 meters settled on $\pm 600\ \text{kHz}$, so a repeater with an output (the frequency listeners tune to) of 146.850 MHz has an input (the frequency users transmit on) of 146.250 MHz, 600 kHz below it. A user's handheld or mobile is set to receive on the output and transmit on the input; the radio does the frequency-switching automatically the moment the user keys the microphone, which is what "working split" or "using the offset" means in amateur practice. Second, because input and output are only 600 kHz apart rather than being widely separated bands, a simple filter cannot cleanly keep the repeater's own transmitter energy out of its own receiver front end using ordinary means — so repeater sites use a *duplexer*, a set of sharply tuned cavity filters, plumbed so that a single shared antenna can be used for both receiving and transmitting at once, with the duplexer providing enough isolation between the two paths that the transmitter doesn't desensitize or destroy the receiver sitting a few feet away in the same equipment rack.

The 600 kHz figure is specific to 2 meters; other VHF and UHF amateur bands that grew repeater activity through the same decade settled on their own standard offsets, each large enough to give a duplexer's cavity filters something to work with relative to the signal's own bandwidth, and each eventually written down in the regional band plans the same coordination bodies were assembling. The offset solves duplex operation, but it does not by itself solve the problem of *which* signals a repeater should retransmit. A repeater that opens its transmitter for anything it hears on its input frequency will just as happily repeat a distant co-channel station, ignition noise, or another repeater sharing the same pair miles away — and as more repeaters went up through the 1970s, this kind of mutual triggering became a real, recurring nuisance. The fix amateur repeater builders borrowed from the same commercial land-mobile world that had supplied their surplus hardware in the first place was CTCSS, Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System, marketed commercially under Motorola's trademark "Private Line" and universally nicknamed the "PL tone" in amateur use whether or not the equipment involved was actually Motorola's. A CTCSS-equipped transmitter rides a continuous, low-frequency tone — in the roughly 67–254 Hz range, well below the bottom of ordinary voice audio — underneath the user's speech on every transmission. The repeater's receiver decodes that tone with a narrow filter and only opens (keys) its own transmitter when the correct tone is present, ignoring signals on its input frequency that carry the wrong tone, no tone at all, or just noise. Because the tone sits below the voice band and gets filtered back out of the audio the repeater actually retransmits, ordinary listeners never hear it — hence "sub-audible" in the operating vocabulary, even though the tone itself is not literally inaudible to a human ear presented with it in isolation. CTCSS spread through amateur repeaters piecemeal across the 1970s as coordinators and repeater trustees adopted it to keep growing, increasingly crowded repeater pairs from stepping on each other.

**Satellite transponders.** A repeater and a satellite transponder solve related problems with fundamentally different architectures, and the difference is worth being precise about. A repeater demodulates its input signal down to audio and remodulates a fresh carrier on the output frequency — it is, functionally, a receiver bolted to a transmitter, working one FM voice channel at a time. A linear transponder does not demodulate anything.

{{fig:transponder}}

It takes an entire slice of spectrum on its uplink band — commonly tens of kilohertz wide — and translates the whole slice, as a block, down or up to a different downlink band, the same way a superheterodyne's mixer translates an entire received band to its IF: multiply the incoming RF by a stable onboard local oscillator, and every signal within the passband comes out the other side shifted by the same fixed amount, still bearing whatever modulation it arrived with. AO-6's and AO-7's Mode A transponders took a slice of the 2-meter band as uplink and retransmitted it, shifted, on 10 meters; AO-7's additional Mode B transponder took 70 centimeters up and retransmitted on 2 meters. Because the whole passband is relayed at once rather than one channel being decoded and rebuilt, dozens of independent SSB and CW conversations can occupy the same transponder simultaneously, each pair of operators picking their own small slice of the passband to work in, exactly as they would pick a clear frequency on an ordinary HF band — the satellite does not care how many signals are inside its passband any more than a mirror cares how many people are looking into it at once.

One subtlety a new satellite operator learns quickly is that a linear transponder's frequency translation can invert the passband. If the onboard mixing scheme produces the downlink by subtracting the uplink frequency from a fixed onboard oscillator rather than adding a fixed offset to it, then the sense of the translation flips: tune the uplink frequency upward, and the corresponding downlink frequency moves *downward* rather than up, mirror-imaged around the transponder's center. A non-inverting transponder behaves the way intuition expects — up on the uplink means up on the downlink — but an inverting one does not, and a station working an inverting transponder for the first time, chasing what sounds like a drifting signal in the wrong direction, quickly learns to check which kind of bird they are working before touching the dial in a panic.

Operating through a linear transponder demands one habit that repeater work never requires: the operator must listen to their own downlinked signal, live, on a separate receiver tuned to the transponder's output band, while transmitting on the uplink band. Because the whole passband shifts together but each operator's own signal appears at a fixed offset from where they're transmitting, hearing your own downlink is the only reliable way to confirm you are actually inside the transponder's passband, at the level you intend, and not obliterating it with excessive uplink power (a transponder's total downlink power is shared across everyone using it at that moment, so one overdriven uplink signal can desensitize or "capture" the whole transponder at everyone else's expense). This full-duplex listen-while-you-transmit discipline turns out to be exactly the tool an operator needs to manage the era's other satellite-specific headache.

**Doppler shift.** A satellite in low Earth orbit — the regime AO-6 and AO-7 both occupied — moves at an along-track speed on the order of 7.5 km/s relative to the ground below it.

{{fig:doppler}}

A ground station does not care about the satellite's total speed; what shifts the received frequency is only the *line-of-sight* component of that velocity, the rate at which the range between satellite and ground station is closing or opening:

$$\Delta f = f \cdot \frac{v}{c}$$

where $f$ is the nominal (unshifted) transmitted or received frequency, $c$ is the speed of light, and $v$ is taken positive when the satellite is approaching (closing) and negative when receding. This is the same relation, and the same underlying physics, that makes a passing train's whistle sound higher in pitch on approach and lower in retreat — only here the "sound" is a radio carrier and the speeds involved are orbital rather than locomotive, which is exactly why the shift is measured in kilohertz on a carrier around a hundred megahertz rather than in a barely perceptible change of musical pitch. As a satellite rises above the horizon at the start of a pass, it is approaching the ground station about as directly as its orbit geometry allows, so $v$ is close to its maximum closing value and the received signal is shifted well above its nominal frequency. As the satellite climbs toward its point of closest approach, the line-of-sight component shrinks toward zero — precisely at closest approach, for a pass that goes directly overhead, $v$ crosses through zero and the Doppler shift vanishes for an instant — and then, as the satellite recedes toward the opposite horizon, $v$ swings negative and grows in magnitude again, pulling the received frequency down below nominal. The overall shape is a smooth S-curve in frequency over the several minutes of a pass, and the curve's steepest, fastest-changing part is right around the moment of closest approach on a high-elevation pass — the least intuitive part of the whole phenomenon, since that is also the moment the satellite feels closest and most "settled" overhead.

> **Worked example — Doppler on a 2-meter uplink, AO-7 Mode A.** Take a favorable pass where the satellite's line-of-sight closing speed reaches close to its full along-track value, $v \approx 7.5\ \text{km/s} = 7{,}500\ \text{m/s}$, on a Mode A uplink near $f = 146\ \text{MHz}$. Using $c \approx 3\times10^{8}\ \text{m/s}$:
> $$\Delta f = f \cdot \frac{v}{c} = 146\times10^{6}\ \text{Hz} \times \frac{7{,}500}{3\times10^{8}} \approx 146\times10^{6} \times 2.5\times10^{-5} \approx 3{,}650\ \text{Hz}$$
> — roughly 3.6 kHz of shift, entirely capable of carrying a signal outside a transponder's passband, or outside another station's SSB filter, if the operator does not compensate. Because Doppler shift scales directly with frequency, the same pass geometry on AO-7's Mode B downlink, near 146 MHz, produces a comparable shift, while the *uplink* of Mode B, near 435 MHz, sees roughly three times as much: about 10.9 kHz. This is the origin of a rule every satellite operator of the era learned quickly: tune the downlink, not the uplink. A downlink shift is easy to correct — simply retune the receiver to follow the drifting signal by ear. Correcting the uplink is harder, because the operator cannot directly hear their own transmitted frequency drift relative to the satellite; the only practical way to know the uplink needs adjusting is to listen to one's own signal coming back down the (Doppler-shifted) downlink and nudge the uplink VFO to keep that returned signal centered where it belongs.

**Basic tracking.** None of this is useful without first knowing when a satellite will be overhead at all. AO-6 and AO-7 each orbit the Earth roughly every hour and a half, appearing above a given ground station's horizon for a window of only a few minutes several times a day, at times that shift from day to day as the orbit precesses relative to the rotating Earth beneath it. AMSAT computed and published orbital predictions — rise time, maximum elevation, set time, and the compass bearing to point an antenna — using the same Keplerian orbital elements that trackers still use today, though 1970s-era hams worked those predictions by hand or with simple mechanical aids rather than the pocket computers of later decades: published pass tables, hand-plotted ground-track charts on a world map, and directional VHF/UHF antennas on a mast that had to be aimed, by hand or with a modest rotator, toward a moving point in the sky that was only above the horizon for a few minutes at a time.

The full station this required was, by the standards of an ordinary HF operating position, an unusual amount of simultaneous work for one operator. A satellite pass demanded a receiver tracking the downlink continuously (retuning by ear as Doppler pulled the signal across the passband), a separate transmitter on the uplink band being nudged in the same direction to keep the operator's own downlinked signal centered, a directional antenna being walked across the sky in both azimuth and elevation as the satellite rose, crossed, and set, and a log being kept of it all — inside a window that might last six or eight minutes on a favorable high-elevation pass and considerably less on a low one skimming the horizon. Clubs often split the job among two or three operators working together at one station, one on the dial, one on the antenna rotator, one logging, precisely because doing all of it correctly, alone, in real time, while also trying to hold an intelligible conversation through a transponder shared with a dozen other stations, pushed against the practical limit of what one person could manage with the era's manually operated equipment. Getting the antenna pointed, the receiver tuned to account for Doppler, and a QSO logged inside that narrow window was as much a discipline of timing and preparation as it was a discipline of radio.

### The State of the Art in 1968, 1974, and 1976

No single Handbook year can capture this chapter's span the way the 1951 edition captured the arrival of SSB, because the era's defining technologies — orbital satellites and VHF FM repeaters — barely existed at its start and were still spreading, unevenly, by its end. The three editions this chapter draws on, 1968, 1974, and 1976, sit at three different points along that spread, and the differences between them trace the era's arc as clearly as any narrative can.

A station built from the 1968 edition's pages still looked, in its bones, like a station from a decade earlier: an HF transmitter and receiver, increasingly built as a single transceiver in the Collins mold but still built mostly around vacuum tubes in its power stages, tuned by hand across CW and SSB. That was also the year incentive licensing's core subband carve-out took effect — a rulemaking with no circuit diagram attached to it at all, but one that shaped, as much as any piece of hardware did, which parts of the HF spectrum a given ham's station could legally use. A newly minted General-class licensee that November found the shape of the band itself had changed under him overnight: portions of 75, 40, 20, and 15 meters phone he could have worked freely a year earlier were now marked, on the same dial he had always used, as Advanced- and Extra-only. VHF material in a 1968-vintage Handbook leaned toward weak-signal CW and SSB work and long-distance experiments like moonbounce, not local FM — repeaters were only just beginning to appear, mostly as one-off projects built by technically ambitious individual clubs rather than as an established category of station a general reader could expect to build from a standard set of plans. Satellite work in 1968 meant, at best, beacon listening and telemetry logging in the tradition OSCAR 1 had set — there was as yet no linear transponder to work two-way through, and AMSAT itself was still a year from being chartered; that capability was still four years away.

By 1974, the picture had visibly shifted. Solid-state circuitry had displaced tubes through most of a station's low- and medium-power stages, though tubes still dominated the highest-power HF final amplifiers, where contemporary transistors could not yet match a tube's voltage and dissipation headroom. FM and repeater construction had grown from a specialty into an established Handbook subject in its own right, reflecting a hobby in which a meaningful and fast-growing fraction of licensees now owned a 2-meter FM rig, whether converted from surplus commercial gear or bought new from an increasingly competitive Japanese import market — a rig that, unlike the crystal-swapped commercial conversions of a few years earlier, was now as likely as not designed from the ground up for the amateur bands and priced to compete for a hobbyist's discretionary budget rather than a police department's procurement office. And satellite operation, though still a minority pursuit requiring real dedication — a station capable of both HF/VHF transmission and reception, a tracking method, and the patience to work short, unpredictable windows — was no longer purely theoretical for an ordinary reader; AMSAT-OSCAR 6, in orbit since October 1972, was there to be worked by anyone willing to build the station and learn the discipline. A 1974-vintage reader could, in the same afternoon, read a construction article for a 2-meter FM transceiver aimed squarely at working the new repeater going up across town, and an operating article explaining how to compute Doppler correction for a pass of a spacecraft orbiting hundreds of kilometers overhead — the near and the far edges of VHF amateur radio, printed a few pages apart.

The 1976 edition caught the era just past its second satellite milestone, AO-7 having reached orbit in November 1974, and reflected a repeater culture that had grown large enough to need active coordination between neighboring machines rather than the ad hoc goodwill that had gotten the first repeaters on the air — a machine going up now had to find an unused input/output pair, and increasingly had to clear that choice with a regional coordinating body rather than simply picking a likely-looking frequency and hoping. It also, in its equipment-review pages, showed the first faint signs of the chapter to come: a handful of the era's most expensive top-of-line HF and VHF rigs beginning to advertise frequency synthesis — a way of generating a stable, precisely settable operating frequency electronically, without a physical crystal for every channel — as a novel, costly feature rather than a standard one. Most stations built from 1976-vintage plans still tuned with an analog VFO on HF or switched between a bank of individually purchased crystals on FM, one crystal per channel, one more crystal to buy and cut every time a new repeater went up somewhere in range.

That last detail is where the era's unfinished business sat. A repeater network that kept growing, city by city and channel pair by channel pair, was outrunning the crystal-bank radio's ability to keep up with it — every new machine on the air meant another trip to a crystal supplier, another few weeks' wait, another socket filled in a radio that could hold only so many, and a mobile rig with eight crystal sockets was, by the standards of a growing regional repeater map, already out of room. A ham standing in front of a rack of crystal envelopes, trying to decide which of the region's dozen repeaters was worth the next slot, was standing at the edge of a problem that transistors and tube-to-solid-state conversion alone could not solve; shrinking the parts and switching the material had made VHF FM equipment cheaper and lighter, but it had not changed the fact that each channel still needed its own separately ordered, separately ground sliver of quartz.

Somewhere in a handful of laboratories, an answer to that exact problem — a single stable crystal reference, a divider a microprocessor could reprogram in software, and a VCO locked to whichever frequency the divider was told to produce — was just beginning to leave the lab bench for the amateur market, expensive and rare enough in 1976 to be a footnote in a Handbook's back pages rather than a chapter of its own. It would take a few more years, and a great deal more work squeezing that circuit down to a price an ordinary ham could justify, before a radio could offer as many channels as a crystal box could ever have held, dialed up from the front panel instead of unscrewed from the case. Until then, the rack of crystal envelopes kept growing, one repeater at a time.
