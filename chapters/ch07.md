## 7. The Microprocessor and the First Digital Modes (1977–1990)

*"September 1978: Peter Martinez, G3PLX, makes the first amateur AMTOR contact, using a Motorola 6800-based microcomputer."*
*— per the ARRL historical record*

### The Story

In 1977 a well-equipped ham's station still spoke one of two dialects that would have been recognizable to an operator from twenty years earlier. The first was crystal control: a socket on the front panel, or a bank of them, each holding a quartz crystal ground to oscillate at one specific frequency, so that working a given channel meant physically owning a crystal cut for it and plugging the right one in before you transmitted. The second was the analog VFO, a continuously tunable LC (or, by the 1970s, mostly transistorized) oscillator that could reach any frequency in its range but that drifted with temperature, with supply-voltage sag, with the cabinet warming up over the course of an evening's operating — and that could not, by its nature, be told to remember a frequency and return to it later without an operator's hand on the dial. Neither dialect scaled gracefully. A 2-meter FM operator who wanted to work every repeater within range of a mobile rig needed a crystal pair — one for receive, one for transmit — for every single machine, purchased individually, cut to order, and mailed from a crystal house that might take weeks to fill an order. An HF operator chasing a weak DX station up and down a crowded band needed an oscillator stable enough to find a frequency and precise enough to return to it exactly, which a bare LC VFO, however well built, essentially could not promise.

That status quo did not end because oscillators got better. It ended because a way of generating a frequency that had nothing to do with either crystals or free-running LC tanks arrived in amateur gear from outside the hobby entirely, riding on the same wave of cheap digital logic that was, in the same years, putting calculators on desks and digital watches on wrists. The frequency synthesizer — a voltage-controlled oscillator locked to a stable crystal reference through a feedback loop, its output frequency set not by which physical component was installed but by a number loaded into a digital divider — had existed in commercial and military equipment since the 1960s, when it was expensive, power-hungry, and built from discrete logic filling a rack. What changed in the second half of the 1970s was that the same function collapsed onto one or two integrated circuits cheap enough for a $500 amateur transceiver, at almost exactly the moment an equally cheap microprocessor became available to control it — to accept a keypad frequency entry or a memory-channel recall, compute the divider value the synthesizer needed, and load it in. **Late 1970s–1980s: PLL-synthesized, microprocessor-managed transceivers become mainstream, displacing crystal-bank/VFO-only designs (period trend, not a single date)**, and that displacement is the mechanical spine this chapter hangs everything else on.

The shift showed up first at the top of the market and worked its way down. General-coverage HF transceivers like R. L. Drake's TR7, introduced in 1978, were built around frequency synthesis and a microprocessor from the ground up rather than having digital control bolted onto an inherited analog VFO design; Icom's early synthesizer-based HF transceivers followed a similar path. By the early 1980s the same architecture had reached the VHF handheld — the sort of radio a ham carried, not just operated from a desk — with keypad-tunable, memory-equipped 2-meter FM handhelds from Icom and Yaesu putting synthesis and microprocessor control into a device that fit in a shirt pocket. Where a mid-1970s handheld had offered a handful of crystal-controlled channels, chosen once at the factory or the dealer's counter and effectively permanent, a synthesized handheld of the early 1980s offered a numeric keypad for direct frequency entry, a small LCD readout in place of a mechanical dial, and typically a few dozen memory channels an operator could fill and refill at will, on the same piece of hardware, as often as the local repeater landscape changed. What all of these radios shared, whatever the badge on the front panel, was the same internal logic: a crystal reference, a phase-locked loop, and a microprocessor that translated a human's frequency request into the one number the loop actually needed. The details of how that loop works are the first full teaching section of this chapter; for the story, what matters is that "memory channel" stopped being a euphemism for a crystal socket and became a genuine piece of digital storage — a few bytes in low-power CMOS RAM, later nonvolatile EEPROM, holding the divider value for a frequency the radio could recall instantly, with no soldering iron and no mail-order wait.

The same wave of cheap microprocessors that reorganized the transceiver's front end was, in these same years, showing up on operating desks in an entirely different guise: as the home computer. Apple II, Commodore PET, and Radio Shack's TRS-80 all reached the market in 1977; the IBM PC followed in 1981. None of these machines was built with amateur radio in mind, but every one of them had exactly the two things a ham needed to bring digital modes into the shack — a keyboard for composing text and a serial or parallel port for talking to outside hardware — and hams, who had never needed much invitation to repurpose consumer electronics, started doing exactly that almost as soon as the machines were affordable. Where a 1960s RTTY operator had needed a mechanical teleprinter — a Model 15 or Model 19 Teletype, a heavy, oil-smelling machine that printed Baudot code onto paper tape or a page one hammer-strike at a time — a 1980s operator increasingly needed only a video terminal or a home computer running terminal software, connected through a simple interface to whatever piece of amateur radio hardware translated between the computer's serial data and the radio's audio. That interface hardware, and the protocols riding on it, is the chapter's second thread.

RTTY itself, the amateur radio-teletype mode inherited more or less unchanged from the commercial and military practice of the 1930s and 1940s, was the obvious starting point and the obvious problem. Its five-bit Baudot code (formally ITA2) packed the alphabet, digits, and punctuation into just thirty-two combinations by using two "shift" characters — Letters and Figures — to reuse the same five bits for two different symbol sets depending on which shift a station had last sent. That economy came at a cost: RTTY carried no error detection of any kind. A single corrupted bit turned one character into a different, wrong character with no way for the receiving equipment to know anything had gone wrong at all — and if the corrupted bit happened to be part of a shift character, everything printed afterward could scramble into garbage until the next deliberate shift arrived to resynchronize letters and figures. On a good day on a clean VHF path this rarely mattered. On HF, fighting fading, multipath, and noise, it mattered constantly, and fixing it — giving a teleprinter mode some way to know when it had gotten a character wrong, and ideally some way to get a clean copy anyway — was exactly the problem an English ham took on in the fall of 1978.

**FACT:** Peter Martinez (G3PLX) develops AMTOR and makes the first amateur AMTOR contact, using a Motorola 6800-based microcomputer.
Martinez did not invent his solution from nothing; the maritime radio services had already solved the same problem for commercial shipping traffic, in a system called SITOR (Simplex Teleprinting Over Radio, standardized by the ITU as Recommendation 476), which used a constant-ratio error-detecting code and a request-repeat protocol to keep ship-to-shore telex traffic legible over noisy HF paths. AMTOR — Amateur Teleprinting Over Radio — was Martinez's adaptation of that maritime technique for the amateur bands, built, fittingly, on the same kind of cheap microprocessor hardware that was reshaping the rest of the hobby: a Motorola 6800-based home computer doing the coding and timing work a dedicated maritime terminal would have done in purpose-built hardware. The technical detail of how the code actually catches errors belongs in the How It Works section below; what belongs in the story is that within two years, American hams wanted the same capability, and the FCC let a handful of them try it under experimental authority before writing it into the regular rules.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** AMTOR's arrival is a small, clean example of a pattern that runs through this whole chapter and the ones before it: a single ham, working essentially alone, taking a technique developed for an entirely different (and considerably better-funded) radio service and re-deriving it for amateur use with whatever hardware happened to be on the hobbyist market that year. Martinez was not affiliated with a national maritime authority or an equipment manufacturer; he was a licensed amateur with access to a microcomputer and a copy of the ITU's SITOR specification, working the problem in his own time. The mode he built spread from the UK across the IARU's member societies with no patent and no licensing fee attached to it, arriving in the United States as an FCC-sanctioned experiment within two years of its first on-air test — a transmission speed for a new mode to cross an ocean and a regulatory border that had no precedent in the amateur radio of the tube era, and that would become the norm for every digital mode this book covers from here forward.

**FACT:** FCC grants a Special Temporary Authority for AMTOR to four US hams (K4PA, K3FLS, W3KET, KB6BT).
The grant, in 1980, let four American stations put AMTOR on the air under experimental license conditions rather than waiting for a full rulemaking, and by the time the FCC formally opened the mode to the wider amateur population later in the decade, dealer-built and home-built AMTOR terminal units were already a fixture of the HF digital subbands — RTTY's noisier, more error-prone older cousin now sharing spectrum with a mode that could at least tell when it had been misheard, and, in one of its two operating modes, actually do something about it.

Packet radio's American origin point is more institutional than AMTOR's, and it starts, appropriately for a chapter about a decade increasingly organized around computer clubs and hobbyist societies, at a meeting of a computer club.
**FACT:** A Tucson IEEE Computer Society meeting leads to the founding of TAPR (Tucson Amateur Packet Radio).
The organization that grew out of that October 1981 meeting set itself a specific, concrete goal: take the packet-switching ideas that had been circulating in the computer-networking world for most of a decade — ARPANET's store-and-forward routing, and especially the ITU-T's X.25 standard for public packet-switched data networks, finalized in 1976 and by the late 1970s spreading through commercial and academic computing — and adapt just enough of that framework to work over an amateur radio channel. The result, formalized as AX.25 ("Amateur X.25"), borrowed X.25's link-layer framing almost directly while discarding the parts of the full X.25 stack that made no sense without a telephone company's leased-line infrastructure behind it. What TAPR was importing, more than any specific bit pattern, was a way of thinking about a radio channel that had nothing to do with propagation or modulation and everything to do with computer science: a message broken into discrete, individually addressed, individually verified chunks, relayed automatically through intermediate stations that made local forwarding decisions without a human operator's intervention, and reassembled correctly at the far end no matter how many hops or how much interference happened along the way. Nothing in the previous six chapters of this book had asked a ham to think in those terms. Packet radio asked it of everyone who wanted to use it.

**FACT:** TAPR ships the first TNC in kit form (TNC-1; the TNC-2 followed), making AX.25 packet radio practical and affordable.
The TNC — Terminal Node Controller — was the piece of hardware that made the whole scheme usable by someone who was not willing to write assembly-language framing code from scratch: a small computer in its own right, sitting between a ham's terminal or home computer on one side and an ordinary FM transceiver's microphone and speaker jacks on the other, that assembled outgoing data into properly framed, addressed, checksummed AX.25 packets and disassembled incoming audio back into data, entirely independent of whatever software the operator's own computer happened to be running. TAPR's second design, the TNC-2, proved simpler and cheaper to build than the original and was licensed out for other manufacturers to clone; within a couple of years, TNC-2-compatible controllers were being sold by AEA, Kantronics, MFJ, Heathkit, and a growing list of others, most of them built around the same handful of off-the-shelf parts — commonly a Zilog Z80 or similar 8-bit microprocessor for the framing logic and a dedicated FSK modem chip for the audio interface — which is a large part of why TNCs from half a dozen different manufacturers could all talk to one another without friction. A packet station no longer required TAPR's own hardware; it required only a TNC that spoke the same protocol, and by the middle of the decade, plenty did.

Once enough hams had TNCs on the air, the network topology took shape almost by itself, the way the 2-meter repeater network had a decade earlier. A station out of direct range of the party it wanted to reach could route its packets through an intermediate station — a digipeater — that simply retransmitted anything addressed with its own callsign in the frame's path field, extending a packet conversation's effective range the same way a voice repeater extended a conversation's effective range, but doing it frame by frame rather than continuously. Clubs put digipeaters on the same hilltops and tower sites that already held their FM repeaters, and by the mid-1980s a packet station in many metropolitan areas could reach not just the next town over but, hop by hop, much of a region. On top of that bare relay function grew a genuine amateur digital culture: packet bulletin-board systems (mailboxes that stored messages for later retrieval, not unlike AMSAT-OSCAR 6's earlier "Codestore" experiment described in the previous chapter), and, from 1985 onward, DX packet-spotting networks — Dick Newell's (AK1A) PacketCluster software let a group of HF operators share real-time spots of rare stations across a linked packet network, a foretaste of the internet-connected spotting networks that would dominate serious DXing a generation later. A further piece of firmware, sold under the name NET/ROM, replaced a stock TNC-2's control software with something that could compute multi-hop routes automatically instead of requiring an operator to type out every intermediate digipeater by hand — a small but real step from "link-layer relay" toward "network-layer routing," the same conceptual leap that separates a chain of repeaters from an actual computer network.

All of this produced a genuinely new kind of operator, one whose closest kin on the bands was arguably not another ham at all but the bulletin-board hobbyists dialing into a local computer over a telephone modem in the very same years. A packet operator's evening might be spent not talking, in any sense a CW or SSB operator would recognize, but composing a message at a keyboard, addressing it to a callsign-plus-SSID somewhere across town or across the region, and letting the network of digipeaters and mailboxes deliver it unattended, sometimes over several hops and several hours, with no two human beings ever occupying the channel at the same moment. Old-guard operators who had learned the hobby on crystal-controlled CW gear did not always warm to this immediately — a "QSO" that consisted of an unattended machine relaying a store-and-forward message struck some traditionalists as barely amateur radio at all, closer to a computer network that merely happened to use a radio link instead of a wire. But the two cultures shared the same license class, often the same club newsletter, and increasingly the same shack, and by the back half of the decade a great many stations ran both: an SSB or CW rig for real-time conversation, and a second VHF radio, tethered to a TNC and a home computer, quietly relaying packet traffic in the background whether its owner was at the microphone or not.

While packet and AMTOR were rewriting how a ham's data got from one station to another, an entirely separate, purely regulatory thread was rewriting which frequencies a ham could use to do it. In 1979, delegates from around the world met in Geneva for the World Administrative Radio Conference — WARC-79 — and, among a great many other items on a crowded agenda, carved out three new amateur allocations that had not existed before, at 30, 17, and 12 meters. The new bands entered into force internationally on 1 January 1982, but "internationally in force" and "available to a licensed US ham" were two different things separated, in practice, by however long it took the FCC to write and adopt its own domestic implementing rules — and the three new bands did not arrive in the United States together, or quickly.
**FACT:** FCC authorizes US amateur use of the 30-meter band (10.100–10.150 MHz) — the first WARC-79 band opened to US hams (secondary, CW/digital only).
Thirty meters reached American operators barely ten months after the international allocation took effect, and its restriction to secondary status, CW and digital modes only, turned out to be an almost perfect fit for a chapter about digital modes: a brand-new sliver of HF spectrum, deliberately kept off-limits to voice, opened right at the moment RTTY, AMTOR, and packet were all looking for uncrowded real estate to operate in. Twelve meters followed in 1985, its exact rollout date less crisply documented than 30 meters' but the year itself well established; seventeen meters brought up the rear, reaching US hams only in the closing days of January 1989, under a Report and Order the FCC had adopted a few days earlier and released on the 31st. By the time this chapter's span closes in 1990, the three WARC bands were fully part of the American amateur's HF toolkit, but a ham reading a mid-decade Handbook would have found only 30 meters actually usable, and even that only for CW and digital work — a regulatory footnote that, decade by decade, this book keeps returning to: the physics and the protocols usually arrive well before the paperwork catches up.

By the second half of the 1980s, an HF or VHF band in the United States held a genuinely mixed population of operators in a way that would have looked strange a decade earlier. CW and SSB traffic, run by hams whose operating habits had not changed in any fundamental way since the postwar era this book covered two chapters ago, shared spectrum with a fast-growing minority running RTTY, AMTOR, and packet — modes whose practitioners increasingly cared as much about baud rates, error-correction schemes, and TNC firmware revisions as about propagation and antenna gain. Successive editions of the ARRL Handbook tracked that shift edition by edition: the 1977 book still treated "digital" mostly as a matter of TTL logic gates and frequency counters bolted onto otherwise-conventional gear; by 1981 microprocessor-based construction projects and digital-readout synthesizers had become a normal part of the book's content rather than a novelty; by 1983, with TNC-1 kits just reaching the market, packet radio and AMTOR had earned real operating-chapter attention of their own. A ham who had learned the hobby on crystal-controlled CW gear in the 1950s and was still active in 1988 had, without necessarily building a single new antenna, watched the entire control layer of the station — how you picked a frequency, and increasingly how you exchanged information once you were on it — get replaced under his hands.

### How It Works

Two technical stories run through this chapter, and they share a single enabling component. One story is about how a radio finds and holds a frequency: the phase-locked loop, and the microprocessor that tells it which frequency to find. The other is about how data finds its way from one station to another intact: the AX.25 packet protocol, and the microprocessor (inside a TNC, this time, rather than inside the transceiver) that assembles, checks, and relays it. Both stories are, at bottom, about a general-purpose digital controller taking over a job that used to require either a physical, single-purpose component (a crystal, cut for one frequency) or a skilled human being applying judgment in real time (a RTTY operator manually resending a garbled line). Understanding both jobs in real depth is this section's task.

**Why crystal control had to end.** A crystal oscillator is stable precisely because it is *not* adjustable — the crystal's mechanical resonance fixes the frequency, and fixing the frequency is what gives a Pierce oscillator (Chapter 3) its excellent stability in the first place. That is a fine trade when a station only ever needs to operate on a small, fixed set of channels, but it is a poor trade for a mode of operation that wants to touch a different frequency every few minutes — scanning a band for activity, following a DX station as it moves, hopping between a dozen local repeaters. An analog VFO solves the "any frequency" problem but gives up the "stable and repeatable" property that made the crystal attractive to begin with: an LC tank's resonant frequency depends on physical component values that shift with temperature and age, so a raw VFO can drift measurably over the course of a single QSO and cannot, without external help, return to an exact frequency it last used an hour ago. What amateur radio needed, and what the rest of the electronics industry had already built for other purposes, was a circuit that combined a crystal's absolute stability with a VFO's continuous tunability — and did it in a form small and cheap enough for a $200 handheld.

**Anatomy of a synthesizer loop.** A phase-locked-loop (PLL) frequency synthesizer achieves exactly that combination using four functional blocks arranged in a feedback loop: a stable **crystal reference** oscillator, a **phase detector**, a **loop filter**, and a **voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO)** whose output is the actual transmitted or received RF, fed back through a **programmable ÷N divider** to close the loop.

{{fig:pll}}

The reference crystal runs at a fixed, known frequency and needs no adjustment; it plays the same role a crystal always has since Chapter 3 — an unimpeachably stable timing standard. The VCO is the loop's tunable element: unlike a plain LC oscillator's mechanical tuning capacitor, its frequency is set by a DC control voltage applied to a varactor diode (a diode whose junction capacitance changes with reverse bias), so raising or lowering that voltage smoothly raises or lowers the VCO's output frequency across a wide range. Left to itself, a VCO is no more stable than any other free-running oscillator — its whole purpose in this circuit is to be *steered*, continuously, by the loop around it. That steering happens through the phase detector, which compares two signals — the reference crystal's output on one input, and the VCO's output divided down by the programmable ÷N divider on the other — and produces an output proportional to the phase (equivalently, frequency) difference between them. The loop filter smooths that error signal into a clean DC control voltage and feeds it to the VCO, closing the loop: if the divided-down VCO output is running slightly slow relative to the reference, the phase detector's output nudges the VCO's control voltage up; if it's running fast, the voltage gets nudged down. Given a few milliseconds to settle, the loop converges on the one VCO frequency that makes its divided-down output exactly match the reference — the condition called **lock**.

**The lock condition, and why it matters.** At lock, the two signals meeting at the phase detector are equal in frequency: $f_{ref} = f_{VCO} / N$. Rearranged, that is the single formula this whole circuit exists to deliver: $f_{VCO} = N \cdot f_{ref}$. In plain prose, restating the core mechanism precisely: **a VCO generates the RF; a programmable ÷N divider (microprocessor-controlled) divides it down; a phase detector compares against a stable reference crystal and steers the VCO until locked.** The payoff is that a *single* crystal reference, unchanged and unswapped, can now produce any of thousands of precisely known output frequencies simply by changing the integer $N$ loaded into the divider — no new crystal, no re-tuning of an LC tank by hand, just a different number written into a register. The divider's step size — the smallest frequency change available, and therefore the channel spacing the synthesizer can offer — equals the reference frequency actually presented to the phase detector, $f_{ref}$; a synthesizer chip commonly derives that comparison frequency itself by dividing a higher-frequency master crystal down to a convenient step, such as 5 kHz or 10 kHz for VHF FM channels, or a finer step for HF work.

**What the microprocessor actually does.** None of this yet explains where "memory channels" and keypad frequency entry come from — that is the microprocessor's job, and it sits entirely outside the PLL loop itself, simply computing which integer $N$ to write into the divider's register and when. When an operator keys in a frequency on a keypad, or recalls a stored memory channel, the microprocessor's firmware performs the arithmetic $N = f_{VCO} / f_{ref}$, checks that the result is a valid integer the divider can accept, and loads it. A memory channel, in this scheme, is nothing more exotic than a stored value of $N$ (plus a few flags — mode, offset, CTCSS tone) sitting in a few bytes of low-power CMOS RAM or, in later designs, nonvolatile EEPROM; recalling channel 12 means reading those stored bytes and writing the same $N$ into the same divider register a keypad entry would have used. Scanning a band for activity becomes a simple software loop: step $N$ up by one reference step at a time, pause briefly to check for a received signal, and continue. None of this required any change to the analog VCO, phase detector, or loop filter hardware at all — it is entirely a matter of what number the microprocessor decides to write, which is exactly why this architecture, once built, could support features (memory scan, priority-channel watch, repeater auto-offset) that would have needed entirely new analog hardware in a crystal-bank or plain-VFO design.

**Trade-offs: lock time versus purity.** The loop filter's bandwidth is not a free parameter; it is the single knob that trades two things a synthesizer designer wants simultaneously and cannot fully have together. A wide loop bandwidth lets the VCO snap to a new frequency quickly after $N$ changes — useful for fast scanning or quick QSY — but it also lets more of the phase detector's own switching noise and any residual reference-frequency artifacts leak through to modulate the VCO, showing up on the air as phase noise and discrete spurious sidebands spaced at multiples of the comparison frequency around the desired signal. A narrow loop bandwidth suppresses that noise far more effectively, producing a cleaner, more spectrally pure signal, at the cost of taking noticeably longer — tens of milliseconds rather than a handful — to settle onto a new frequency after every retune. Every synthesized transceiver design of this era, from a $150 handheld to a top-of-line HF flagship, embodies a specific answer to that trade-off, and it is one of the reasons two radios built around superficially similar PLL chips can sound audibly different next to each other on a spectrum analyzer. A handheld built for fast repeater scanning might accept a somewhat noisier signal in exchange for snappy channel-to-channel response; a flagship HF transceiver, whose owner cares far more about a clean, spur-free signal for weak-signal DX work than about retuning speed, generally makes the opposite choice — one more example of a decision that used to live entirely in the physical layout of an LC tank now living instead in a designer's choice of a single resistor-capacitor time constant inside a feedback loop.

> **Worked example — programming a synthesized 2-meter channel.** A synthesized handheld of this era derives its reference from a 10.000 MHz crystal, internally divided down to a 5 kHz comparison frequency at the phase detector — so $f_{ref} = 5\ \text{kHz}$ is the radio's channel step. The operator keys in a popular repeater's output frequency, 146.940 MHz, and the microprocessor computes $N = f_{VCO} / f_{ref} = 146.940\ \text{MHz} / 0.005\ \text{MHz} = 29{,}388$. That integer, loaded into the programmable divider, locks the VCO to exactly 146.940000 MHz. Pressing the handheld's "reverse" or "offset" button, the same firmware recomputes the transmit frequency using the standard 2-meter $\pm 600\ \text{kHz}$ shift established in Chapter 6: $146.940\ \text{MHz} - 0.600\ \text{MHz} = 146.340\ \text{MHz}$, giving $N = 146.340 / 0.005 = 29{,}268$. Two divider values, computed in microseconds and stored in two bytes of memory, replace what a crystal-bank radio would have needed two individually purchased and cut crystals to accomplish — and unlike the crystals, either value can be recomputed and reused for any other repeater in range without a trip to a crystal supplier.

**A different problem: getting bits there intact.** Frequency synthesis solves the problem of *finding* a channel. It says nothing about what happens once two stations are on that channel trying to exchange information reliably — and that is precisely the problem RTTY had never solved, that AMTOR partially solved, and that packet radio's AX.25 protocol solved comprehensively, at the cost of considerably more complexity than either of its predecessors.

**AMTOR's answer: a constant-ratio code.** AMTOR starts from Baudot's familiar 5-bit alphabet but re-encodes each of its 32 possible characters into a 7-bit code word chosen so that every valid transmitted character contains exactly four "mark" (1) bits and three "space" (0) bits — a **4-of-7 constant-ratio code**. There are $\binom{7}{4} = 35$ possible 7-bit patterns with exactly four ones, comfortably more than the 32 characters Baudot needs to represent, so every legal AMTOR character maps to one of those 35 patterns and none of the other 93 seven-bit combinations. The payoff is a simple, purely local error check: if a receiving terminal unit ever sees a 7-bit word that does *not* contain exactly four mark bits, it knows immediately, without reference to anything else, that the character has been corrupted in transit. This is error *detection*, not error *correction* — a bad character is flagged, or simply dropped, but the code carries no information about which bit is wrong or what the original character actually was. AMTOR uses that detection capability two different ways. In **ARQ mode** (AMTOR's Mode A), two correspondents — and only two, since the protocol has no addressing scheme for more — exchange short blocks of characters on a fixed timing cycle: one station sends a block and pauses to listen; the other returns a brief control signal either confirming clean copy or silently prompting a repeat of the same block, and the sending station resends until an acknowledgment arrives. In **FEC mode** (Mode B), used for one-to-many bulletins and nets where there is no return channel to request a repeat, each character is simply transmitted twice, separated by a short time gap, so that a brief fade or noise burst affecting one of the two copies is unlikely to affect both — any station copying the broadcast keeps whichever of the two copies passes the 4-of-7 check. Compared to what follows, AMTOR is a modest, single-purpose fix: it catches errors within one character, but it has no concept of a multi-character frame, no address field beyond the fixed pair of correspondents in ARQ mode, and no way to relay a message through an intermediate station at all.

**Borrowing from X.25: AX.25's framing.** AX.25 solves a considerably larger problem than AMTOR does, and it does so by importing, nearly wholesale, the link-layer conventions of a public commercial data network standard rather than inventing something amateur-specific from scratch. Where AMTOR checks one character at a time with no concept of addressing, AX.25 organizes data into discrete, individually addressed, individually checksummed **frames** — the packet-switching mindset TAPR imported from ARPANET-era computer networking and from the ITU-T's X.25 standard for public data networks. Each frame follows an HDLC-style structure: a flag, address fields, a control field, a protocol-ID field, an information field, and a frame check sequence, bracketed by opening and closing flags.

{{fig:ax25-frame}}

Reading the frame field by field: it opens with a **flag** byte, the fixed bit pattern 01111110 (hexadecimal 0x7E), marking the start of the frame. Because that exact bit pattern could otherwise occur naturally inside the data being carried and be mistaken for a frame boundary, HDLC framing uses **bit-stuffing**: the transmitting station inserts an extra 0 bit after any run of five consecutive 1 bits anywhere inside the frame (outside the flags themselves), and the receiving station removes it, so the flag pattern can never appear anywhere except where it is meant to. After the opening flag come the **address fields** — a **destination address** and a **source address**, each occupying seven bytes: six bytes for the station's callsign (padded with spaces if shorter than six characters) and a seventh byte carrying the **SSID**, a value from 0 to 15 that lets one licensed callsign operate several distinct logical stations at once — a home station, a digipeater, a mobile rig, and a packet BBS could all run under the same call, distinguished only by SSID, the way a single postal address might have several distinct mailboxes hanging off it. Following the two required addresses, the frame can carry up to eight further **digipeater addresses**, each also seven bytes, listing the path the frame is meant to be relayed through hop by hop. After the addresses comes a one- or two-byte **control field**, which identifies the frame's type — an information-bearing I-frame carrying send and receive sequence numbers for the ARQ scheme described below; a supervisory S-frame (used for acknowledgments, flow control, or rejection, but no user data); or an unnumbered U-frame, used for connectionless traffic and for setting up or tearing down a connection. A one-byte **PID** (protocol identifier) field follows, indicating what kind of data the information field contains (a value of 0xF0 flags "no layer-3 protocol," the common case for a plain text packet conversation). The **information field** itself carries the actual payload — up to 256 bytes in the AX.25 v2.0 specification — followed by a 16-bit **frame check sequence (FCS)**, and a closing flag identical to the opening one.

**Catching errors: the FCS.** The frame check sequence is computed with a **16-bit CRC-CCITT** — a cyclic redundancy check using the generator polynomial $x^{16} + x^{12} + x^5 + 1$ — run across every byte of the frame from the first address byte through the end of the information field. The transmitting station computes this checksum and appends it as the FCS; the receiving station performs the identical calculation on the frame it actually received and compares its result against the FCS that arrived with it. Unlike AMTOR's 4-of-7 code, which can only catch an error inside a single 7-bit character, a 16-bit CRC over an entire frame is overwhelmingly likely to catch *any* combination of bit errors in that frame — multiple corrupted bits, bits flipped in the address field, anything — because the odds of a random corruption accidentally producing a different but still-valid-looking checksum are on the order of 1 in 65,536. If the two checksums disagree, the receiving TNC simply discards the entire frame without acknowledging it, and says nothing further — it relies on the sender's own timeout, not on any explicit "I got garbage" signal, to trigger a retransmission.

**Recovering from errors: ARQ.** That reliance on silence is deliberate, and it is the mechanism that gives AX.25's **connected mode** its name: **ARQ**, Automatic Repeat reQuest. Each I-frame a connected station sends carries a sequence number, N(S); the station on the other end tracks which sequence numbers it has successfully received and periodically returns supervisory frames — RR (Receive Ready) acknowledging everything up through a given sequence number, or REJ (Reject) explicitly asking for retransmission starting from a specific frame — carrying its own N(R) field. A sending station keeps a copy of every I-frame it has sent but not yet had acknowledged, running a retransmission timer (conventionally called T1) for each; if that timer expires with no RR advancing past the frame's sequence number, the station retransmits it, up to a configured retry limit (N2) before giving up and declaring the link down. Because sequence numbers are tracked with a sliding window rather than one frame at a time, several I-frames can be outstanding — sent but not yet acknowledged — simultaneously, letting a connected packet link keep data moving continuously rather than stopping to wait for an acknowledgment after every single frame. The overall effect is a connection that behaves, from an application's point of view, almost like a reliable wire: characters typed at one end arrive at the other in the order they were sent, with no silent corruption, even though the actual radio path underneath might have dropped, garbled, or delayed any individual frame along the way.

**Getting there in hops: digipeating.** A **digipeater** extends this scheme across distance the same way a repeater extends a voice conversation, but the mechanism is entirely different in kind. A voice repeater listens and retransmits *continuously and simultaneously* on two separate frequencies for as long as anyone is talking; a digipeater listens on a *single* shared frequency, and when it hears a frame whose path field lists its own callsign as the next unused hop, it retransmits that exact frame — flags, addresses, control field, checksum and all — marking itself as "already repeated" in the address field so the same digipeater does not retransmit the same frame twice. A frame addressed with a path of several digipeater calls in sequence gets relayed hop by hop, each station along the chain repeating it once, until it either reaches its destination or exhausts the listed path.

**The physical layer: Bell 202 AFSK.** Everything described so far — framing, addressing, the FCS, ARQ — describes how bits are organized. None of it says how those bits actually ride on a radio wave, and the answer, for VHF packet, is deliberately unglamorous: the TNC does not touch the transceiver's RF stages at all. Instead, it treats the radio exactly as if it were a voice signal, using an ordinary FM transceiver's microphone input on transmit and its speaker or discriminator audio output on receive, and does all of its own modulation and demodulation in the audio domain, using the AFSK (audio frequency-shift keying) scheme standardized decades earlier for telephone-line modems as **Bell 202**.

{{fig:afsk}}

Bell 202 represents each data bit as one of two audio tones: a **1200 Hz "mark"** tone for a binary 1, and a **2200 Hz "space"** tone for a binary 0, switching continuously between the two as the bit stream demands. Because each symbol carries exactly one bit (this is plain two-tone FSK, not a multi-level scheme), a **1200-baud** symbol rate delivers exactly 1200 bits per second of raw data throughput. On receive, the TNC's modem does the reverse: it listens to the transceiver's demodulated audio output, decides at each symbol interval whether it is hearing mark or space, and reconstructs the original bit stream, which the TNC's controller logic then reassembles into AX.25 frames and checks against their FCS. Because the whole scheme lives entirely in the audio passband, it needs no special-purpose RF hardware at all — any ordinary 2-meter FM transceiver, exactly as sold for voice use, works as a packet radio the moment its mic and speaker jacks are wired to a TNC.

HF digital traffic — RTTY, AMTOR, and the earliest experiments in HF packet — used a slower, older AFSK standard instead: **Bell 103**, running at roughly 300 baud rather than 1200. The difference is not arbitrary conservatism; it reflects the physical channel. A VHF FM simplex or repeater path is, propagation-wise, close to a clean, essentially line-of-sight channel with comparatively little multipath distortion, so 1200 baud's faster, tighter tone transitions arrive largely intact. An HF ionospheric path, by contrast, routinely suffers multipath (several sky-wave hops of slightly different length arriving at slightly different times) and fading deep enough to blank a tone entirely for tens of milliseconds — conditions that smear and corrupt fast tone transitions far more readily than slow ones. Running HF digital traffic at a quarter of VHF packet's symbol rate was a direct, practical concession to that physical difference, trading throughput for a signal each symbol could actually survive the ionosphere to deliver.

> **Worked example — how long a packet actually takes.** Consider a maximum-size AX.25 UI frame carrying a full 256-byte information field over 1200-baud VHF AFSK. Tallying the frame's fields in bytes: flag (1) + destination address (7) + source address (7) + control (1) + PID (1) + information (256) + FCS (2) + closing flag (1) = 276 bytes, or $276 \times 8 = 2208$ bits. At 1200 bits per second, transmitting the whole frame takes $2208 / 1200 \approx 1.84\ \text{seconds}$. That number is the concrete payoff of the packet-switching mindset: a conversation that would tie up a voice repeater or a continuous RTTY circuit for as long as anyone kept transmitting instead occupies the channel in well under two seconds per burst, leaving it free the rest of the time for digipeater relays, other stations' packets, or simply silence — bandwidth shared in bursts rather than claimed continuously for the length of a whole conversation.

Stepping back from the field-by-field detail, what AX.25 actually represents is a layered way of thinking about a radio link that none of this book's previous six chapters needed. Bell 202 AFSK is a **physical layer**: how a bit becomes a tone. AX.25's framing, addressing, FCS, and ARQ scheme form a **link layer**: how bits become a frame that can be trusted to arrive correctly between two directly-connected stations. Digipeating, and later NET/ROM's automatic route computation, gesture toward a **network layer**: how a frame finds its way across more than one hop without a human operator manually managing every link along the route. Nobody building a TNC in 1983 necessarily called these things "layers" in the formal sense a computer-science textbook would use a few years later, but the separation of concerns is exactly the same one, arrived at independently by hams solving the same problem the wider computing world was solving at the same time: how do you move information reliably across an unreliable channel, automatically, without a human in the loop for every hop.

### The State of the Art in 1977, 1981, and 1983

The three Handbook editions this chapter draws on — 1977, 1981, and 1983 — sit close enough together in time, six years total, that reading them in sequence feels less like watching an era unfold than watching a single machine being disassembled and rebuilt in front of you, one system at a time.

The 1977 edition still describes a hobby whose control layer was overwhelmingly analog. Its "digital" content leaned toward TTL and CMOS logic gates, digital frequency counters, and the arithmetic of binary and BCD encoding — genuinely useful building blocks, and a sign that digital electronics had already earned a permanent place in the amateur curriculum, but not yet organized around a microprocessor doing general-purpose control work inside the transceiver itself. A ham building from 1977-vintage plans still expected to select a transmitting frequency either by inserting the correct crystal or by hand-tuning an analog VFO, checking the result against a separate digital frequency counter bolted on for a precise numeric readout — a halfway step, giving the operator confidence in exactly what frequency the dial was sitting on without yet solving the underlying problems of drift, of repeatability, or of storing more than one frequency at a time.

By 1981, the picture had shifted enough that microprocessor-based construction projects and PLL frequency synthesis had become an established, if still relatively advanced, part of the book's content rather than a curiosity. Home computers — the same Apple II- and TRS-80-class machines reaching consumer shelves in these very years — had begun appearing in the amateur press as legitimate station accessories: logging aids, code-practice tools, and, increasingly, as terminals for the digital modes just then reaching the amateur bands. AMTOR was on the air under its FCC experimental authority; RTTY remained the dominant teleprinter mode by sheer weight of numbers, its lack of any error checking notwithstanding. Packet radio, in 1981, was still a plan rather than a product — TAPR itself had only just been founded that October, and a ham reading that year's Handbook would have found no ready-made packet hardware to buy at any price.

The 1983 edition catches the moment that changes. TAPR's TNC-1 kits reached the market in November of that same publication year, meaning the ham holding the freshly printed 1983 Handbook was, quite literally, reading about a capability that had either just become available or was about to. A well-equipped station built from this edition's pages might combine a fully synthesized, microprocessor-controlled HF transceiver — general-coverage receive, a split-frequency VFO pair, dozens of memory channels holding a whole season's worth of favorite frequencies — with a synthesized, keypad-tunable 2-meter FM handheld or mobile rig sitting alongside it; and, on the operating desk, a home computer increasingly displacing the mechanical RTTY teleprinter as the shack's text-handling device, its serial port already anticipating the TNC that would soon plug into it. Thirty meters — the first WARC-79 band to reach American hams, restricted to CW and digital modes only — sat open and nearly empty on the dial, a brand-new sliver of HF spectrum arriving at almost exactly the moment RTTY, AMTOR, and the first trickle of HF packet experiments needed somewhere quiet to operate. A ham who had built a station from the 1977 edition and kept building from each successive one had, in six years and without a single change to the laws of propagation, watched frequency control move from a socket and a dial into a keypad and a microprocessor's memory, and watched "getting a message through" grow from a single continuous tone a trained ear had to copy by hand into a checksummed, acknowledged, automatically relayed packet a machine could verify without any human listening at all.

***

Every one of the changes this chapter has described happened in the transceiver's *control* layer. The microprocessor picks the frequency by computing a divider value; it assembles, checksums, and relays data by running AX.25's framing and ARQ logic; it remembers a hundred channels in a way no crystal bank ever could. But none of that touches the signal itself once it leaves the synthesizer and enters the transmitter's mixer stage, or arrives at the receiver's front end on its way to the detector. The mixer that up-converts a packet TNC's audio tones onto a carrier is still the same kind of diode or transistor mixer this book met in Chapter 2; the IF filter that gives an AMTOR receiver its selectivity is still a crystal or mechanical filter built the way Chapter 4 described it; the discriminator that turns a Bell 202 tone pair back into audio for a TNC to decode is still the same FM discriminator, unchanged in principle, that Chapter 6 explained for voice repeaters. A microprocessor now decides *which* frequency that whole analog chain operates on, and *what* to do with the bits that come out the other end — but the chain itself, mixer to filter to detector, is still built exactly the way it would have been built in 1965, one discrete analog stage bolted to the next. Somewhere close by, on a laboratory bench rather than an operating desk, the question that would define the next fifteen years of amateur radio was already starting to be asked: if a microprocessor can decide which frequency a synthesizer produces and what a string of received bits means, is there any real reason the mixing, filtering, and detecting themselves still have to happen in analog hardware at all — or could all of it, eventually, become just another number a fast enough processor could compute?
