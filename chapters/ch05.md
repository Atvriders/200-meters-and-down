## 5. The Postwar Reinvention (1945–1960)

*"By April 1953, at least some 300 amateur stations in the United States were operating single sideband."*
*— per ARRL figures, April 1953*

### The Story

When the war ended in 1945 and amateurs were let back onto the air, the hobby that returned was not the hobby that had left. Tens of thousands of the new hams flooding into the bands had just spent several years as radio operators, radar technicians, and aircraft radiomen — trained by the military itself in theory, procedure, and Morse, and mustered out with no station of their own but with every reason to want one. What they found waiting for them was the largest, cheapest supply of surplus radio equipment in history, a federal license structure about to be rebuilt from the ground up, a brand-new solid-state device nobody yet knew how to use, and a fight over how to talk that would occupy *QST*'s letters column for the better part of a decade. None of it happened all at once, and none of it happened in isolation — the surplus glut made building a station cheap just as a new license class made getting one easy, and both landed on a hobby about to argue, loudly and at length, over what that station should actually sound like on the air.

The surplus flood came first, and it came fast. The government that had spent 1942 through 1945 buying hundreds of thousands of Signal Corps and Navy radio sets by the boxcar now had no use for most of them, and the War Assets Administration existed for exactly one purpose: get military property off the books, at any price the market would bear. For a receiver-hungry ham, that price was often a few dollars. The BC-348, a rugged low-frequency/high-frequency liaison receiver built for bombers, came out of the surplus pipeline by the tens of thousands and became a fixture on postwar amateur benches — its 915 kHz IF strip, standard superheterodyne architecture, and solid mechanical construction needed only a BFO for CW and a little recalibration to become a perfectly serviceable amateur-band receiver. The ARC-5 "Command Set" family — compact transmitter-receiver pairs built for aircraft — arrived in even greater numbers and at even lower prices, and hams stripped, retuned, and rebuilt them into low-power exciters, VFOs, and receivers by the thousand. The BC-221 frequency meter, a heterodyne frequency standard accurate enough for military calibration work, became the surplus item every serious builder wanted on the bench, because it let a ham verify a crystal or a VFO's actual output frequency to a precision no amateur-grade gear had offered before. Mail-order surplus houses — Fair Radio Sales in Lima, Ohio, foremost among them — turned this glut into a mail-order catalog culture, and a newly minted ham with no money and a lot of ambition could put a station together from parts that had, a few years earlier, been classified war matériel.

Not all of it was HF gear, either. The SCR-522, a VHF aircraft command set built for fighter and bomber control work up around 100–156 MHz, showed up on surplus tables by the thousand and gave hams their first real mass-market foothold on 2 meters; converted SCR-522 sets, alongside ex-military APR-4 search receivers repurposed as crude panoramic displays, seeded a base of VHF activity that the next era's repeater boom would later build on. Hamfests — swap meets that had existed in smaller form before the war — grew into the era's real distribution network for all of it: folding tables of BC-348 chassis, ARC-5 command sets, and unlabeled tubes pulled from a wooden crate, priced by the pound as often as by the part number, at every regional gathering from spring through fall.

None of this required inventing anything new. The superheterodyne receiver, crystal control, class-C amplification, pi-network output coupling — all of it had matured in the 1930s and been hardened by wartime production discipline, exactly the technology Chapters 3 and 4 traced into being. What surplus offered was volume: a generation of new hams could own gear that would have been unaffordable, or simply unbuildable at home, a decade earlier. The postwar reinvention began as an economic event before it became a technical one — and the technical event, when it came, would not be a new circuit topology so much as a new argument about which sidebands were worth keeping.

Public service tradition came back with the equipment. The ARRL's National Traffic System, formalized in the years right after the war to move message traffic efficiently from local nets up through section and region nets to a national level and back down again, gave the newly surplus-equipped amateur population a structured, disciplined outlet beyond casual ragchewing — and the Amateur Radio Emergency Service, dormant like everything else for the war's duration, resumed organizing hams by locality to support disaster and emergency communications the moment peacetime restored the privilege to do so. A QSL card confirming a contact, a Worked-All-States certificate on the wall, a traffic net check-in on a weeknight: the social infrastructure of the hobby came back online just as fast as the technical infrastructure did.

The regulatory reinvention followed close behind. On 1 July 1951 the FCC replaced the old amateur license structure — Class A, B, and C, dating to the 1930s — with something considerably more elaborate.

**FACT:** FCC license restructuring takes effect: six classes — Novice, Technician, Conditional, General, Advanced, Amateur Extra.

The old Class B became General; the old Class C, reachable by mail-in examination for hams too far from an FCC field office to sit for a full exam, became Conditional; the old Class A became Advanced. Two classes were entirely new. Amateur Extra sat at the top, requiring code proficiency and years already spent holding a lower license — a class built to have somewhere higher to climb, though for the moment it carried no additional operating privileges beyond prestige and a shorter, more desirable callsign format. Novice sat at the bottom: a one-year, non-renewable license, issued on a simpler written exam and a 5-word-per-minute code test, restricted to low power, and — by rule — restricted to crystal control. A Novice's transmitter had to sit on a fixed, crystal-ground frequency; no VFO, no drifting around the band while hunting for a clear spot. It was a return, for beginners, to a constraint the rest of the hobby had left behind in the 1920s, and it was deliberate: a crystal-controlled Novice signal was a signal that stayed exactly where the operator put it, easy for both examiners and fellow hams to identify by its distinctive "WN" prefix and impossible to accidentally wander outside the band edge. Within a few years of the class's creation, that two-by-three "WN" prefix was a common sight in established hams' logbooks, evidence the class was doing exactly the recruiting job the FCC and ARRL had designed it to do. Technician, the other new class, was built for a different kind of newcomer — one more interested in the experimental VHF and UHF territory above the traditional HF bands than in working DX on 20 meters. The whole six-class structure was meant to reward technical progression with expanded privilege. It did not entirely survive contact with the membership: in early 1953 the FCC restored full HF operating privileges to General, Conditional, Advanced, and Extra licensees alike, quietly walking back part of the stratification the 1951 order had introduced. It would not be the last time the FCC tried to trade privilege for technical achievement and found the amateur community pushing back — a fight that would return, considerably angrier, in 1968.

While the FCC was redrawing the license chart, a five-man team at Bell Telephone Laboratories in Murray Hill, New Jersey was quietly redrawing the future of every circuit in the book.

**FACT:** First working point-contact transistor at Bell Telephone Laboratories (Bardeen, Brattain, under Shockley); Nobel Prize 1956.

The device itself was almost absurdly modest to look at — a sliver of germanium with two gold-foil contacts pressed into its surface a few thousandths of an inch apart — but it did something no vacuum tube could: it amplified without a heated filament, without hundreds of volts of plate supply, and without the fragility of blown glass. Amateurs would not get their hands on usable transistors for several years yet, and when they did, the devices were expensive, inconsistent from unit to unit, and useless for the kind of RF power a transmitter's final amplifier needed to put out — a single germanium junction transistor, once it finally started turning up in mail-order catalogs later in the decade, could cost several times what a comparable receiving tube did, for a part that could not yet do a tube's whole job. But the seed was planted in December 1947, and everything solid-state that amateur radio would become over the following three decades traces back to that germanium sliver on a lab bench. It took most of a decade for that seed to reach the general public in any visible form at all — the first commercial transistor radio, a pocket-sized broadcast-band receiver marketed as the Regency TR-1, did not reach store shelves until the mid-1950s — but once it did, the demonstration effect on hams was immediate and obvious: if a whole broadcast receiver could shrink to fit a shirt pocket and run for weeks on a small battery, there was no reason a code-practice oscillator, an audio stage, or eventually a whole low-power VHF rig could not do the same.

The technical story that actually dominated the era, though, was not the transistor — it was sideband. In June 1948, an engineer at General Electric named Don Norgaard, W2KUJ, published an article in *QST* that would reorganize how hams thought about getting a signal from one station to another.

**FACT:** Don Norgaard (W2KUJ) publishes "A New Approach to Single Sideband" in *QST*, popularizing the phasing method for amateurs.

The phasing method of generating single sideband was not new — Ralph Hartley of Western Electric had patented it back in 1928 — but it had sat, mostly, as a laboratory curiosity and a telephone-company long-haul-carrier technique, far too finicky and expensive for the average ham's basement. Norgaard's contribution was practical, not theoretical: he showed hams how to build a phasing-type sideband exciter from parts they could actually get and afford. The article landed in a hobby that had spent twenty-five years perfecting amplitude modulation and saw no obvious reason to abandon it, and the argument that followed became one of the defining cultural fights of the decade.

*QST* gave the fight a stage. From July 1948 through March 1954, Byron Goodman, W1DX, ran a monthly column titled "On the Air with Single Sideband" — nearly six years of sustained editorial attention to a mode that, at the column's start, almost nobody was using. ARRL's own institutional instincts through the early 1950s ran cautious, more comfortable with AM's established practice than with the sideband insurgency Norgaard's article had set loose, and *QST*'s editorial tone through the "sideband wars" years often read as refereeing a fight it had not entirely wanted to host. But the League published Goodman's column every month regardless, gave both camps space to make their case in print, and by doing so did as much as anyone to turn SSB from a curiosity into a movement. AM diehards complained, not without cause, that early phasing rigs sounded distorted, that SSB was maddening to tune (a badly netted SSB signal came out sounding like a cartoon duck, since every recovered audio frequency shifted by however far the receiver's tuning was off), and that the whole enterprise demanded a level of receiver stability and operator skill that plain AM never had. SSB converts answered with signal reports: watt for watt, a sideband signal was simply easier to copy at distance, and easier to pack into a crowded band. There was real substance behind the AM camp's loudest specific complaint, too — "splatter," the wideband interference an overdriven or poorly neutralized early phasing exciter could spray several kilohertz either side of its intended channel, was a genuine, measurable problem on crowded band segments before ALC (automatic level control) and cleaner exciter designs tamed it later in the decade, and it gave AM operators a legitimate grievance to point to that was not simply nostalgia for the mode they already knew. By April 1950, *QST* reported that phasing-method builders outnumbered filter-method builders by roughly two to one — not because phasing was technically superior (it was not; its sideband suppression was markedly worse), but because a sharp crystal or mechanical filter cost real money and a set of matched phase-shift networks did not. In November 1950, Norgaard's own design reached the market as a commercial kit, the "SSB, Jr." Momentum kept building through the early 1950s; by April 1953, per ARRL's own count — the figure that opens this chapter — some 300 amateur stations nationwide were on the air with sideband. It was still a minority mode. It would not stay one.

The filter camp got its own popularizer that same year. From June through August 1953, *QST* ran a three-part series by Walter Van B. Roberts, W2CHO, on magnetostriction devices and mechanical filters — the technique Collins Radio had pioneered for its own commercial and amateur gear, using a filter built from mechanically resonant metal elements rather than a bank of separately ground quartz crystals. As parts costs fell through the 1950s and mechanical filters became something a well-equipped ham could actually buy rather than machine himself, the filter method's clean 40-to-60-decibel sideband suppression — utterly independent of how carefully anyone had trimmed an audio phase-shift network — began winning converts back from phasing. By the middle of the decade, Collins had built a complete SSB-first product line around the technique: the 75A-4 receiver and KWS-1 transmitter, released in 1955, engineered from the ground up around sideband rather than treating it as an afterthought bolted onto an AM rig. Collins' established rivals in the commercial-receiver business — Hallicrafters, National, Hammarlund — had spent two decades competing on AM and CW sensitivity and selectivity alone, and none of them could afford to watch Collins own the sideband conversation uncontested; through the second half of the decade each began fielding its own SSB adapters, and eventually its own SSB-capable receivers and exciters, turning what had started as a magazine argument between amateur builders into a genuine commercial product race.

Then, in 1957, Collins closed the loop entirely.

**FACT:** Collins ships the KWM-1, the first purpose-built amateur SSB/CW transceiver (~100 W PEP, 14–30 MHz); USAF Strategic Air Command adopts SSB for B-52 HF.

Every rig described so far in this chapter — surplus command sets, phasing exciters, the 75A-4/KWS-1 pair — was still two boxes: a receiver with its own tuning dial and a transmitter with its own, "netted" to the receiver's frequency by ear before every contact, a fussy ritual that mattered enormously once SSB made pitch and intelligibility hostage to precise frequency matching. The KWM-1 did away with the ritual by doing away with the second dial: transmitter and receiver shared a single frequency-determining oscillator, so wherever you tuned to listen was, automatically, exactly where you'd transmit. That the US Air Force's Strategic Air Command adopted SSB the same year for B-52 long-range HF communications was not a coincidence of timing so much as a confirmation from the most demanding possible customer: for getting intelligible voice across thousands of miles of HF skip on a fixed power budget, sideband had no serious rival left. The KWM-1 also popularized VOX — voice-operated switching that sensed the operator's own speech on the microphone and keyed the transmitter automatically, with no separate push-to-talk button or footswitch required — a small operational convenience that, paired with a shared-oscillator transceiver, made HF sideband conversation feel, for the first time, almost as natural as talking on a telephone.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** America's postwar reinvention was economic and regulatory — a surplus glut, a license reshuffle. Elsewhere, amateur radio had to be *relegalized* before any of that could even start. Amateur transmitting in the defeated Axis nations had been banned outright by the occupying powers, and getting it back took years, not months: West Germany's hams did not have a national organization of their own again until the Deutscher Amateur-Radio-Club formed at the end of 1950, and full civilian licensing in Japan, banned since the war by the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers, did not resume until 1952, alongside the end of the Occupation itself. Britain fared better — amateur transmitting, suspended for the war's duration, was restored in 1946 — but even there the hobby had to be rebuilt station by station after six dark years. Through all of it, the International Amateur Radio Union that Chapter 2 traced back to that 1925 Paris congress kept doing the quiet coordinating work international amateur radio had always needed — pressing case by case, nation by nation, for the licenses and the internationally agreed band allocations that let a rebuilt German or Japanese station legally work an American one again once its home government finally allowed it back on the air at all. The same postwar decade that gave American hams a flood of cheap surplus gear gave hams elsewhere something more basic: their license back.

### How It Works

Amplitude modulation, as Chapter 3 explained, works by varying a carrier's amplitude in step with an audio waveform. That carrier, plus the two sidebands it generates, is what a transmitter radiates and what a receiver's envelope detector needs, in full, to recover the original audio. Single sideband starts from the same modulation product but keeps only a fraction of it — and understanding exactly which fraction, and why, is the technical heart of the postwar reinvention.

**The balanced modulator and double-sideband suppressed carrier.** Every SSB signal, whichever method generates it, begins life as a balanced modulator: a circuit — commonly a ring of diodes or a push-pull pair of tubes or transistors — driven simultaneously by the RF carrier and the audio signal, arranged so that its output contains the *product* of the two rather than either one alone. For a single audio tone at frequency $f_m$ and a carrier at $f_c$, that product works out, by an ordinary trigonometric identity, to:

$\cos(2\pi f_c t)\cdot\cos(2\pi f_m t) = \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi (f_c - f_m) t) + \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi (f_c + f_m) t)$

Two new frequencies appear on the right-hand side — the lower sideband at $f_{LSB} = f_c - f_m$ and the upper sideband at $f_{USB} = f_c + f_m$ — and neither one is the carrier itself. That is the defining property of a *balanced* modulator: its symmetry cancels the carrier term out of the output entirely, leaving double-sideband suppressed-carrier (DSBSC) signal — both sidebands, full information, no carrier. A DSBSC signal is not yet SSB. It is the shared starting point that both the filter method and the phasing method take and reduce, by different means, down to one sideband alone.

Physically, the simplest balanced modulator is a ring of four diodes, driven at its center by the carrier and fed the audio signal across the ring itself. The carrier's own alternating polarity steers the audio through the ring first one way, then the other, chopping it at the carrier's own rate; because the carrier drives the ring in push-pull, symmetrically, any carrier energy that leaks straight through cancels against its own mirror image at the output, while the chopped audio — now shifted up into sum-and-difference sideband energy around the carrier — does not cancel at all. Match the diodes and balance the transformer windings closely enough, and 40 dB or more of carrier suppression is achievable with four diodes and two small transformers, no tube, crystal, or exotic part required — which is a large part of why the balanced modulator, unlike the filter that follows it, was never itself a point of contention between the phasing and filter camps. In practice no balanced modulator is perfectly balanced; some trace of carrier always leaks through a real diode ring, from component mismatches too small to null out entirely. That residual carrier matters more here than it might seem, since it eats directly into the same power budget the whole "SSB is more efficient" argument depends on — a well-aligned modulator held its carrier leakage 40 dB or more below full output, low enough to add negligibly to a signal already dominated by the wanted sideband, while a poorly aligned one gave AM's defenders one more legitimate technical complaint to add to their list.

**The filter method.** The most direct way to throw away one sideband is to filter it out. A balanced modulator generates its DSBSC product at a fixed, relatively low intermediate frequency — commonly somewhere in the range of 455 kHz up to about 9 MHz — chosen specifically because a sharp bandpass filter is easier to build at a lower, fixed frequency than at a variable operating frequency. A crystal-lattice filter, or the Collins-style mechanical (magnetostriction) filter that W2CHO's *QST* series popularized in 1953, passes one sideband essentially unattenuated while rejecting the other by 40 to 60 dB — a suppression figure that holds steady across the whole audio passband, because the filter does not care what audio tone produced a given sideband component, only where in frequency that component landed. After filtering, the surviving single sideband is mixed up to the desired operating frequency and amplified. The cost of this precision was, for a 1950s ham, real money: a well-made crystal-lattice or mechanical filter was a precision mechanical or piezoelectric component, and building or buying one was the single biggest expense in an SSB rig — which is exactly why, as the Story section described, the cheaper phasing method got a two-year head start in amateur adoption.

The two flavors of filter got there by different physical routes. A crystal-lattice filter builds its passband from several quartz crystals, each ground to a very slightly different frequency and wired into a bridge (lattice) arrangement, so that their individually narrow, very-high-Q resonances overlap just enough to form one continuous, sharp-skirted passband rather than several separate notches. The mechanical (magnetostriction) filter Collins favored, and that W2CHO's 1953 series popularized, reached the same electrical result by an entirely mechanical path: a chain of small metal discs or rods, sized and coupled to resonate at the filter's design frequency, with a magnetostrictive transducer converting the incoming electrical signal into physical vibration at one end of the chain and a second transducer converting that vibration back into an electrical signal at the other. A mechanically resonant disc can hold far higher Q than a practical LC circuit at these frequencies, and that higher Q is exactly what buys the few-kilohertz-wide, steep-skirted passband a sideband filter needs — narrow enough to pass one voice sideband cleanly and reject its mirror image by tens of decibels, in a package no equivalent LC filter of reasonable size or cost could match.

**The phasing method.** Rather than filtering after the fact, the phasing method cancels the unwanted sideband algebraically, using a second balanced modulator and a pair of 90°-quadrature networks — one shifting the audio path, one shifting the RF carrier path — so that the two modulators' outputs interfere constructively for one sideband and destructively for the other. Feed the second modulator carrier and audio each shifted a quarter-cycle (90°) from the first modulator's inputs, and its product works out to:

$\sin(2\pi f_c t)\cdot\sin(2\pi f_m t) = \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi (f_c - f_m) t) - \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi (f_c + f_m) t)$

Compare this against the first modulator's output above. Both contain the same lower-sideband term with the same sign; both contain an upper-sideband term, but with *opposite* sign. Add the two modulator outputs together and the upper-sideband terms cancel, leaving only the lower sideband:

$f_{LSB} = f_c - f_m$

Subtract instead of add, and the lower-sideband terms cancel while the upper sideband survives — a simple switch (or a second summing network) is all it takes to choose which sideband a phasing rig transmits. No filter, no precision piezoelectric component: just two balanced modulators, a resistor-capacitor audio phase-shift network, and an RF quadrature splitter, all of it buildable on a 1950s ham's bench for a fraction of a mechanical filter's price. That was Norgaard's whole pitch in June 1948, and it is why the phasing method won the amateur population, if not the engineering argument, in the years right after.

One more thing falls out of either method almost for free: CW. Key the balanced-modulator chain with a steady, unmodulated tone — or, more simply, key the carrier oscillator feeding the modulator directly and let the SSB chain pass it through as a single, unwavering sideband — and the same hardware that generates a voice sideband generates a clean CW signal on the same frequency, with no separate CW oscillator or keying circuit required. That is the quiet engineering reason the era's transceivers, the KWM-1 chief among them, could advertise themselves as "SSB/CW" rather than needing to solve CW generation as a second, unrelated problem.

{{fig:ssb-phasing}}

> **Worked example:** Take a carrier at $f_c = 3.885\ \mathrm{MHz}$ — a plausible spot on the 75-meter phone band — modulated by a single 1 kHz test tone, $f_m = 1\ \mathrm{kHz}$. Summing the two balanced-modulator outputs above leaves only the lower sideband: $f_{LSB} = 3.885\ \mathrm{MHz} - 0.001\ \mathrm{MHz} = 3.884\ \mathrm{MHz}$. Subtracting instead leaves only the upper sideband, $f_{USB} = 3.886\ \mathrm{MHz}$. But real voice audio is not one tone — it is a continuous band running roughly 300 Hz to 3 kHz — and the phasing network has to hold its 90° relationship *flat across that whole range* to cancel the unwanted sideband cleanly at every audio frequency at once. Miss the phase or amplitude balance by even a few degrees or a few percent anywhere in that band, and a smear of unwanted-sideband energy leaks through at that frequency. That is precisely why phasing rigs topped out around 20–30 dB of sideband suppression while a filter — which rejects by frequency alone, with no dependence on which audio tone got you there — reliably held 40–60 dB. The same worked example that shows phasing's appeal (cheap, buildable, no exotic parts) shows its limit in the same breath.

Put in plain power terms, the gap between those two suppression figures is enormous, not incremental. Twenty decibels means $P_2/P_1 = 10^{20/10} = 100$: the unwanted sideband sits at only one one-hundredth the power of the wanted one, which on a quiet band is still audible as a thin, reversed ghost of the wrong voice riding underneath the right one. Sixty decibels means $P_2/P_1 = 10^{60/10} = 10^{6}$ — the unwanted sideband a full million times weaker, for practical purposes simply gone. That million-to-one gap between a mediocre phasing alignment and a well-built filter is the real, measurable stake the "sideband wars" were fought over, however much the argument in *QST*'s letters column was conducted in adjectives rather than decibels.

**Why SSB wins: power and bandwidth.** A conventional AM signal, 100%-modulated by a single audio tone, can be written as

$e(t) = A_c(1 + m\cos(2\pi f_m t))\cos(2\pi f_c t)$

where $m$ is the modulation index. Multiplying this out puts a carrier term of amplitude $A_c$ and two sideband terms, each of amplitude $mA_c/2$, on the air simultaneously. Since power is proportional to amplitude squared, each sideband carries

$P_{sb} = \frac{m^2}{4} P_c$

of power relative to the carrier power $P_c$, and the transmitter's total radiated power is

$P_{total} = P_c\left(1 + \frac{m^2}{2}\right)$

At full, 100% modulation ($m = 1$), that works out to $P_{total} = 1.5\, P_c$ — carrier plus two sidebands, each at a quarter of the carrier's power. Of that total, the carrier alone accounts for $P_c / P_{total} = 1/1.5 \approx 66.7\%$, and each sideband for only $P_{sb}/P_{total} = 0.25/1.5 \approx 16.7\%$.

> **Worked example:** Take a 100-watt AM carrier, 100%-modulated by a steady tone. The math above puts 100 W in the carrier and 25 W in each sideband, for 150 W total radiated. Two-thirds of that 150 W — the 100 W carrier — carries *no* information at all; its only job is to give a simple envelope (diode) detector something to ride against at the receiving end. The two sidebands, 50 W combined, carry the actual voice. An SSB transmitter throws away the carrier and the redundant second sideband entirely and puts its whole output into the one sideband that matters. A 150-watt-capable SSB rig therefore delivers roughly 150 W into that single sideband where the AM rig delivered only 25 W — about six times the information-carrying power for the same total transmitter capability. That arithmetic, more than any single testimonial in *QST*'s letters column, is what the "sideband wars" were actually being fought over.

But "150-watt-capable" raises a question AM never had to ask: 150 watts of *what*, exactly, when there is no steady carrier left to measure? A CW or AM transmitter's output is a continuous, more-or-less constant carrier, and rating its power is straightforward. An SSB transmitter carrying real voice has no carrier at all — its instantaneous output rises and falls with the syllables of speech, and a single audio tone through the whole SSB chain produces nothing more than a single, constant-amplitude RF sine wave, indistinguishable in its steadiness from CW and useless for revealing how an amplifier actually behaves under real, dynamic modulation. The industry's answer, adopted across the whole SSB era, was **PEP** — Peak Envelope Power, the power delivered at the very crest of the modulation envelope — measured with a standard *two-tone test*: feed the transmitter two equal-amplitude audio tones simultaneously, close together in frequency, rather than one.
>
> Each tone alone, run through the same final amplifier, delivers some average power $P_1$. Run together, $v(t) = A\cos(2\pi f_1 t) + A\cos(2\pi f_2 t)$, and because the two tones are uncorrelated, their average powers simply add: $P_{avg} = 2 P_1$. But their instantaneous voltages do not just add on average — at the moments the two sine waves swing into phase together, the envelope peaks at twice the single-tone amplitude, $V_{peak} = 2A$, and since power scales with voltage squared, that peak represents $PEP = k(2A)^2 = 4kA^2 = 4P_1$. Compare the two results: $\mathrm{PEP} = 2 \times P_{avg,\,two\text{-}tone}$ — the peak envelope power a two-tone test reveals is always exactly double what an ordinary average-reading RF wattmeter shows on the same test. That factor of two was not an abstraction to a 1950s builder: it was a calibration procedure, done with an audio two-tone generator and the same average-reading wattmeter already on the bench, that let him verify his SSB rig's true PEP rating without owning any specialized peak-reading instrument at all.

Occupied bandwidth tells the same story from a different angle. Because AM's simple envelope detector needs both sidebands present in their original phase relationship to reconstruct the amplitude envelope correctly, an AM signal must occupy

$BW_{AM} \approx 2 f_m$

— twice the highest audio frequency, roughly 6 kHz of spectrum for 3 kHz voice audio. SSB's product detector (described next) needs only one sideband to recover the same audio, so an SSB signal occupies only

$BW_{SSB} \approx f_m$

— about half the spectrum space for the same voice bandwidth. Twice the stations per kilohertz of band, and roughly six times the useful power per watt of transmitter capability: between them, those two numbers are the entire technical case for SSB, and they are why, by the back half of the 1950s, sideband was well on its way to overtaking AM on the HF phone bands.

{{fig:ssb-vs-am}}

**The product detector.** An AM envelope detector — a diode that simply follows the peak amplitude of the incoming signal — works because AM's information is carried entirely in that envelope, and the carrier is present to give the envelope something to be measured against. Feed an envelope detector a suppressed-carrier SSB signal and it fails outright: with no carrier riding along, there is no stable reference amplitude for a diode to follow, only a single sideband whose amplitude wanders with the voice waveform in a way an envelope detector cannot untangle back into audio. SSB reception instead needs a product detector — in effect, a receive-side balanced modulator run backwards. A local oscillator, the beat-frequency oscillator (BFO), regenerates a substitute for the missing carrier at the receiver, and the incoming IF signal is *multiplied* against it rather than merely rectified. The same trigonometric identity that generated the sidebands in the first place now recovers audio from them:

$\cos(2\pi f_{sig} t)\cdot\cos(2\pi f_{BFO} t) = \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi (f_{BFO}-f_{sig}) t) + \frac{1}{2}\cos(2\pi (f_{BFO}+f_{sig}) t)$

> **Worked example:** Suppose a receiver's IF is $f_{IF} = 455\ \mathrm{kHz}$ and the incoming lower-sideband signal, corresponding to the same 1 kHz test tone from the earlier example, sits at $f_{sig} = 454\ \mathrm{kHz}$. The BFO is set to the suppressed carrier's own frequency, $f_{BFO} = 455\ \mathrm{kHz}$. Mixing the two in the product detector produces a difference term at $f_{BFO} - f_{sig} = 1\ \mathrm{kHz}$ — the original audio tone, recovered exactly — and a sum term at $f_{BFO} + f_{sig} = 909\ \mathrm{kHz}$, far above audio and simply filtered out by the detector's own low-pass output stage. Notice what this depends on: the BFO has to sit within a few tens of hertz of the *exact* right frequency, because every hertz the BFO is off shifts every recovered audio tone by that same amount. Set it 50 Hz high and a 1 kHz tone comes out at 1050 Hz; set it 50 Hz high across an entire voice signal and every formant shifts together, which is exactly the "Donald Duck" mistuning AM listeners loved to complain about. SSB reception demands a level of receiver frequency stability and tuning precision that AM reception never asked for — one more reason the era's better-built rigs, like the Collins 75A-4, commanded a premium. It also demanded that operators know their own frequency more precisely than an AM-era band plan had ever required, since a signal packed into roughly half the spectrum space left correspondingly less margin for error at a band edge — which is exactly why the surplus BC-221 heterodyne frequency meter, already a bench fixture for calibrating crystal-controlled transmitters, became just as essential for the sideband operator checking that his signal, and not merely his dial, actually sat where he thought it did.

SSB reception also demanded a rethink of a receiver stage Chapter 3 had already made standard: automatic gain control. AGC works by feeding a receiver's own detected output back to cut IF gain when a signal is strong, holding the audio level roughly constant across weak and strong stations alike — and an AM-era AGC circuit, built with the steady, ever-present AM carrier in mind, could afford a relatively slow, sluggish response, since the carrier itself never disappeared between syllables. An SSB signal is nothing like that: with the carrier gone, receiver output rises and falls syllable by syllable, right along with the speaker's own voice, and an AGC circuit tuned for AM's steady carrier either overreacts to every consonant or, worse, "thumps" audibly at the start of each transmission before it catches up. The era's better SSB receivers redesigned their AGC time constants specifically for speech — a fast attack to grab the first syllable's peak almost instantly, and a slower decay to hold the gain level through the natural pauses between words and sentences rather than opening back up in the silence and amplifying band noise — a small circuit refinement, but one that separated a receiver merely capable of demodulating SSB from one genuinely comfortable to listen to for an evening's ragchew.

That same demand for precision pushed the era's best SSB receivers toward an architecture Chapter 3's superheterodyne math already explains: dual conversion. Recall the image-frequency problem from that chapter — a superhet's front end also passes a spurious response at $f_{image} = f_{LO} \pm f_{IF}$, on the opposite side of the local oscillator from the wanted signal, and the lower the IF, the closer that image sits to the frequency you actually want to hear. A single-conversion receiver built straight down to a 455 kHz IF — ideal for the sideband filter's own selectivity — puts its image only 910 kHz away, uncomfortably close on a crowded HF band unless the front end's own tuned circuits do a great deal of rejection work before the signal ever reaches the mixer. The era's better sideband receivers split the difference instead: convert first to a considerably higher intermediate frequency, pushing the image response safely far away where simple front-end tuning can reject it with ease, then convert a second time down to the low IF where the mechanical or crystal filter does its narrow, sideband-selecting work. Two mixers and two IFs bought what one mixer and one low IF could not — good image rejection and a sharp sideband filter at the same time — at the cost of one more oscillator to keep stable.

**The transistor and the common-emitter amplifier.** The point-contact transistor Bell Labs demonstrated in December 1947 was fragile and inconsistent, but the junction transistor that followed it in the early-to-mid 1950s was a practical amplifying device, and by the back half of the decade hams were beginning to put it to work — first in audio stages, code-practice oscillators, and battery-powered portable receivers, later in low-power VHF converters. Its basic amplifying stage, the common-emitter configuration, does for a solid-state circuit what a triode's grounded-cathode stage did for a tube circuit: an input signal drives the base-emitter junction, forward-biased at only a few tenths of a volt for the germanium devices of the era (roughly 0.2 V, well below the ~0.6–0.7 V a later silicon device would need), and a much larger current flows in the collector-emitter path as a result.

There is one difference here worth holding onto, because it tripped up more than one tube-trained builder making the switch: a tube's grid is a voltage-controlled input that draws essentially no current at all, while a transistor's base-emitter junction is, physically, a forward-biased diode junction, and it draws real current to do its job. Nothing in a tube circuit's equations looks quite like a base current term — a grid simply sits at a voltage and controls plate current through it, drawing nothing. A transistor's base has to be *fed* current, in a specific, small, well-regulated amount, or the stage will not bias correctly at all. That relationship is captured by the transistor's current gain, $\beta$ (also written $h_{FE}$):

$I_C = \beta I_B$

— a small base current controlling a collector current tens of times larger. Route that collector current through a load resistor $R_C$ and the varying voltage dropped across it becomes the amplified output, inverted in phase from the input, exactly as a tube's plate signal inverts relative to its grid.

{{fig:ce-amp}}

> **Worked example:** Take a germanium junction transistor with $\beta = 50$, biased by its base network to draw a steady $I_B = 20\ \mu\mathrm{A}$. Collector current follows directly: $I_C = 50 \times 20\ \mu\mathrm{A} = 1\ \mathrm{mA}$. With a 9-volt supply — comfortably within reach of a few flashlight cells, something no tube stage could say — and a collector resistor $R_C = 4.7\ \mathrm{k}\Omega$, the quiescent voltage dropped across that resistor is $V_{R_C} = I_C R_C = 1\ \mathrm{mA} \times 4.7\ \mathrm{k}\Omega \approx 4.7\ \mathrm{V}$, leaving $V_{CE} \approx V_{CC} - V_{R_C} \approx 9\ \mathrm{V} - 4.7\ \mathrm{V} = 4.3\ \mathrm{V}$ of headroom for the output signal to swing in. For small-signal voltage gain, the stage's intrinsic emitter resistance is approximately $r_e \approx V_T/I_E \approx 26\ \mathrm{mV}/1\ \mathrm{mA} = 26\ \Omega$, using the standard ~26 mV thermal voltage at room temperature, giving a voltage gain of roughly $A_v \approx -R_C/r_e \approx -4700/26 \approx -180$. A single stage, running on a fraction of the voltage of a single tube's plate supply, drawing no filament current at all, delivering gain that rivaled a tube stage outright — that combination was the whole promise of solid-state amateur radio, even in an era when it could not yet touch a tube's raw RF power-handling.

That last qualifier mattered enormously through the 1950s. Germanium's biggest practical vice was thermal sensitivity: its collector-junction leakage current roughly doubles for every 10°C rise in temperature, and if that leakage feeds back into the bias point uncorrected, a transistor can run away with itself — heating, drawing more leakage current, heating further, until it destroys itself. The bias network shown in the figure, with its voltage-divider base bias and its bypassed emitter resistor $R_E$, exists specifically to hold the operating point stable against that runaway, swamping out the transistor's own temperature drift with a fixed, predictable resistive network. Even with that problem solved for small-signal stages, though, 1950s transistors simply could not survive the voltages and currents a transmitter's power amplifier needed to put a strong signal on 75 meters.

**FACT:** Tubes still dominate high-power HF stages into the 1960s–70s.

Solid state's takeover of amateur radio would come, but not yet, and not at the transmitter's business end.

### The State of the Art in 1951

A representative station of the 1951 ARRL Handbook's era — the twenty-eighth edition, the last one this book can call fully public domain before the copyright clock resets on later editions — still ran on two separate boxes rather than one. The receiver, as often as not, was a converted BC-348 or ARC-5 "Command" set pulled straight from surplus stock, retuned and fitted with a BFO for CW work and, increasingly, a product-detector conversion for the growing minority running sideband; hams with more money bought a purpose-built commercial receiver from National or Hallicrafters instead. The transmitter sat beside it as its own chassis, its own power supply, its own dial — and, for the era's flood of new Novice licensees, its own crystal, since the rules gave them no choice: a Novice-class rig ran exactly where its crystal put it, full stop, no VFO permitted. A ham's final amplifier tube was, more often than not, a type that had been standard issue for a decade or more already — the 807 beam tetrode was as close to a universal choice for a 1950s ham transmitter's output stage as amateur radio has ever had, cheap, rugged, and forgiving, equally at home keyed for CW or plate-modulated for AM phone. A newcomer with a still-thinner budget than a surplus-BC-348 conversion could buy a purpose-built entry-level communications receiver instead — Hallicrafters' inexpensive S-38 series, aimed squarely at beginners and short-wave listeners, sold in enough volume through these years to become nearly as familiar a first receiver as any surplus conversion. On the test bench, the era's universal tool was the grid-dip oscillator: a simple, self-contained variable oscillator with a built-in meter that a builder coupled loosely to any unpowered tuned circuit — an antenna trap, a tank coil, a filter section — and swept until the meter dipped, reading off the circuit's own resonant frequency without ever having to apply power to the device under test at all. No 1950s home station's bench was complete without one. The Handbook itself was getting visibly thicker with each passing edition through these years, its construction chapters racing to keep pace with FM tuner kits, television alignment gear, and, as the decade wore on, the earliest amateur projects built around the new transistor — even while its core transmitter and receiver chapters stayed solidly, unapologetically tube-based.

Most stations on the air in 1951 were still running plain AM or CW, exactly as they had for two decades. But the fraction running sideband was growing month over month, and it showed in the classified pages and construction articles: a phasing exciter built around a design descended from Norgaard's 1948 article, or — increasingly, as the decade wore on — a mechanical-filter unit built along the lines W2CHO had popularized. The Handbook's transmitter chapter, like the amateur population it served, was in the middle of a slow-motion argument between two philosophies of building the same signal, and neither had yet won.

Operating culture was recovering its own prewar momentum alongside the equipment. The ARRL's DX Century Club award, established in 1937 to recognize confirmed two-way contact with at least 100 different countries, had gone dormant for the war and was reinstated once amateur operation resumed after 1945; by the early 1950s, chasing DXCC — and the contests the League ran alongside it — gave the growing SSB minority a concrete competitive reason to care about the mode's power and bandwidth advantages, beyond mere technical novelty. A station's antenna, through this period, was still overwhelmingly a wire: a half-wave dipole or a random-length end-fed long wire strung between the house and whatever tree, mast, or chimney stood handy. Rotatable Yagi beams, built on the parasitic-array principles Chapter 4 introduced, were becoming a realistic home-station project as mass-produced rotators reached the market — no longer confined, as they largely had been before the war, to the well-funded contest and DX specialists who had pioneered them.

Mobile operation was becoming a visible fixture of the hobby by 1951 as well, and it demanded an awkward compromise that only tube technology's limits made necessary. An automobile's electrical system supplied 6 or 12 volts DC; a tube transmitter's plate circuit wanted several hundred volts, and no transistor of the era could survive RF power duty at all. Mobile rigs bridged that gap with a vibrator power supply — a mechanically buzzing relay-and-transformer unit that chopped the car battery's low DC voltage into AC, stepped the AC up through a transformer, and rectified it back down into the high-voltage DC a tube's plate circuit needed — or, in higher-power installations, a dynamotor doing the identical job with a spinning rotary converter in place of a vibrating reed. Both were noisy, both wasted a meaningful share of the car battery's capacity as heat, and both would remain a fact of mobile amateur life for another decade, until solid-state power conversion finally made the trip from car battery to transmitter efficiently.

What the 1951 Handbook could not yet show, because it did not exist as an amateur product, was the transceiver. That was still six years away. A 1951 station's operator tuned two dials to work one contact, "netted" the transmitter to the receiver's frequency by ear before every transmission, and lived with the fact that his receiver and his transmitter were, electrically, strangers to each other that happened to share a bench. The surplus boom had put a station within reach of nearly anyone who wanted one; the license restructuring had given that station's operator a clearer ladder to climb; sideband had given him a technically better way to be heard. What postwar amateur radio had not yet done, by 1951, was put the receiver and the transmitter in the same box.

That would take Collins, six more years, and one more idea: that a rig only needs one oscillator to know where it is.

Then, in October 1957, a beeping 23-inch sphere crossed the sky, and every radio amateur on earth who owned a receiver capable of 20 MHz found out about it within hours, whether or not their government had told them first. Sputnik did not change amateur radio's technology overnight — the following chapter's story is still built on tubes, still built on the FM and repeater work that had barely begun by 1960 — but it changed what amateurs let themselves imagine building. A rig no longer had to stay on the ground.

Half a world away and a few years further on, a different kind of transmitter was about to leave the atmosphere entirely — built not by a corporation with a factory in Cedar Rapids, but by a handful of engineers with a rocket booster's worth of unused payload space and an idea nobody in the FCC's 1951 rulebook had anticipated needing a rule for.
