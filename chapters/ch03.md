## 3. The Golden Age of Tubes (1928–1936)

*"16,829 licensed amateurs in the United States in 1929; 46,850 by 1936 — growth through the worst years of the Depression."*
*— ARRL/FCC license statistics*

### The Story

The shortwave revolution had proved a point and left a mess. By the end of 1927, amateurs on both sides of the Atlantic had shown that a few watts on the right wavelength could cross oceans more reliably than a kilowatt of longwave spark ever had. But "the right wavelength" was still, in practice, whatever an operator's home-ground coil-and-condenser oscillator happened to drift onto that evening. A regenerative or Hartley-Colpitts transmitter built from a magazine article could wander tens of kilocycles as its tubes warmed, as the operator's hand neared the panel, as the antenna current changed with weather. In a world where every station shared a handful of narrow slices of spectrum with a thousand competitors, that kind of looseness was no longer merely annoying. It was the problem the entire next decade of amateur engineering would be organized around solving.

The framework for that decade had just been fixed at a conference table.

**FACT:** International Radiotelegraph Conference (Washington, DC) sets worldwide treaty amateur band allocations (80/75, 40, 20, 10 m) and callsign-prefix blocks.

Through the last years of the 1920s, national administrations — the U.S. Federal Radio Commission among them — brought their domestic rulebooks into line with that Washington treaty, and by the early 1930s an amateur tuning across the high-frequency spectrum found something the previous decade had never offered: a stable, internationally recognized ladder of bands, each one roughly double the frequency of the one below it. Eighty meters sat near 3.5 MHz, forty near 7 MHz, twenty near 14 MHz, ten near 28 MHz. It looked like bureaucratic tidiness. It was actually a gift to circuit designers, and amateurs of the era spent the better part of a decade discovering exactly how useful a gift it was.

Each rung of that ladder also developed its own personality, and every operator learned it by ear long before anyone wrote it down as propagation theory. Eighty meters was the reliable local-and-regional band, thick with signals after dark and prone to daytime absorption that shrank its range to a few hundred miles; forty split the difference, carrying respectable distance work at most hours; twenty meters, as the decade wore on, emerged as the great DX band, the one where a modest station with a clean crystal note could plausibly work five continents in an evening; and ten meters, wide open some days and eerily silent on others, taught its operators the first hard lessons about a still poorly understood ionosphere that behaved differently, band by band, hour by hour, and — though nobody could have named the cause yet — year by year with the sun.

The other half of the problem was: once you have a band, how do you *stay in it*? An oscillator that wanders is useless in an 8000-Hz-wide phone channel packed with a hundred other stations, and the Federal Radio Commission — soon to become something more permanent — was making its opinion on the matter increasingly plain through tightened frequency-tolerance rules and public irritation at "swinging" and "chirpy" signals in its enforcement notices. Amateurs did not need a law to tell them crystal control was coming; the DX logs and the QRM told them.

**FACT:** *QST* publishes its first crystal-controlled-transmitter construction schematic, seeding rapid mid-1920s amateur uptake of crystal control.

That article predates this chapter's opening year, but its consequences define it: through the late 1920s and into the 1930s, crystal control went from a novelty only the well-heeled or the well-connected could afford — finished, ground, calibrated quartz plates were originally scarce and often cut to order — to something a small manufacturer could sell finished and mounted for a few dollars by mail order. Firms such as Bliley Electric, founded in Erie, Pennsylvania at the top of this era, made a business model out of it: order a frequency, receive a plate ground to hold it. By 1936 an amateur who wanted a rock-steady CW note no longer needed a machine shop and a lapidary's patience. He needed a catalog and a postal money order.

None of this was a mandate. No single regulation ever required an American amateur to run crystal control; the shift was a matter of frequency-stability rules, competitive pressure, and plain peer pressure operating together, and it was gradual enough that plenty of stations on the low end of the era still ran self-excited MOPA rigs, particularly on CW where a modest chirp was tolerated in ways a wandering phone carrier was not. But the direction of travel was unmistakable, and it is the technical throughline of this whole chapter: master-oscillator power-amplifier architecture, introduced in the previous era to keep antenna loading from "pulling" a self-excited oscillator, found its ideal master oscillator in a slab of quartz that would barely notice the antenna was there at all.

The receiving side of the hobby went through its own quiet revolution over the same years, and for a similar reason — narrower channels demand sharper selectivity, and sharper selectivity is exactly what the tuned-radio-frequency and regenerative receivers of the 1920s struggled to deliver as tube counts and gang-tuned condensers were pushed to their practical limits. The superheterodyne, patented by Edwin Armstrong back in 1920 but expensive and finicky to build with 1920s tubes, became cheap and reliable enough through the early 1930s — helped enormously by new multi-element tubes purpose-built for the job — that it displaced the TRF set almost completely in both the amateur and the consumer marketplace. Manufacturers who had been building simple regenerative receivers for hams turned out landmark superheterodynes instead: the Hammarlund Manufacturing Company's Comet Pro, one of the first superhets marketed specifically to amateurs, appeared at the opening of the decade; a newly organized Chicago outfit called Hallicrafters, started by William Halligan in 1932, began building affordable communications receivers within a year or two of its founding; and the National Company's HRO, introduced in the middle of the decade with a distinctive plug-in coil system and an unusually precise geared dial, became the receiver against which every serious station measured itself — and would still be doing communications-intelligence work for two governments a decade later. None of these firms existed, in any recognizable form, before this era. The Golden Age of Tubes did not just refine amateur radio's circuits. It built the industry that would supply the hobby, and much of the Allied war effort, for the following generation.

Voice kept pace with code. Plate-modulated "phone" transmission — simpler to build well than the suppressed-carrier techniques that would eventually replace it, and increasingly practical as Class B audio amplifiers made high modulator power efficient to generate — grew from a curiosity into a mainstream mode through these years, crowded enough by the mid-1930s that national administrations began carving explicit phone subbands out of the CW-dominated allocations to keep the two communities from talking over one another, in every sense. The cultural friction that produced — code purists grumbling about "phone hogs," phone operators grumbling about Morse elitism — would recur, with different vocabulary, for the rest of the book's history.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** The Washington treaty's band ladder was never only an American story. Through the early 1930s, IARU-affiliated national societies from Britain's RSGB to Japan's JARL worked their own governments toward compatible rules, so that a crystal ground for 14 MHz worked whether the operator sat in Hartford or Hertfordshire. But the same high-frequency spectrum amateurs had proved out was also, by now, obviously useful to states — and states noticed. The Dutch electronics firm Philips had been running its own international shortwave service, PCJJ at Eindhoven — home to the pioneering multilingual "Happy Station" program — since the tail end of the previous decade, proof that a receiver capable of chasing amateur DX on 20 meters could just as easily follow a growing menu of state and commercial shortwave voices sharing the same spectrum. The BBC's Empire Service, launched in December 1932, was built to reach London's colonies and dominions over exactly the shortwave paths amateurs had pioneered a decade earlier, and other governments were quick to follow with international shortwave services of their own. In Germany, the National Socialist government that took power in 1933 moved within months to fold the country's amateur clubs into a single state-supervised body, tightening the leash on private transmitting even as the state's own shortwave voice grew louder. The spectrum amateurs had made safe for weak-signal experimentation was becoming, in the same years, a spectrum governments wanted for themselves.

The last piece of the era's story is regulatory, and it is a genuine turning point rather than a technical one.

**FACT:** Communications Act of 1934 signed; creates the FCC, superseding the FRC and consolidating wire and wireless regulation.

The Federal Radio Commission had always been something of an emergency improvisation — a five-member board bolted onto the 1927 Radio Act to referee a spectrum crisis — and Franklin Roosevelt's administration, in the broader New Deal push to consolidate federal regulatory machinery, folded it along with the Interstate Commerce Commission's wire-communications authority into a single new independent agency. For the working amateur, the Federal Communications Commission's arrival changed remarkably little about day-to-day operating; call signs, license classes, and band assignments carried over more or less intact. What changed was durability: the FCC that took over amateur licensing on 19 June 1934 is, institutionally, the same body that regulates the Amateur Radio Service into the twenty-first century, and every rulemaking discussed in the rest of this book — incentive licensing, the no-code Technician, the retirement of the Morse requirement — happens under its authority, not the FRC's.

And through it all, the Depression that was quietly bankrupting radio manufacturers and broadcast stations across the country did remarkably little to slow the hobby's growth. Sixteen thousand-odd licensed amateurs at the decade's start had nearly tripled by its end, a fact the ARRL noted with evident satisfaction and the FCC's own statistics bear out without qualification. Whatever else the Golden Age of Tubes was — an era of settling bands, spreading crystals, and a receiver architecture finally coming into its own — it was also, against every economic headwind of the period, a decade in which amateur radio simply kept growing.

Part of what carried that growth through hard times was that a would-be builder in 1936 no longer had to invent circuits from scratch, or even trust a single magazine article, to get on the air correctly. By that year the Handbook had stopped being merely a construction manual and had become something closer to institutional memory. Its editor, Ross A. Hull, could report without much exaggeration that the book had gone through more than twenty separate printings and put upward of 370,000 copies into circulation since F. E. Handy's modest 1926 original — a scale no other amateur publication in the world approached, and one that let the Handbook set, as much as merely describe, the era's practice. Hull's own foreword put the ambition plainly: a book that combined the constructional guidance a builder needed with the manufacturers' own technical data on the tubes and parts actually on the market was meant to be nothing less than the complete standard manual of amateur high-frequency communication. Whether or not every subscriber read it cover to cover, an entire generation of builders learned crystal oscillators, superheterodyne receivers, and neutralized power amplifiers from its pages, edition after edition, in one shared notation and one shared set of circuit conventions — a standardization that did as much to spread this era's core technologies across a fast-growing, geographically scattered hobby as any single circuit innovation did. The same growth showed up in the Handbook's advertising pages, which by mid-decade had swollen into a small industry directory in their own right: firms that had not existed, in any recognizable form, before this era — crystal grinders, coil and condenser houses, the new receiver manufacturers already discussed above — bought space to sell components and finished gear to a customer base that, Depression or no Depression, kept expanding every single year of the 1930s.

### How It Works

Four technologies define this era's circuits, and they reinforce one another in ways that reward being taught together: a crystal that replaces an LC tank's inductor with something orders of magnitude more stable; a receiver architecture built around a single fixed frequency where all the hard work of amplification and selection can happen once and for all; a modulation scheme whose spectrum every operator needed to understand to avoid splattering across a neighbor's channel; and a new tube structure that made high RF gain cheap and self-oscillation rare. Understood properly, all four are really one story: 1920s vacuum-tube circuits pushed toward the frequency stability and selectivity that narrow, crowded, harmonically related bands now demanded.

#### Crystal control: quartz as a resonator, not just a rock

An inductor-capacitor tank circuit resonates at $f_0 = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{LC})$, and its sharpness — its quality factor $Q = f_0 / BW_{3dB}$ — is set by how much energy the circuit loses to resistance, radiation, and the tube's own loading on each cycle. A carefully built 1930s LC tank might reach a Q in the low hundreds. That sounds respectable until you set it beside quartz.

Quartz is piezoelectric: mechanically strain a properly cut slab of it and a voltage appears across its faces; apply a voltage and the slab flexes. A thin plate of quartz, ground to the right dimensions, has a natural mechanical vibration frequency — determined almost entirely by its physical thickness and cut angle — at which it will ring like a tuning fork if excited electrically, and because that ringing is a mechanical resonance in a nearly loss-free crystal lattice rather than an electrical resonance in a lossy coil, its effective Q runs from the tens of thousands into the hundreds of thousands: one to three orders of magnitude sharper than the best LC tank a 1930s builder could wind by hand. Just as important, a quartz plate's resonant frequency depends on its geometry and its elastic properties, both of which are far less sensitive to the everyday enemies of frequency stability — supply-voltage sag, tube warm-up, a hand near the panel, a gust of wind on the antenna — than an inductor's magnetic field or a capacitor's dielectric ever were. That combination, colossal Q plus physical stability, is the entire reason crystal control mattered.

George W. Pierce of Harvard worked out how to put a crystal directly into an oscillator's feedback loop in 1923, and the circuit that carries his name is the era's gold standard for staying "on frequency."

**FACT:** A Colpitts-derived oscillator with a quartz crystal replacing the inductor, giving very high frequency stability.

The Colpitts oscillator, recall, uses a tapped capacitor to set the feedback ratio between grid and plate; the Pierce arrangement keeps that capacitive divider but removes the inductor from the tank entirely, letting the crystal itself supply the frequency-determining element. A quartz plate, electrically, does not behave like a simple capacitor or a simple inductor — near its mechanical resonance it presents a rapidly swinging reactance, capacitive on one side of resonance and inductive on the other, across an extraordinarily narrow band of frequencies. Biased correctly, the Pierce circuit's feedback loop can only be closed — can only sustain the phase relationship an oscillator needs to keep itself going — in that narrow inductive-reactance window. Push the circuit's operating point even slightly outside it and the loop gain or the phase relationship collapses; there is no gentle detuning the way there is with an LC tank, no smooth glide to a slightly different frequency as conditions drift. The crystal simply refuses to oscillate anywhere else. That all-or-nothing behavior, which would be a defect in almost any other kind of oscillator, is exactly what makes crystal control valuable: it converts "approximately the right frequency, more or less" into "exactly this frequency, or nothing."

One practical complication tied this circuit tightly to the newly settled band ladder. Quartz plates ground to vibrate cleanly and robustly in the 1930s were most reliably cut for relatively low fundamental frequencies — commonly in the range that mapped onto 80 meters — because a crystal's fundamental frequency rises as its thickness falls, and a plate ground thin enough to resonate directly at 20 or 10 meters was fragile, expensive to finish accurately, and prone to fracturing under its own drive power. The practical solution was the same trick used everywhere else in RF engineering: run a tuned amplifier stage hard enough into its nonlinear region that its plate current is rich in harmonics, then tune that stage's output tank to the harmonic you want instead of the fundamental. A "doubler" tunes to twice the input frequency, a "tripler" to three times, and so on; several such stages can be cascaded into a multiplier chain between the crystal oscillator and the power amplifier, each stage buffering the delicate crystal circuit a little further from the antenna's loading, in the same MOPA spirit as the isolated master oscillators of the previous chapter.

This is where the harmonic band ladder stopped being administrative tidiness and became an engineering convenience. A crystal ground near 3.5 MHz sits an amateur directly on 80 meters. Run its output through one doubler stage and the multiplier chain lands at roughly 7 MHz — 40 meters. Double again and it reaches roughly 14 MHz — 20 meters. Double a third time and it reaches roughly 28 MHz — 10 meters. A single crystal, one robust, cheaply ground low-frequency plate, could in principle put a transmitter on all four HF amateur bands simply by retuning the multiplier and final-amplifier tanks, because the 1927 Washington conference had, whether by design or fortunate coincidence, allocated the amateur HF bands in a clean chain of octaves. Few amateur builders actually ran a single crystal through three cascaded doublers in practice — losses and spurious outputs accumulate with every stage — but two-stage chains (crystal, one doubler, final amplifier) putting an 80-meter or 40-meter crystal onto 40 or 20 meters were commonplace construction-article fare throughout the era, and every builder who ground through the arithmetic understood exactly why a crystal cut for the bottom of the band ladder was worth having.

The multiplier chain existed for a second reason as well as the first: a crystal cannot simply be driven as hard as a builder likes. Too much RF current through the plate, and the mechanical flexing that current excites can exceed what the quartz can stand, cracking or fracturing a plate that took real money and real skill to grind — amateur construction articles of the era were emphatic about running the oscillator stage at the lowest drive level that would reliably start and sustain oscillation, and about letting the multiplier and amplifier stages that followed do the work of building the signal back up to real power. A crystal, in other words, supplied frequency, not power; the whole point of the MOPA-descended chain behind it was to take a signal too weak and too delicate to put on the air directly and amplify it, stage by stage, into something that could.

Quartz's stability is not perfect, either, and era engineers already understood why. A crystal plate's resonant frequency depends slightly on temperature, and the cuts common through most of this era — ground more or less parallel to the crystal's electrical axis, and generally called X-cuts — carried a noticeable negative temperature coefficient, drifting measurably downward in frequency as the plate warmed from the shack's evening heat or its own oscillator tube's nearby filament. Two answers to that problem emerged in these very years. The blunt one, borrowed straight from precision timekeeping, was to stop fighting the coefficient and instead hold the crystal's temperature constant, mounting the plate inside a small thermostatically controlled oven so that its own regulated temperature, not the room's, set its frequency — a technique the era's more demanding commercial and government installations used, though it added expense and complexity beyond what an ordinary amateur QSO needed. The more elegant answer arrived in 1934, when a new way of slicing the quartz — cutting the plate at a carefully chosen angle through the crystal's axes rather than parallel to them, in a style that came to be known as the AT-cut — produced a temperature coefficient that very nearly vanished across an ordinary operating range, without any oven at all. Even the era's simplest, unheated X-cut crystals, though, drifted only a few dozen cycles per million per degree — a rounding error next to the many-kilocycle wander a self-excited LC oscillator could produce over the same temperature range, and the entire reason amateurs accepted the crystal's one real limitation: you were locked to whatever exact frequency the plate had been ground for, with none of a variable-frequency oscillator's freedom to slide a few kilocycles to find open space in a crowded band, in exchange for stability nothing else in the era's toolbox could touch.

#### The superheterodyne, taken seriously

By the early 1930s, the tuned-radio-frequency receiver — several ganged, simultaneously tuned RF amplifier stages ahead of a detector — had run into a wall that no amount of clever winding could get around. Every stage's gain and selectivity change as you retune it across a band, tracking multiple tuned circuits together gets mechanically harder as you add stages, and pushing a TRF stage's gain higher to compensate for weak high-frequency amplification tends to push it toward instability. The superheterodyne receiver, patented by Armstrong in 1920, sidesteps the whole problem with one structural idea.

**FACT:** A receiver that mixes the incoming signal with a local oscillator to a fixed intermediate frequency where most gain and selectivity live.

{{fig:superhet-block}}

Follow a signal through the block diagram. An antenna delivers a weak RF signal at $f_{RF}$ to a lightly tuned preselector stage — enough selectivity to reject the most obviously out-of-band interference, but nowhere near the sharpness the receiver will ultimately need. That signal is fed into a mixer along with a strong, locally generated sine wave at $f_{LO}$ from the receiver's own local oscillator. A mixer is a deliberately nonlinear device; multiplying two sinusoids together produces new components at their sum and their difference, and the receiver's designer arranges for a fixed-tuned bandpass filter immediately after the mixer to pass only the difference:

$f_{IF} = |f_{RF} - f_{LO}|$

That difference frequency, the intermediate frequency, is the whole point of the exercise. However far the operator tunes the front end — whether $f_{RF}$ is 3.5 MHz or 28 MHz — the local oscillator is mechanically ganged to the front-end tuning capacitor so that it tracks a fixed distance above (or below) the incoming signal, and $f_{IF}$ therefore stays put. Every stage that follows the mixer — one or more IF amplifier stages, the detector, the AVC rectifier — is built once, permanently tuned to that single frequency, and optimized as hard as 1930s tube technology allows, because it never has to retune. A designer could lavish two or three cascaded, double-tuned IF transformers on selectivity that no ganged TRF front end could match across an entire tuning range, simply because the IF strip only ever has to be good at one frequency. This is the entire reason the superheterodyne displaced the TRF set through this era: it converts "build good selectivity at every frequency you might tune to" into "build good selectivity once, at one frequency, and let the mixer do the rest."

It does not do this for free. Because the mixer only preserves the *magnitude* of the difference $f_{RF} - f_{LO}$, two different input frequencies — one above the local oscillator, one below it, each exactly $f_{IF}$ away — produce the identical intermediate frequency and are, as far as the IF strip and everything after it can tell, the same signal:

$f_{image} = f_{LO} \pm f_{IF} = f_{RF} \pm 2 f_{IF}$

Whichever one the operator wanted, the other rides along uninvited unless something ahead of the mixer keeps it out. That something is the lightly tuned preselector stage — the only part of the whole receiver still doing old-fashioned, frequency-tracking, single-tuned-circuit selectivity — and its job is to attenuate the image before it ever reaches the mixer, since nothing downstream can tell the two apart once they arrive at the same IF. This structural weakness has a name: the image-frequency problem, and it sets up a trade-off that occupied receiver designers for the whole rest of the tube era. A low intermediate frequency is easy to build sharp, stable, high-gain amplifier stages for — a few hundred kilocycles is a friendly frequency for 1930s tube technology — but it places the image close to the wanted signal, only $2 f_{IF}$ away, which is a demanding target for a single preselector stage to reject. A high intermediate frequency pushes the image comfortably out of the preselector's passband, but building high-gain, sharply selective amplifier stages gets progressively harder as the IF itself climbs. Era designers split the difference, and era IFs commonly ran **175–500 kHz** — a fair summary of what an amateur superheterodyne of 1931, 1933, or 1936 was likely to use.

> **Worked example:** Take a receiver tuned to 14,300 kHz — near the top of the 20-meter band — with a local oscillator running above the signal frequency and an intermediate frequency of 450 kHz, comfortably inside the era's common 175–500 kHz range. The local oscillator sits at 14,300 + 450 = 14,750 kHz. The image frequency, on the far side of the local oscillator from the wanted signal, sits at 14,750 + 450 = 15,200 kHz — equivalently, 14,300 + (2 × 450) = 15,200 kHz. A spurious carrier at 15,200 kHz, if the preselector doesn't stop it, mixes down to exactly the same 450 kHz IF as the wanted 14,300 kHz signal and is heard right on top of it. That 900 kHz of separation is about 6.3 percent of the 14,300 kHz signal frequency — well within what a single tuned preselector circuit of ordinary Q can reject. Now retune the same receiver, same 450 kHz IF, to 28,600 kHz — in the 10-meter band, and, not coincidentally, exactly double 14,300 kHz. The local oscillator moves to 29,050 kHz and the image to 29,500 kHz. The absolute separation is still 900 kHz, but it is now only 3.1 percent of the operating frequency — half the fractional separation of the 20-meter case — and the very same preselector circuit that comfortably rejected the image on 20 meters rejects it only half as well, in relative terms, on 10 meters. Nothing about the receiver changed. Only the arithmetic of how close, proportionally, the image sits to the signal.

That worked comparison is not an idle curiosity. It is the specific technical seed of a problem this book will return to: as amateurs pushed their equipment toward the top of the HF spectrum and beyond, into the 5- and 2½-meter VHF bands the next era would take up in earnest, a fixed low intermediate frequency's image rejection only gets worse, because the image's fractional distance from the signal keeps shrinking as the operating frequency climbs.

The other circuit that made a well-built 1930s superheterodyne noticeably pleasant to operate, compared to its 1920s ancestors, was automatic gain control — contemporary builders more often called it automatic volume control, or AVC, though this book uses the modern term consistently: feedback that reduces receiver gain on strong signals, to level output and prevent overload. A small rectifier, usually sharing a tube envelope with the detector, samples the strength of the incoming IF signal and develops a DC control voltage proportional to it; that voltage is fed back to the grid bias of the RF and IF amplifier stages — built, by the mid-1930s, from tubes specifically designed with a "variable-mu" or remote-cutoff grid structure so that increasing negative bias smoothly reduces gain over a wide range without distorting the signal — so that a strong local station and a weak DX signal produce roughly comparable audio output without the operator's hand on the volume control. By the middle of this era, AGC of this kind had become standard equipment in any receiver design an amateur would consider modern, precisely because a superheterodyne's IF strip, unlike a TRF set's cascaded RF stages, offers one single, convenient point at which to sample signal strength and apply the correction.

Two further pieces of tube engineering made the superheterodyne recipe practical to build cheaply, and both were new inside this era. Early superhets needed a separate oscillator tube and a separate mixer tube — two envelopes, two sockets, two sets of filament current, and a fussy business of coupling the oscillator's output into the mixer without either stage dragging the other off frequency. The pentagrid converter tube, which reached the American market as the type 2A7 in the early 1930s and quickly spread into cheaper and improved descendants, solved that by building both functions into a single five-grid envelope: one part of the tube's internal structure works as a small self-contained oscillator, and the electron stream it controls is then further modulated, inside the same envelope, by the incoming RF signal on a separate control grid, so the plate current already contains the wanted difference-frequency component before it ever reaches the first IF transformer. One tube, one socket, and — just as valuable to a designer — one less opportunity for the oscillator to pull off frequency under load. A similar consolidation happened at the detector end: tubes marketed as a "duo-diode-triode" packed a pair of diode plates, for AVC rectification and signal detection, alongside a full triode audio-amplifier section into one envelope, so a set that had needed three separate tubes for detection, AVC, and first-audio amplification a few years earlier could now do the same job with one.

None of this worked, though, unless the local oscillator actually tracked the front-end tuning across the whole band — held that constant $f_{IF}$ offset from one end of the dial to the other — and getting a single-shaft, multi-gang tuning capacitor to do that with mass-manufacturable coils and condensers was a real mechanical and electrical puzzle, not just a matter of ganging two identical tuned circuits together, since the local-oscillator circuit's tuned components have to trace a systematically *different* capacitance curve than the RF circuit's in order to hold a constant frequency *offset* rather than a constant frequency *ratio*. Designers solved it with small trimmer and padder condensers wired in parallel and in series with the oscillator's main tuning capacitor, adjusted — usually at just two or three points across the band — to bend the oscillator's tracking curve close enough to the front end's that the error stayed within the IF amplifier's own passband everywhere in between. It was fiddly bench work for the factory, invisible to the operator, and one more reason a well-tracked, three-gang superheterodyne from National or Hammarlund commanded real money next to a home-brew set built from mismatched parts.

#### Amplitude modulation: what plate modulation actually puts on the air

A CW transmitter keys a pure carrier on and off. A phone transmitter using amplitude modulation instead varies that carrier's amplitude, continuously, in step with an audio waveform, and the most common way 1930s amateurs did it was plate modulation: audio power, developed by a Class B (or, less commonly, Class A) audio amplifier and coupled through a modulation transformer, is added in series with the power amplifier stage's high-voltage DC plate supply, so the instantaneous plate voltage — and with it, the instantaneous RF output amplitude — rises and falls at the audio rate.

{{fig:am-spectrum}}

The depth of that variation is the modulation index, $m = A_m / A_c$ — the ratio of the peak modulating (audio) amplitude to the steady carrier amplitude. At $m = 0$ there is no modulation at all, just a bare carrier. As $m$ climbs toward 1, the transmitted envelope swings from double its unmodulated amplitude at an audio peak down toward zero at an audio trough; $m = 1$, or 100 percent modulation, is the deepest an amplitude-modulated signal can go without the envelope clipping at the bottom, since a negative-going audio peak beyond $m = 1$ would ask the transmitter to produce less than zero carrier amplitude, which no real circuit can do. Push past $m = 1$ — "overmodulation" — and the envelope flat-tops instead of following the audio faithfully, generating harmonic distortion and, worse for a crowded band, spurious sideband energy well outside the channel the operator thought they were using. Keeping $m$ safely at or under 1 was as much a matter of good amateur citizenship as it was of audio fidelity, and every construction article of the era that touched on phone operation said so.

What actually leaves the antenna when a carrier is modulated is not a single frequency smeared wider, but three distinct components. Multiplying a steady carrier by an audio-varying envelope and expanding the result mathematically shows a full-amplitude carrier at the original frequency $f_c$, unchanged in frequency and reduced only slightly in relative power, plus two new components — an upper sideband at $f_c + f_m$ and a lower sideband at $f_c - f_m$ — each carrying an amplitude proportional to $m$, for every frequency $f_m$ present in the modulating audio. In short: audio varies the PA plate supply, and the output is carrier plus two sidebands, with occupied bandwidth of roughly twice the highest audio frequency present — about 6 kHz total for 3 kHz audio — which follows directly from the sideband picture: since the highest audio frequency present, $f_m$, pushes a sideband out that far on *both* sides of the carrier,

$BW_{AM} \approx 2 \cdot f_m$

Communications-grade voice audio of the era ran to roughly 3 kHz, which is where the canon's "about 6 kHz total for 3 kHz audio" comes from directly — a bandwidth figure every phone operator sharing a crowded band needed to have in his head, whether or not he could derive it.

There is a sobering corollary buried in that spectrum picture, one period builders felt in their power bills even if they rarely stated it in these terms: at 100 percent modulation, fully half of the transmitted sideband power is concentrated in a carrier component that, by itself, conveys no information at all — it is the two sidebands, together carrying at most half the carrier's power even at maximum legal modulation, that do all the useful work. Put concrete numbers on it: a rig running 100 watts of unmodulated carrier, driven to full 100 percent modulation, transmits a total of 150 watts — the original 100-watt carrier, unchanged, plus 50 watts split evenly between the two sidebands, 25 watts apiece. Turn the modulation down to a more typical, conversational 50 percent and the sidebands shrink to a combined 12.5 watts, a quarter of their full-modulation figure, because sideband power scales with the *square* of the modulation index, not with the index itself — a fact every builder chasing "full quieting" modulation on a distant contact, rather than a timid, safely-under-modulated whisper, had good engineering reason to care about. Getting that sideband power onto the air took real audio muscle from the modulator: period design rules of thumb held that, for 100 percent plate modulation, the audio modulator had to be capable of supplying peak audio power on the order of half the DC input power delivered to the modulated RF stage — a demand efficient enough tube designs like the Class B push-pull audio amplifier were specifically chosen to meet, since a Class B stage draws essentially no plate current between audio peaks and so wastes far less heat than a Class A modulator built to the same peak-power capability. This basic inefficiency of carrier-plus-two-sidebands AM — half the power spent carrying no information — is not this chapter's problem to solve. It becomes, a decade and a half later, the whole justification for single sideband.

#### Pentodes and the fading art of neutralization

A triode is a wonderfully simple amplifying device — cathode, control grid, plate, three elements and nothing more — but that simplicity carries a cost at radio frequencies. The plate and the control grid, sitting close together inside the envelope, form a small but real capacitor between them, and at RF that stray grid-to-plate capacitance provides an unwanted feedback path straight from a stage's high-gain output back to its input. Combine enough gain with that feedback path and an RF amplifier stage will, sooner or later, turn itself into an oscillator nobody asked for — a parasitic oscillation that can radiate off-frequency energy, desensitize a nearby receiver, or simply make the stage impossible to tune predictably.

Through the 1920s and into this era, the standard cure was neutralization — a feedback-cancelling network that nulls a tube's plate-to-grid capacitance to prevent parasitic self-oscillation in an RF amplifier. A small neutralizing capacitor, deliberately added outside the tube, taps a sample of the plate signal — inverted in phase, typically by center-tapping the plate or grid tank coil — and feeds it back into the grid circuit in exactly the amount needed to cancel the effect of the tube's own internal grid-plate capacitance. Get the neutralizing capacitor's value right and the stage behaves, from the standpoint of unwanted feedback, as though the offending internal capacitance simply were not there; get it wrong, even slightly, and the stage either oscillates or loses gain. Every builder who worked through a triode RF power amplifier construction article in this era's Handbooks learned the ritual: apply plate voltage with the stage's own excitation removed, watch a neon bulb or a grid-current meter for any sign of self-sustained oscillation, and trim the neutralizing capacitor a fraction of a turn at a time until the indication vanished. It worked, but it demanded careful, stage-by-stage adjustment that could drift and had to be checked again if the tube was replaced.

{{fig:pentode}}

The pentode, which reached commercial maturity through the tail end of the 1920s and the opening years of this decade, attacked the problem at its source rather than compensating for it afterward — a five-electrode tube adding screen and suppressor grids to a triode for higher gain and reduced neutralization need. Between the triode's control grid and plate, the pentode adds a second grid — the screen grid — held at a steady, RF-bypassed positive potential, which acts as an electrostatic shield: it intercepts the electric field lines that would otherwise run directly from plate to control grid, cutting the tube's internal grid-to-plate capacitance by roughly two orders of magnitude compared to an equivalent triode. Less grid-to-plate capacitance means less unwanted feedback to cancel in the first place, and by the mid-1930s a well-designed pentode RF stage often needed no neutralization at all, or only a token amount, where the equivalent triode stage would have demanded careful, ongoing attention. The screen grid brings a second benefit along with the first: because it sits at a fixed positive potential doing much of the work of accelerating electrons toward the plate, a pentode's voltage gain and effective plate resistance both run far higher than a triode's, for a given envelope size and filament power.

The screen grid alone, though, creates a smaller problem of its own — one earlier four-element "screen-grid" tubes (tetrodes, without a fifth element) suffered from directly. Electrons striking the plate hard enough knock loose secondary electrons, and in a simple tetrode those secondaries, finding themselves in the space between two positively charged elements, can be pulled back toward the positively biased screen grid rather than returning to the plate — a spurious current that can actually make the plate's effective resistance go *negative* over part of its operating range, a "dynatron kink" that plagued early screen-grid designs. The pentode's fifth element, a suppressor grid inserted between screen and plate and typically tied to cathode potential, creates a low-potential barrier that turns those wandering secondary electrons back toward the plate where they belong, eliminating the kink. Five elements, two problems solved together — reduced feedback capacitance and suppressed secondary emission — is why, by the middle of this era, the pentode had become the default choice for new RF amplifier, oscillator, and audio output designs, and why the elaborate neutralization ritual that had been a rite of passage for every triode-PA builder of the 1920s was, for many amateurs building fresh in 1936, something they read about rather than something they did.

The pentode was not, quite, the final word on the problem. Late in this era a related but distinct structure — the beam power tube, introduced commercially as the type 6L6 in 1936 — attacked the same secondary-emission issue the pentode's suppressor grid solved, but without a physical fifth grid at all. Instead, beam-forming plates, connected internally to the cathode and shaped to focus the electron stream from control grid to plate into dense, aligned sheets, create a region of space charge in front of the plate dense enough on its own to turn back stray secondary electrons the way a suppressor grid does — while also allowing the control and screen grids to be wound with wider spacing between turns, which intercepts less of the electron stream on its way to the plate. The result was a tube that delivered more RF or audio output power, at higher efficiency and lower distortion, from a given plate voltage and physical envelope than an equivalent pentode could manage, and did it more cheaply to manufacture, since a beam-forming structure was, if anything, simpler to build accurately than a fifth wound grid. Amateurs who got their hands on the new 6L6 in 1936, whether for an audio modulator or an RF final amplifier, were working with what was, at that moment, the single most advanced small transmitting-and-audio tube commercially available — and one that would remain a mainstay of amateur and commercial equipment for decades after this chapter's story moves on.

### The State of the Art in 1936

Open the 1936 ARRL Handbook to its transmitter chapters and the station it expects a competent amateur to build looks nothing like the self-excited MOPA rigs of a decade earlier. A crystal — bought finished, in a calibrated holder, from one of the small specialist firms that now existed purely to grind them — sits in a Pierce oscillator stage built around a single low-power tube. Its output feeds one or two multiplier stages, tuned to double or triple the crystal's fundamental up toward the operator's chosen band, and those feed a power amplifier stage running perhaps a few dozen watts of DC plate input — some builders considerably more, some considerably less, since the era's rules bounded the top of the range without dictating what an ordinary station actually ran. If that final stage uses an older triode, a neutralizing capacitor sits nearby, carefully adjusted; a growing number of builders in 1936 reached instead for one of the new pentode or beam-power tubes — the type 6L6 beam power tube, newly introduced that very year, was exactly the kind of component an ambitious builder was eager to try in a fresh design — and skipped the neutralization ritual almost entirely. For phone operation, a Class B audio modulator, built around a matched pair of tubes running push-pull, transformer-couples its output into the final amplifier's high-voltage supply, sized to deliver on the order of half that stage's DC input in peak audio power so that 100 percent modulation is actually achievable rather than merely advertised. Coupling that final tank to the antenna feeders in 1936 still means link coupling almost universally — a separate small coil of one or two turns, loosely wound alongside the main tank coil, adjusted by physically sliding it closer to or farther from the tank until the plate ammeter shows the amplifier correctly loaded. It works, and generations of amateurs tune up exactly that way, but it demands a second physical adjustment, coupled awkwardly to the first, every time the operator changes bands or antennas; a single tunable network that could match the antenna directly from the tank circuit while also filtering harmonics, with no separate movable link to fuss over, is still a few years off.

On the receiving side, the same Handbook walks its reader through a superheterodyne rather than the regenerative sets its earlier editions favored. A pentagrid converter tube — a single envelope combining local oscillator and mixer functions, of the kind that reached the market as the type 2A7 and its descendants earlier in the decade — does the frequency conversion, feeding one or two stages of intermediate-frequency amplification built around remote-cutoff pentodes so that automatic gain control can smoothly reduce their gain on strong signals without distortion. A separate beat-frequency oscillator, carried over from the previous era's regenerative-detector heritage, lets the same receiver copy CW by heterodyning the steady IF carrier into an audible tone. A power pentode or one of the newer beam tubes drives a speaker or a pair of headphones from the detected audio. Plenty of amateurs, particularly newer or more budget-conscious ones, were still working from older regenerative or TRF designs rather than building the full superheterodyne the Handbook now led with — the shift described in this chapter was real but never complete or compulsory — but the direction every serious construction article pointed was unmistakable, and it was the direction the whole receiver-manufacturing industry had already gone.

The antenna outside is, more often than not, still a half-wave dipole or an end-fed wire, cut using the same $c = f\lambda$ arithmetic Chapter 1 introduced, sized for whichever band the crystal in the shack happens to serve and pressed into service on other bands via its harmonics when convenience demands it. What has changed since the shortwave era is less the antenna than everything behind it: a transmitter that holds its frequency well enough to stay inside an assigned channel all evening, and a receiver sharp enough to pull a single desired signal out of a band now crowded with tens of thousands of fellow license holders. The Handbook itself, thickening edition over edition through this decade and reaching print runs its 1926 originators would not have dared predict, had become, for a growing fraction of that license-holder population, simply the place you started — the reference a well-equipped station's builder reached for before reaching for a soldering iron.

Somewhere in the last months of 1936, an amateur finishes a station built more or less to that recipe — crystal-controlled, superheterodyne-received, plate-modulated for phone, a fresh 6L6 warming up in a socket that would have held a neutralized triode two years earlier — and starts working the world with it. The rig holds its frequency. The receiver rejects its image, most of the time, on the bands it was built for. And if that amateur, restless, starts nudging a multiplier chain up past ten meters, toward the five-meter band a fringe of experimenters have been quietly exploring since the previous decade, the same image-frequency arithmetic worked through above starts closing in on him: the higher he climbs, the closer, proportionally, the ghost signal sits to the one he wants, and the harder his front end has to work to keep it out. Meanwhile the crystal in front of him — ground to a tolerance no 1920s builder could have matched, housed in a holder stamped with a manufacturer's name that did not exist ten years earlier — sits on the bench next to that season's newspapers, whose front pages carry dispatches from a civil war in Spain and a rearming Germany that a shortwave listener, tuning past the amateur bands some evenings, can already hear broadcasting in several languages at once. Higher power, higher frequencies, tighter tolerances: the next decade will demand all three from amateur radio's builders, for reasons that have nothing to do with circuit theory and everything to do with what those builders are about to be asked to build toward.
