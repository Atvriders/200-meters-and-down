## Epilogue — Where the Art Is Heading (2026)

*"Continuation and extension of the amateur's proven ability to contribute to the advancement of the radio art."*
*— 47 CFR § 97.1(b), Basis and Purpose of the Amateur Radio Service*

### The Story

By the time FT8 settled onto the bands, the pattern of the whole book had repeated once more.

**FACT:** FT8 released in WSJT-X by Joe Taylor (K1JT) and Steve Franke (K9AN); becomes the most-used digital mode within ~2 years.

A university physics department had done what the ARRL lab and a thousand basements had done a dozen times before: taken the newest available processing power and turned it into range. The pattern was old. Spark hams had traded bandwidth for keying speed. Regeneration traders had traded stability for gain. SSB traders had traded circuit complexity for spectrum. FT8 traded something new — patience, fifteen seconds of it per exchange — for a working link where a human ear could hear only hiss. Every one of those trades looks, in hindsight, like an argument about what "reach" is actually made of. That argument has never closed, and in the years since FT8 it has opened onto ground the book's earlier chapters could only gesture toward: orbit, network topology, and now, tentatively, the statistics of noise itself.

The most visible of these is also the oldest idea in the book restated at a much higher altitude. Amateur satellite work had meant OSCAR 6 and OSCAR 7's Mode A and Mode B linear transponders — low-earth-orbit birds a few hundred kilometers up, in view for a ten-minute pass, gone the moment they crossed the horizon, their signal sliding audibly up or down the passband from Doppler shift the whole time they were overhead. In November 2018 a very different kind of amateur satellite reached orbit: Es'hail-2, a Qatari communications satellite carrying, as a secondary payload, the first geostationary amateur radio transponder — designated QO-100 once it was on the air. Built and still largely operated by AMSAT-DL, the German amateur satellite organization, it does not orbit the earth in the sense OSCAR 7 did. It sits at a fixed longitude over the equator, its orbital period matched to the earth's rotation, and from any point within its beam it simply never sets. A station in São Paulo and a station in Bangkok can, in principle, both have their dish pointed at the same spot in the sky, permanently, and work each other across a downlink that requires no tracking, no Doppler correction, and no waiting for the next pass.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** QO-100's transponder was designed and is run by AMSAT-DL out of Germany, riding on a Qatari commercial satellite operated by Es'hailSat, reachable continuously from a footprint stretching roughly from Brazil to Thailand — a piece of infrastructure that belongs to no single national amateur service and could only exist as the product of the same international cooperation the IARU was built to coordinate in 1925. A ham on Réunion Island, a ham in Kazakhstan, and a ham in Ghana can all hear the same beacon at the same moment, a form of shared, simultaneous, wide-area access earlier amateur satellite generations never had.

The second new front is not in the sky at all but on the roof and in the basement, and it is a direct descendant of the packet-radio movement that TAPR built around the AX.25 protocol in the early 1980s.

**FACT:** A digital repeater that relays AX.25 packet frames hop-by-hop along a path listed in the frame.

For a 1980s packet station that digipeater was the whole of the network: a chain of individually addressed relays passing 1200-baud frames from node to node. The mesh-networking projects that grew up in the 2010s and 2020s — AREDN (the Amateur Radio Emergency Data Network) foremost among them — kept that same hop-by-hop relay idea but replaced the fixed, hand-typed digipeater path with self-organizing mesh routing protocols running on repurposed commercial Wi-Fi hardware, reflashed and retuned onto amateur microwave allocations where Part 97 permits far higher power and gain than an unlicensed 2.4 or 5 GHz network ever could. A rooftop node no longer just repeats; it discovers its neighbors, calculates the best multi-hop route across a whole neighborhood or county of similar nodes, and heals around any single link that drops — carrying not a single teleprinter channel but ordinary Internet Protocol traffic, video, and VoIP at megabit rates. Winlink extended the same idea back down to HF, tunneling email over PACTOR and VARA links to reach a laptop with no other path home. None of it needs a repeater trustee or a commercial ISP. It needs only what packet radio needed in 1981: hams willing to build their own network rather than merely use somebody else's.

The third front is the least settled and the newest. The software-defined receiver that Chapter 8 introduced — a radio that does its demodulating, filtering, and decoding as arithmetic on digitized samples rather than as physics in a fixed analog chain — turned every stage of reception into code that could, in principle, be rewritten. Through the 2010s that meant better filters and sharper FFT waterfalls. By the mid-2020s it increasingly means machine-learned models doing some of that arithmetic: neural-network noise reduction, trained on large libraries of recorded HF and VHF noise, now ships as a marketed feature in mainstream transceiver DSP chains, distinguishing a voice or a CW tone from an impulse-noise floor more effectively than a fixed adaptive filter tuned by hand. Research groups have gone further, training classifiers to recognize and label signals directly off a waterfall display, and experimenting with decoders for CW and other legacy modes that learn the statistics of an individual operator's fist or a specific band's interference rather than assuming a single fixed, textbook signal model. None of this yet has an ARRL Handbook chapter to anchor it, and much of it is closer to a conference paper than a consumer product — but the direction is unmistakable, and it raises a question the book's earlier chapters never had to ask: when the decoding itself is learned rather than merely automated, what exactly is the operator doing?

That question sits inside a larger and less comfortable one. Every earlier chapter of this book described a hobby growing — in license count, in band access, in the sheer variety of what a station could do. Growth has not been the story of the last two decades in most of the licensed world. National societies from the ARRL to the RSGB have tracked, and repeatedly publicized, a license population whose median age keeps climbing even as absolute numbers hold roughly flat or slip. Lowering the entry barrier — dropping the Morse requirement worldwide in 2003 and in the United States by 2007, and later selling complete SDR receivers for the price of a few band badges — did not by itself reverse the trend; it mostly made it easier for an already technically inclined adult to get licensed quickly, not necessarily easier to recruit a teenager away from every other claim on their attention. At the same moment, the RF environment amateurs share has gotten more contested from both directions at once. Switching-mode power supplies, LED lighting drivers, solar inverters, and vehicle electronics have measurably raised the noise floor on HF and VHF in many neighborhoods, degrading receive sensitivity no antenna or transceiver can restore. And at every World Radiocommunication Conference cycle, IARU delegations have had to argue, band by band, against reallocating slices of amateur spectrum to mobile broadband and to the burgeoning satellite mega-constellations competing for the same microwave real estate the amateur service has held, in some bands, since the 1927 international allocations this book opened with. An aging, shrinking-in-relative-terms population defending spectrum against well-funded commercial demand is not a new amateur predicament — the 1912 confinement to wavelengths below 200 meters was itself a spectrum fight the amateurs lost before they won it — but it is the one still open at the time of writing.

### How It Works

{{fig:modes-timeline}}

Nothing in this closing chapter is a new circuit. What it can do, now that the whole arc from spark to software-defined radio is on the record, is name precisely what each leap actually bought — because the three developments just described (a satellite that never sets, a network that heals itself, a receiver that learns) are not one thing wearing three costumes. They are three different axes along which "reach" can be extended, and the book's earlier chapters already demonstrated the first two.

Read straight down the timeline, each era's leap attacked a different variable:

- **Spark → CW (Ch. 1–2).** A pure, un-decaying carrier occupies a small fraction of a damped spark train's bandwidth for the same information; concentrating the same transmitted power into a narrower slice, and receiving through a correspondingly higher-Q tuned circuit, raises the effective signal-to-noise ratio at the ear without changing a single watt at the antenna.
- **Regeneration and the superheterodyne (Ch. 2–3).** Feedback and heterodyne mixing to a fixed IF turned weak-signal amplification and selectivity into a controllable engineering problem rather than an accident of a single tuned stage — reach bought with active gain, $f_{IF} = |f_{RF} - f_{LO}|$.
- **SSB (Ch. 5).** Removing the carrier and one redundant sideband roughly halves occupied bandwidth for the same audio, packing more usable channels — and, at fixed transmitter power, more watts per hertz of intelligence — into the same allocation.
- **FM (Ch. 6).** Carson's rule, $BW = 2(\Delta f + f_m)$, spends bandwidth deliberately to buy noise immunity: a limiter strips amplitude noise before the discriminator ever sees it, and the capture effect lets the stronger signal win cleanly instead of blending into mush.
- **Digital modes and SDR (Ch. 7–8).** PSK31 and FT8 spend time and computation instead of bandwidth or power, using forward error correction and, in FT8's case, long coherent integration across a fixed 15-second cycle to pull a known, highly structured waveform out from well beneath the noise.

Each of those five leaps operates on the classical point-to-point radio link: one transmitter, one receiver, one path, and a budget of power, bandwidth, and time to spend against noise. Claude Shannon's channel-capacity relation, $C = B\log_2(1 + \mathrm{SNR})$, states the boundary all five were pushing against — here SNR is the ordinary linear power ratio, not the dB figure used elsewhere in this book, and $C$ is the maximum error-free information rate a channel of bandwidth $B$ can carry at that ratio. A code like FT8's (174,91) LDPC is deliberately built to sit close to that Shannon boundary for an additive-white-noise channel; there is very little headroom left to extract more raw sensitivity from a single link by inventing a cleverer code alone. That ceiling is exactly why the newest work described above does not try to out-code FT8. It moves to different axes of the same problem instead.

QO-100 extends reach along an *availability* axis rather than an SNR axis. A geostationary satellite orbits at roughly 35,800 km, timed so its orbital period matches the earth's rotation; from a fixed ground point it therefore appears motionless. Chapter 6 gave the general relation for the frequency shift a moving satellite imposes on its signal.

**FACT:** The frequency offset Δf = f·v/c caused by a satellite's line-of-sight motion relative to the ground station.

For a LEO bird such as OSCAR 7, moving at several kilometers per second relative to the ground station, that shift ran to several kilohertz on 2 meters and proportionally more at 70 centimeters — "tune the downlink, not the uplink" was not a suggestion. For a geostationary transponder, the line-of-sight range rate $v$ is, for practical amateur purposes, zero: no continuous retuning, no Doppler chase, and — critically — no pass that ends. The trade is a much longer path (tens of thousands of kilometers instead of a few hundred) demanding a small dish and a low-noise downconverter rather than a handheld whip, but the reach it buys is temporal and geographic rather than a decibel figure: continuous, simultaneous, wide-area access that a scheduled ten-minute LEO pass structurally cannot offer.

AREDN-style mesh extends reach along a *topology* axis.

**FACT:** A radio in which demodulation, filtering, and mode decoding are done in software/DSP on digitized I/Q rather than fixed analog hardware.

That definition is what makes an inexpensive commodity Wi-Fi radio reprogrammable into an amateur microwave mesh node in the first place, but the reach gain itself comes from multiplying paths, not from squeezing more bits through any single one. A signal that cannot cross a hill in one hop can cross it in three, each hop running comfortably within its own SNR budget, with the mesh-routing protocol continuously recomputing which set of hops is currently best. It is the same principle Chapter 7's digipeaters used to relay a packet frame from node to node across a city — extended from a single, manually chosen relay path to a self-healing web of dozens of them, and from 1200 baud to tens of megabits per second.

Machine-learning-assisted decoding is the newest and least mature of the three, and it targets a fourth axis: the *statistical model of the noise itself*. LDPC and every fixed forward-error-correction code in this book's history assumes a known, tractable noise model — additive white Gaussian noise being the friendliest and most common assumption. Real HF and VHF noise is not that clean: it is bursty, non-Gaussian, full of structured impulse noise from switching power supplies and structured co-channel interference from other signals. A model trained on real recorded band noise can, in principle, exploit that structure in ways a fixed code cannot, buying additional effective SNR from the noise's own statistical shape rather than from more bandwidth, more time, or a better antenna. Whether that gain generalizes reliably band to band and season to season, and whether "the operator" in such a system is still meaningfully doing the copying, are open engineering and cultural questions this book cannot yet answer — only note.

> **Worked example:** FT8's oft-quoted decode threshold sounds almost implausible on first hearing: roughly −20 to −24 dB SNR in a 2500 Hz reference bandwidth, a signal supposedly 20 dB below the noise. Converting that reference-bandwidth figure to the SNR actually present within FT8's own occupied bandwidth shows where the apparent "magic" really comes from. FT8 occupies about 50 Hz — eight tones spaced 6.25 Hz apart. Relating SNR in one bandwidth to SNR in another (same noise power spectral density, same signal power) adds $10\log_{10}(B_1/B_2)$ in decibels when narrowing from $B_1$ to $B_2$: $10\log_{10}(2500/50) = 10\log_{10}(50) \approx 17$ dB. Applied to the headline threshold, $-20\text{ dB} + 17\text{ dB} = -3\text{ dB}$ — the true per-tone SNR at FT8's own decode threshold is closer to −3 dB, not −20 dB. That −3 dB is still a genuinely weak signal, one no ear could copy, but it is a believable number rather than an impossible one. The remaining apparent gain — the reason −3 dB in 50 Hz becomes a clean decode at all — comes from exactly the LDPC coding gain and the ~12.6 seconds of coherent symbol integration per transmission described above: not a violation of the physics this book has taught since Chapter 1's discussion of Q and selectivity, but its furthest extension yet. A high-Q 1920s regenerative receiver narrowed its passband to raise SNR by brute selectivity; FT8 narrows its *effective* passband in software and then integrates coherently in time, and the arithmetic is the same 10log₁₀(bandwidth ratio) relation either way.

### The State of the Art in 2026

A well-equipped station built in 2026 looks less like a single radio than a small integrated software system with a few pieces of RF hardware hanging off it. At its center is very often a single SDR-based transceiver covering HF through microwave, its "front panel" a touchscreen or a laptop displaying a live FFT waterfall rather than a mechanical dial — the direct descendant of the FlexRadio SDR-1000 and the RTL-SDR dongles that made wideband digitized I/Q commonplace across the 2000s and 2010s. That radio typically runs WSJT-X or one of its derivatives around the clock on a weak-signal HF band, decoding a screenful of simultaneous FT8 exchanges in each 15-second cycle without an operator needing to touch a tuning knob at all. On the shelf beside it, a growing number of stations now run a small dish and a repurposed satellite-TV low-noise downconverter feeding a second, narrower-band SDR pointed permanently at 25.9°E — a QO-100 ground station, drawing continuous wide-area access that would have been unimaginable to an OSCAR 7 operator riding a ten-minute pass. On the roof, alongside the HF wire and the VHF/UHF vertical, an increasing number of hams — particularly those active in emergency-communications groups — run a small weatherproofed AREDN or similar mesh node, quietly relaying IP traffic to the next node over without needing any commercial internet service at all. A digital-voice hotspot bridges a handheld radio's DMR, D-STAR, or System Fusion traffic to a worldwide reflector network over ordinary broadband, and increasingly an open, non-proprietary alternative such as M17 competes for the same role without licensing a commercial vocoder chip. None of these pieces requires a soldering iron to assemble; nearly all of it is purchased, downloaded, or 3D-printed and configured through a web interface. What has not changed since the very first Handbook chapters of this book is the underlying discipline: a licensed amateur, operating within an allocated band, extending the reach of a signal as far as the physics, the regulation, and the available technology of the year will allow.

***

Point a dish at 25.9° east longitude on a clear night in 2026 and the geostationary transponder sitting there has not moved, and will not move, relative to the ground beneath it — the same patch of sky it occupied at sunset will still hold it at dawn. Somewhere within its enormous, unmoving footprint a receiver is running a model it was not hand-tuned to run, trained on noise recorded from a band its designer may never have operated, quietly deciding what in the waterfall is signal and what is not. Whether the next ten decibels of reach come from a better antenna, a smarter mesh, a satellite that never sets, or a machine that has simply learned to listen better than any of the operators training it — that argument is still open, on the air, tonight.
