## Prologue — Before the Amateurs (1864–1900)

*"We have strong reason to conclude that light itself—including radiant heat and other radiation, if any—is an electromagnetic disturbance in the form of waves propagated through the electromagnetic field according to electromagnetic laws."*
*— James Clerk Maxwell, "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field" (1864/1865)*

### The Story

There is no radio industry in this chapter. There is no amateur, no callsign, no band plan, no government agency with an opinion about the ether. There is only a Scottish physicist doing algebra with a set of equations that other men had written down piecemeal — Coulomb's law of electric attraction, Ampère's law of the magnetic field around a current, Faraday's law of the voltage induced by a changing field — and noticing that if you added one small correction of his own, the equations stopped describing a set of separate phenomena and started describing a single, self-propagating disturbance.

**FACT:** Maxwell presents "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field" to the Royal Society (published *Phil. Trans.* 1865); first unified field theory predicting EM wave propagation at the speed of light.

James Clerk Maxwell did not build anything. He did not even claim to have discovered a new force. What he showed, working through the mathematics of electric and magnetic fields in the early 1860s, was that a changing electric field creates a magnetic field exactly as a changing magnetic field creates an electric field — a mutual, self-sustaining loop that needs no wire, no medium, no conductor at all to keep itself going. Run the arithmetic on how fast such a disturbance would have to travel, using nothing but the electrical and magnetic constants that laboratory experiments of the day had already measured, and the number that falls out is the speed of light. Maxwell's conclusion — audacious for 1864, still faintly audacious now — was that light itself is nothing but this same electromagnetic disturbance, just at a much higher frequency than anyone had thought to look for. Sound, heat, visible light, and (though nobody could yet produce or detect one) some undiscovered family of much longer, much lower-frequency electromagnetic waves were, in this view, all the same phenomenon wearing different wavelengths.

**FACT:** Maxwell publishes *A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism*, the complete statement of Maxwell's equations.

It took Maxwell until 1873 to publish the complete, textbook-ready statement of what are now simply called Maxwell's equations — four coupled relationships that remain, unaltered, the working description of every antenna, every waveguide, and every radio wave in this book. But a prediction is not a demonstration. For more than twenty years after 1864, Maxwell's electromagnetic waves existed only on paper. No one had built a source. No one had built a detector. No one, strictly, knew whether the mathematics described anything that actually happened in a laboratory rather than merely being an elegant way to reorganize equations everyone already had.

That changed at the Karlsruhe Polytechnic in Germany, where a young physics professor named Heinrich Hertz set out, deliberately, to manufacture Maxwell's invisible waves and catch them in the act.

**FACT:** Hertz, at Karlsruhe, generates and detects EM waves with a spark-excited dipole and resonant loop; demonstrates reflection, refraction, polarization, finite propagation speed.

Hertz built a transmitter — two straight brass rods in a line, each ending in a metal sphere, with a small spark gap where the rods nearly met in the middle, the whole assembly driven by a high-voltage induction coil. And he built a receiver: nothing more than a loop of wire, open at one point with its own tiny adjustable gap, sized so that its own natural electrical rhythm matched the transmitter's. When he fired the transmitter's spark gap across a room, a second, much fainter spark flickered across the gap in the receiving loop, with no wire connecting the two. Over 1887 and 1888, Hertz went well beyond that single demonstration: he showed the waves could be reflected off a metal sheet like light off a mirror, refracted through a large prism of pitch, and polarized by passing them through a wire grid — and by comparing the wave pattern in open air against the pattern along a wire, he calculated their speed and found it matched light's. Every property Maxwell's mathematics predicted, Hertz went out and found sitting in a lecture hall in Karlsruhe. The waves were real, they behaved exactly like light, and they could be made and caught with apparatus a determined experimenter could build on a bench.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** Hertz's demonstration did not stay a German curiosity. Within a decade, physicists on three continents pushed the same discovery in different directions. In England, Oliver Lodge lectured on Hertzian waves and coherer detection at the Royal Institution in 1894, coming tantalizingly close to signaling before setting the work aside. In Calcutta, Jagadish Chandra Bose built his own apparatus and gave public demonstrations in 1895 of wireless signals ringing a bell and firing a charge across a lecture hall, work he never patented. In St. Petersburg, Alexander Popov built a coherer-based lightning detector for the Russian navy in 1895 and was sending Morse messages between buildings within a year. None of these men built a company. The recognition that Hertz's trick could carry a message was arriving simultaneously across Europe and Asia — the surest sign a discovery's time has come, and a reminder that the amateur story ahead, however American its regulatory spine, was never a one-nation invention.

The man who did build a company out of Hertz's demonstration was a twenty-year-old with money, family connections on two continents, and a stubborn conviction that a physics-lab trick was actually a business. Guglielmo Marconi, son of an Italian father and an Irish mother, had been tinkering with batteries and coils at his family's estate outside Bologna when he read about Hertz's work. Where university physicists treated the spark-and-loop apparatus as a closed chapter, Marconi saw an open one: if a spark here could twitch a loop over there with no wire between them, then in principle it could twitch a loop across a city, a valley, or an ocean. He was not the first to think this. He was the first to treat it as an engineering program rather than a physics demonstration, and to keep pushing range and reliability long after the men who had done the fundamental physics moved on.

At his family's villa at Pontecchio, starting in 1894, Marconi combined a Hertzian spark oscillator with a far more sensitive receiving detector than Hertz's own spark-gap loop: the coherer, a small glass tube of loose metal filings that changed resistance under radio-frequency energy, which the French physicist Édouard Branly had described a few years earlier. Marconi did not invent the coherer. He wired it to a relay and a battery so that, instead of squinting for a faint spark, an operator could read a signal from a bell or a telegraph sounder — a loud, mechanical output that mattered outside a physics lab.

**FACT:** Marconi adds a grounded antenna and extends range to ~1.5 mi (~2–2.4 km), including over an intervening hill.

His early apparatus, following Hertz's design closely, used two matched conductors and reached only a few dozen yards. In the summer of 1895 Marconi made the single modification that turned a laboratory curiosity into a communications system: he grounded one side of his transmitter to a buried metal plate and raised the other side on a tall wire. Letting the earth itself serve as the return conductor, rather than balancing two matched rods, roughly tenfolded his range — past a mile, and reportedly over an intervening hill that should have blocked a line-of-sight signal. Marconi had no language yet to explain why the grounded, single-wire configuration worked so much better than the symmetric dipole; he simply noticed that it did, and kept it. Every ground-mounted amateur vertical built in the century that followed descends from that afternoon's modification.

The Italian government showed no interest. The British Post Office's chief engineer, William Preece, did, and by 1896 Marconi had crossed to England, where Preece gave him laboratory space, funding, and an audience of engineers and journalists for a string of increasingly ambitious demonstrations. A British patent followed that year; a company, later Marconi's Wireless Telegraph Company, in 1897. By 1899 his apparatus was reliable enough to bridge the English Channel, and to cross to New York, where reporters covering the America's Cup yacht races used Marconi-built links to relay race results to shore faster than any boat could carry them. None of this required a license, a callsign, or an act of Congress, because none yet existed — only capital, mechanical skill, and a willingness to keep pushing a physics demonstration toward an engineering product long after the physicists had stopped looking at it.

**FACT:** Marconi's *claimed* first transatlantic reception (Morse "S"), Poldhu → Signal Hill, St. John's (~3,500 km). Traditionally cited; disputed then and since.

The climax of this opening act, and the traditional bookend of the pre-regulatory period, came on 12 December 1901. Marconi, at a receiving station on Signal Hill overlooking St. John's, Newfoundland, reported hearing three faint clicks — the Morse letter S — sent from a high-powered station he had built at Poldhu, on the Cornish coast of England, roughly 3,500 kilometers away. No independent witness heard the signal alongside him, and no paper record survived beyond his own notes. Skeptics at the time, and later historians armed with a better understanding of the frequencies and power actually involved, have questioned whether Marconi heard a genuine signal or heard atmospheric static resolved by expectation into the pattern he was listening for. The claim was disputed the week it was made and has stayed disputed since; the first transatlantic contact later engineers accept without qualification came the following year, in far more carefully documented tests between Glace Bay, Nova Scotia, and Poldhu. But claimed or disputed, the December 1901 announcement did the work that mattered here: it convinced people on both sides of an ocean no wire had ever crossed that Hertz's spark-and-loop trick could span the planet with no wire at all.

That conviction did something no engineer had planned for. It sent a wave of teenagers and tinkerers — in Britain, the United States, and a dozen other countries — out to their attics and basements to build the same apparatus Marconi had, out of induction coils and coherers ordered from mail-order catalogs or scavenged from telegraph-repair scrap. Nothing stopped them. There was no license to apply for, no band to keep inside, no government office that had yet noticed the ether was a finite, shareable resource. Before there was a Radio Act, before there was an ARRL, there were just people with spark coils and an idea from a newspaper, discovering they too could throw a signal across a city with equipment built at a kitchen table. What they would eventually do to each other's signals, and what the law would do about it, is the next chapter's problem. This one belongs to the physics.

### How It Works

Every circuit this book describes — from a 1902 spark coil to a 2017 FT8 decoder running an LDPC error-correction algorithm on a laptop — is built from the same three ideas that appear for the first time in this chapter. Get comfortable with them here, because nothing later replaces them; later chapters only add complexity on top.

**The electromagnetic wave.** Maxwell's four coupled equations describe how electric and magnetic fields relate to charges, currents, and each other. Two matter most here: Faraday's law says a *changing* magnetic field creates a circulating electric field; Maxwell's own addition to Ampère's law — the piece that made his name — says a *changing* electric field creates a circulating magnetic field, even in empty space with no current flowing. Put those two effects together and a changing E field makes a changing H field, which makes a changing E field, indefinitely, each regenerating the next without any wire or medium to carry it. That self-sustaining relay race, propagating outward from wherever it started, is the electromagnetic wave — a transverse wave, in which E oscillates in one plane, H oscillates at right angles to E, and both are perpendicular to the direction of travel.

{{fig:p-emwave}}

The speed at which this E/H relay race propagates through free space is fixed by nature, independent of frequency, power, or anything else an operator controls. It is the speed of light, and it ties frequency and wavelength together in the single equation this whole book keeps coming back to:

$$c = f\lambda$$

Here $c$ is the speed of light (about $3\times10^8$ m/s as a working value; $299{,}792{,}458$ m/s exactly), $f$ is frequency in hertz — cycles per second — and $\lambda$ (lambda) is wavelength in meters, the distance the wave covers in one cycle. Because $c$ never changes, frequency and wavelength are strictly reciprocal: double the frequency and you halve the wavelength. This book uses the quick shortcut $\lambda(\text{m}) = 300 / f(\text{MHz})$, which falls directly out of $c = f\lambda$ once frequency is expressed in megahertz.

That conversion is not an abstraction. For most of amateur radio's first four decades, operators described where they worked not in kilohertz or megahertz but in meters — "80 meters," "20 meters," "200 meters" — and the regulatory history of the hobby, starting with the next chapter, turns on a specific wavelength limit written into a specific 1912 law. It is worth doing the conversion once, now, before that law appears.

> **Worked example:** What frequency corresponds to a wavelength of 200 meters? Using $f(\text{MHz}) = 300 / \lambda(\text{m})$: $f = 300 / 200 = 1.5$ MHz. Two hundred meters and 1,500 kHz are the same point on the spectrum, described two different ways. Remember that number — 1.5 MHz — because it is about to become a legal boundary line, and this book's title.

**Resonance and the LC tank circuit.** A wave has to be generated somewhere and picked out from the background elsewhere, and both jobs, in every era this book covers, are done with some version of the same two-component circuit: an inductor (a coil, symbol $L$, in henries) and a capacitor (two conductive plates separated by an insulator, symbol $C$, in farads), connected in a loop.

Charge a capacitor and connect it across an inductor, and the stored electric field doesn't just sit there. The capacitor discharges through the coil, and as current flows the inductor builds up its own magnetic field, storing the same energy in a different form. Once the capacitor is fully discharged, the collapsing magnetic field, by Faraday's law, drives current that recharges the capacitor with the opposite polarity — and the cycle reverses and repeats. Energy sloshes between the capacitor's electric field and the inductor's magnetic field exactly the way a pendulum's energy sloshes between potential and kinetic — and just as a pendulum's length sets how fast it swings regardless of how hard you push it, an LC circuit's component values set how fast it oscillates, regardless of how much energy you put in:

$$f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$

$f_0$ is the resonant frequency — the one rate the circuit naturally wants to ring at. Make $L$ or $C$ larger and it oscillates more slowly; make either smaller and it oscillates faster. This equation is the beating heart of every tuned circuit in this book: it is why a receiver has a dial, why an antenna has a length that matters, and why "tuning in" a station is a literal, physical act of adjusting $L$ or $C$ until a circuit's rhythm matches the wave you're trying to catch.

> **Worked example:** Take a coil of $L = 100\ \mu\text{H}$ ($100\times10^{-6}$ H) and a capacitor of $C = 100\ \text{pF}$ ($100\times10^{-12}$ F) — values well within reach of hand-wound wire and a simple parallel-plate capacitor. First, $LC = (100\times10^{-6})(100\times10^{-12}) = 1\times10^{-14}$. Take the square root: $\sqrt{LC} = 1\times10^{-7}$. Multiply by $2\pi$: $2\pi\sqrt{LC} \approx 6.28\times10^{-7}$. Invert it: $f_0 = 1 / 6.28\times10^{-7} \approx 1.59\times10^6$ Hz, or about 1.59 MHz — a frequency, and a wavelength near 189 meters by $\lambda = 300/f$, not far at all from the 200-meter line this chapter has already flagged as significant.

This is precisely the kind of circuit Hertz built, though he had no reason yet to call it an "LC tank" — that engineering language came later. His transmitting dipole was, electrically, an inductor (the straight rod, current flowing its full length) in series with a capacitor (the metal end-spheres and the gap, storing charge as any two separated conductors do). His receiving loop was the same kind of circuit at a different physical size, and therefore a different $L$, $C$, and $f_0$ — deliberately matched, as closely as 1887 workshop tools allowed, to the transmitter's frequency, so the receiver's own natural ring would be excited by the transmitter's wave and nothing else nearby.

**Why a spark radiates.** None of this explains why closing a spark gap should launch a wave at all, rather than just making light and noise. The answer follows from another consequence of Maxwell's equations: an accelerating or decelerating electric charge radiates electromagnetic energy. A steady current radiates essentially nothing — a battery and a straight wire, sitting quietly, produce no detectable radio wave. But a *changing* current, especially one that changes violently and quickly, radiates a great deal.

A spark gap produces exactly that kind of violent change. Charge builds up on the capacitor plates until the voltage across the gap ionizes the air and a conductive path forms. The stored charge doesn't leak away gently — it discharges explosively into the attached LC circuit, and because that circuit is resonant, it doesn't just dump once and stop. It rings: current sloshes back and forth at the circuit's natural frequency $f_0$, and every reversal is an acceleration of charge along the wire, and every acceleration radiates a small burst of wave. This is physically no different from striking a bell: the blow (the spark) excites the object's own resonant frequency (the LC circuit's $f_0$), and it rings at that frequency until its stored energy is used up — partly to resistive heating, and partly, crucially, radiated away as the wave a receiver picks up.

That "until its stored energy is used up" clause matters for everything that follows. A struck bell does not ring forever; it rings loudly and dies away. A spark-excited LC circuit does the same: each spark produces not a steady, continuous wave but a short burst that starts at full strength and decays — a *damped* wave, ringing down after each excitation rather than persisting. The transmitter must fire another spark for every dot and dash the operator sends. What that damping does to the *bandwidth* of a spark signal — and why it becomes a serious regulatory problem within twenty years — is a story for the next chapter. For now: a spark is not a switch that turns a wave on and off, it is a hammer blow that sets a tuned circuit briefly ringing.

**The coherer — a detector, but barely one.** Radiating a wave solves only half the problem; something at the far end has to notice it arrived. Hertz's own receiving loop, with its tiny visible spark, worked as a physics demonstration but not as a communications instrument — nobody was going to read Morse code by squinting at a spark in a darkened room. Branly's coherer, which Marconi adopted at Pontecchio, solved the sensitivity problem, though crudely, in a way later chapters spend real effort replacing.

A coherer is nothing more than a short glass tube, loosely packed with metal filings, with an electrode at each end. Left alone, the filings barely touch and the tube presents a high resistance — nearly an open circuit. When radio-frequency energy strikes the tube, the filings — for reasons not well understood at the time, likely tiny arcing and micro-welding at their contact points — cohere, clumping together, and the tube's resistance drops sharply, sometimes by orders of magnitude. Wire the coherer in series with a battery and a relay, and that drop is enough to close the relay and operate something loud — a telegraph sounder, a Morse inker, a bell.

But a coherer, once cohered, stays cohered — the filings do not spontaneously fall back apart. Left alone, it would register the first dot of a message and remain permanently "on," deaf to everything after. Every practical coherer receiver needed a second mechanism — a decoherer, typically a small electric tapper triggered by the same relay — to rap the tube and shake the filings apart the instant after each detection, resetting it for the next pulse. This defines the ceiling on what "receiving a radio message" could mean in 1895: a coherer offers no amplification (the energy operating the relay comes entirely from the local battery, not the received wave), no fidelity (its output is a single bit, present or absent), and no memory beyond the tap-reset cycle. It is a binary, on/off detector, and nothing more.

That binary ceiling is not a limitation of Marconi's particular apparatus. It is, at this point, the ceiling on radio itself. **There is no modulation yet.** Nobody in 1895, or 1900, or 1901, has any way to vary the *shape* of a radio wave to carry speech or music — only to switch a damped, spark-excited burst on and off, exactly as an operator's hand works a telegraph key. Every signal in this chapter is Morse code carried by on/off keying of a train of damped oscillations. Voice over the air, and the tubes that will eventually make it possible, are decades away.

**The grounded antenna.** One more piece of apparatus from this chapter earns a permanent place in every later one: Marconi's grounded, single-wire antenna. Hertz's dipole was symmetric — two matched rods radiating from the gap between them. Marconi broke that symmetry deliberately: one terminal went to a plate buried in the earth, the other up a mast as a single elevated wire. The earth became the return half of the circuit, and more of the radiating structure stood tall and clear rather than split between up and down — pushing more of the circuit's energy out as radiated wave rather than near-field loss. Marconi had no vocabulary yet for radiation resistance or effective height — that belongs to twentieth-century antenna theory — but he could measure range, and the grounded antenna simply worked better by a wide margin. Every ground-mounted vertical described later in this book descends from that one modification.

### The State of the Art in 1900

There is no anchor *Handbook* for this chapter — the ARRL will not exist for another fourteen years, and the first *Radio Amateur's Handbook* is still a quarter century away. What there is, by 1900, is a small, self-taught, unlicensed population of experimenters scattered across Britain, the United States, and continental Europe, working from newspaper accounts of Marconi's exploits, technical journals like *The Electrician* and *Scientific American*, and whatever apparatus they could buy, scavenge, or build.

A well-equipped experimenter's transmitting station in 1900 looked, functionally, like a scaled-down version of what Marconi had built five years earlier: an induction coil — a step-up transformer driven by an interrupted battery current, generating several thousand volts from a low-voltage cell — charging a bank of capacitance (often literal Leyden jars, though purpose-built capacitor plates were increasingly available) through a spark gap and into a tuned coil. The whole assembly connected, Marconi-fashion, to an elevated wire antenna on one side and an earth ground on the other. A telegraph key in the primary circuit let the operator interrupt and re-establish the coil's charging cycle, keying the spark train to spell Morse code. Range ran from a few hundred yards to a few miles, depending almost entirely on how much power the operator could afford and how high a mast the local landscape and budget allowed.

Receiving apparatus was, if anything, the more fragile link. A coherer, its decohering tapper, a local battery, and a relay driving a telegraph sounder made up the whole chain; sensitivity was poor, the reset cycle mechanically noisy and slow enough to blur fast sending, and false triggers from nearby machinery or even streetcar arcing were a constant nuisance. None of this stopped enthusiasts. Apparatus could be ordered by mail, assembled from salvaged telegraph and electrical-supply parts, or wound from scratch using instructions reprinted in journals on both sides of the Atlantic. There was no examination, no fee, no callsign to apply for, no band edge to respect, because none of those concepts existed yet for a private individual. A teenager with an afternoon, a few dollars of parts, and a magazine article had, by 1900, everything he legally needed to put a signal on the air.

***

Picture one of these attic tinkerers at his workbench: a spark coil in one hand, a coherer in the other, keying out Morse under no callsign because none has been assigned. Every oscillator he can build is, by the physics worked out in this chapter, a damped, low-Q ringing circuit — inherently broad, inherently sloppy, incapable of the narrow, selective signal a crowded spectrum will eventually demand. Commercial and naval wireless stations are growing more numerous and more powerful around him, on that same handful of usable wavelengths, with no rule yet dividing who transmits when, at what power, or how close to a receiver straining to hear something as urgent as a ship in distress. The amateurs who inherit these three ideas — the wave, the resonant circuit, and the spark that excites it — are about to find out exactly how much of the spectrum the law will let them keep, and how far below two hundred meters they will be pushed to find it.
