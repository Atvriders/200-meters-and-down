## 4. On the Eve of War (1937–1945)

*"56–60 MHz — five meters — where American amateurs made their first serious push into VHF territory in the years before Pearl Harbor."*
*— the historical record of US amateur VHF allocations, 1937–1941*

### The Story

By 1937 the well-equipped American amateur station had stopped looking like a hobbyist's improvisation and started looking like a small commercial installation. The superheterodyne had won its argument with the TRF receiver; AGC and the pentode had made that receiver quiet and sensitive; the Pierce crystal oscillator had made a transmitter's frequency a solved problem rather than a nightly wrestling match with a drifting LC tank. What remained was mostly a matter of scale — more power in, more selectivity on receive, more direction to the antenna overhead — and the closing years of the 1930s delivered all three, in tube types, filter circuits, and antenna designs that a ham of 1928 would barely have recognized on sight, let alone been able to duplicate from the parts bin of a decade earlier. The League marked the moment in its own way that September: the ARRL announced its DX Century Club, an award for working and confirming one hundred different countries, built from a "List of Countries" the League's technical staff had published earlier the same year. Confirming a hundred countries by 1937 was a serious undertaking, not a weekend's work, and the program instantly gave the era's most ambitious operators something concrete to chase across every band the treaty allocations of 1927 had granted them.

The tube that did the most to raise the era's power ceiling arrived from RCA a year earlier and became, almost immediately, the transmitting valve every home builder reached for. RCA introduced the 807 — a beam-power tetrode, a scaled-up transmitting cousin of the already-popular 6L6 receiving/audio tube — publicly in the November 1936 issue of *QST*, rated for use at frequencies up to 125 megacycles and priced within reach of an ordinary ham's budget in a way that larger transmitting tubes had never been. Hams "latched onto" it, as the trade press of the day put it, and by the end of the decade the 807 had become the default final amplifier tube in an enormous fraction of published amateur transmitter designs, civilian and — within a few years — military alike. It was not, by itself, a new circuit idea; it was simply a better, cheaper, more rugged vessel for an idea the era had already settled on: bias the amplifier stage into gross nonlinearity, run it hard, and let a tuned output network clean the result back into a usable radio-frequency sine wave. That idea has a name — the Class-C amplifier — and this chapter's *How It Works* section takes it apart in detail below, because understanding it is the key to understanding almost every transmitter this era built. The 807 did not have the field to itself — the 6L6 remained the default choice for a driver stage or a modest low-power final, and independent tube makers like Eimac and Taylor sold larger triodes and tetrodes, the 811s and 812s and their kin, to stations building all the way up toward the legal ceiling — but no other single tube type appeared in more published transmitter designs, amateur or, within a few years, military, than the 807.

The legal ceiling those transmitters worked against had been fixed for years and stayed fixed through this whole era: 1000 watts of DC input power to the final amplifier stage, a limit expressed not as what came out of the antenna but as what went into the tube — output was whatever the final's efficiency happened to deliver, and nobody's receiver could tell the difference between a station running the limit at 50 percent efficiency and one running it at 75. Getting licensed to run that power at all meant passing through one of three FCC operator classes then in force — Class A, Class B, and Class C, an alphabet with no relation to the Class-C amplifier circuit sharing its middle letter — Class A carrying full privileges and requiring an in-person examination at an FCC field office, Class C available to operators examined by mail and correspondingly more restricted, a stratification the Commission would eventually replace entirely, but not until well after this era's bands had gone dark and come back.

Power and selectivity were only half the story. The other half was frequency — specifically, how high a frequency an amateur transmitter could usefully reach — and here the era's central technical adventure was already underway. The 5-meter band, 56 to 64 MHz, had been opened domestically all the way back at the Third National Radio Conference in 1924, a decade before anyone had equipment worth putting on it, but by the mid-1930s a determined push into "five meters" — by this period generally worked in the 56–60 MHz segment — had become one of *QST*'s most energetic running stories, and no single person pushed harder than Ross A. Hull, the magazine's Australian-born technical editor. Hull treated VHF not as a curiosity but as the frontier the hobby most needed to open, filling *QST*'s pages through the mid-1930s with construction articles, propagation reports, and relentless encouragement aimed at getting more stations onto five meters and, eventually, onto the still higher and stranger territory above it. He did not live to see how far that push would go. On the evening of 13 September 1938, while working on an experimental television receiver, Hull came into contact with a 4,400-volt transformer and was killed instantly. The League's own obituary, printed weeks later, called the loss the world's, not merely amateur radio's — an unusually large claim for a house organ to make about one of its own editors, and a measure of how central Hull's advocacy had been to the VHF work already underway when he died.

That work did not stop. Behind Hull's enthusiasm sat a genuine engineering problem this chapter's technical section explains at length: ordinary receiving and transmitting tubes, built with the same long internal leads and generously spaced electrodes that served perfectly well at broadcast and even shortwave frequencies, began to fail at 56 MHz and above, their gain collapsing and their tuned circuits mistuning themselves as internal capacitances and electron transit times that had been negligible at lower frequencies suddenly were not. RCA's answer, the tiny "acorn" tube family introduced from 1935 — the 954 pentode, 955 triode, and 956 remote-cutoff pentode chief among them — shrank the tube itself down, minimizing internal lead length and electrode spacing to cut the parasitic capacitance and the electron transit time responsible for the collapse in gain, and acorn-tube front ends became standard equipment in the era's best VHF converters and in prewar receivers built expressly for the new territory, including sets from Hallicrafters and National. Five meters, by the end of the 1930s, was no longer a wasteland; it was a working band with its own specialized tube technology, its own construction culture, and — as later sections of this chapter explain — its own propagation rules, sharply different from the ionospheric skip that had made the shortwave bands famous a decade earlier.

While hams pushed toward 56 MHz from below, a separate and much better-funded VHF story was unfolding just above their reach, and it belonged almost entirely to one obsessive inventor rather than to the amateur community as a whole. Edwin Armstrong — already amateur radio's most consequential engineer twice over, for regeneration and for the superheterodyne — had spent the 1930s developing and defending wideband frequency modulation, a scheme for carrying information in a carrier's frequency swings rather than its amplitude. He had been granted four foundational FM patents simultaneously in December 1933 and had demonstrated the system publicly before the Institute of Radio Engineers in New York in November 1935, arguing that trading bandwidth for a dramatic reduction in static and interference was worth the cost. Through the second half of the decade Armstrong built an experimental FM transmitting station of his own, W2XMN, on a ridge at Alpine, New Jersey, broadcasting on 42.8 MHz beginning in 1938 and 1939 to prove wideband FM could deliver the fidelity and quiet he had promised. Regional broadcasters took notice — New England's Yankee Network had begun planning a chain of FM relay transmitters across Mounts Asnebumskit, Washington, and Mansfield as early as 1936–37 — and in May 1940 the FCC formally authorized a commercial FM broadcasting band of forty channels spanning 42 to 50 MHz, effective the first of January 1941. Through the whole of this chapter's period, though, FM remained overwhelmingly a broadcast and, as the next section of this story shows, a military and civil-defense technology. It was not yet something an ordinary radio amateur transmitted on the amateur bands — that would be a postwar development, and this book takes it up in its proper place.

> **Meanwhile, Worldwide:** American amateurs would not go off the air until December 1941, but their British counterparts had already been silent for more than two years by then. When the United Kingdom declared war on Germany in September 1939, the General Post Office cancelled the issuance of new amateur licenses outright and ordered every licensed transmitting amateur to surrender their equipment to the police — both to keep it out of the hands of a possible spy or saboteur and to make it available, in some cases, to the war effort itself. Amateur radio in Britain would not formally resume until 1946. Many of the Radio Society of Great Britain's own council members and members did not sit out the intervening years idle: a considerable number were recruited into MI8's Radio Security Service, whose mission was to intercept clandestine wireless transmissions — the exact skill a lifetime of chasing weak, hand-tuned signals through static had trained them for. And in February 1940, in a laboratory at the University of Birmingham, two physicists named John Randall and Harry Boot coaxed a first working cavity magnetron into producing over 400 watts at a wavelength of 9.8 centimeters — a wavelength no amateur transmitter of this era came anywhere near, and the seed of a wartime radar revolution that would, within a few years, hand the whole world's radio engineers a working vocabulary for microwaves that amateur radio would not seriously touch until long after the peace.

Back in the United States, the storm clouds over Europe reached into the amateur bands well before Congress declared anything. As 1940 wore on, with France fallen and Britain under air attack, the FCC began tightening amateur operating privileges in the name of a national security the country was not yet formally fighting for: hams were barred from making contact with foreign stations, closing off the international DX chasing DXCC had only just begun to formalize, and portable and mobile operation below 56 MHz — the very frequencies most useful for genuine long-distance work — was restricted as well, with narrow exceptions carved out for events like Field Day and for the amateur organizations already drilling for wartime emergency communications. None of it was the full shutdown that was still more than a year away, but it was a visible tightening of the screws, an unmistakable sign to any operator paying attention that the country's peacetime radio freedoms were being spent down in installments. A hobby that had spent the previous four years chasing DXCC countries across every ocean now found a growing fraction of its favorite correspondents legally off-limits, and a station owner who had built a portable rig specifically to operate away from home now needed to check, before ever keying the microphone, whether the frequency and the location were still permitted at all.

The installments ran out on a Sunday morning in December.

**FACT:** FCC suspends all US amateur operation "for the duration" (Order No. 87), with exceptions only for authorized national-defense operation.

By the best available estimate, something on the order of 60,000 Americans held amateur licenses when Japanese aircraft attacked Pearl Harbor on 7 December 1941. The very next day, the Commission's Order No. 87 took every one of those stations off the air at once, with no ambiguity and, unlike the FCC's mid-1940 restrictions, no meaningful exceptions beyond stations specifically authorized for wartime national-defense work. Antennas that had carried DX chases and relay traffic for the better part of a decade went slack. Transmitters were unplugged, in many cases packed away in closets and attics for the duration, exactly as an earlier generation had packed away its spark coils in 1917. A hobby that had spent twenty years rebuilding itself from that first wartime blackout into a technically sophisticated, internationally organized pursuit found itself, for the second time in its short history, entirely and abruptly silent.

Silence did not mean idleness. The same technical fluency that had made hams valuable to the Army Signal Corps and Navy in 1917 made them far more valuable in 1941: radio and radar were now central to how the war would be fought, not peripheral to it, and licensed amateurs enlisted or were drafted into roles — radio operator, radar technician, instructor, cryptologic intercept operator — that put military-grade VHF, UHF, and even the new centimetric radar technology the British had opened the door to at Birmingham directly into hands that had spent years building five-meter converters out of acorn tubes in a home workshop. The skills transferred in both directions: the hobby had trained a generation of technicians the military badly needed, and the military, in turn, was about to spend four years and a fortune the hobby could never have raised developing exactly the VHF, UHF, and FM technique that generation would bring home again.

The FCC and the League did not leave the country's amateur talent entirely without an outlet even while the amateur bands themselves stayed dark. In June 1942, at ARRL's urging, the Commission established a wartime substitute service built specifically around the frontier hams like Hull had spent the previous decade opening.

**FACT:** War Emergency Radio Service (WERS) established by the FCC at ARRL's urging; VHF-only FM on 112–116 MHz and 219–225 MHz, licensed to communities, not individuals.

WERS was a deliberately narrow instrument. It operated FM, not AM or CW — the same wideband frequency modulation Armstrong had spent a decade proving on broadcast frequencies, now put to a civil-defense purpose he had never designed it for — on VHF territory well above every prewar amateur allocation, in the neighborhood of what hams had informally called "two and a half meters." And its licenses went not to individual amateurs, the way every amateur license before and since had been issued, but to municipalities and civil-defense organizations, who then staffed their stations with whatever trained operators — many, though not all, prewar hams — they could muster for air-raid warden networks, emergency medical communication, and local civil-defense coordination. A typical WERS installation sat at a fire station, a police headquarters, or an air-raid control center, linked by FM to a scatter of portable and fixed stations around the community it served; equipment was whatever a licensed municipal operation could scrounge or build, since the manufacturers who had been selling amateurs' receivers and transmitters a year earlier were, by 1942, running their factories flat out on military contracts instead. It was a small, tightly bounded exception carved into an otherwise total wartime silence, and it kept a portion of the country's VHF-FM technical culture alive and even growing through years when every other amateur frequency sat unused.

The much larger technical story unfolding alongside WERS belonged to institutions no ham could touch directly, and it turned on the same basic idea that had briefly flickered across the Atlantic that February at Birmingham. Radar — radio detection and ranging — works by timing an echo: a transmitter fires a short pulse, and a receiver listens for the fraction of that pulse's energy reflected back by a distant aircraft, ship, or coastline, with the round-trip delay giving range directly through the same $c = f\lambda$ family of relationships that governs every other radio phenomenon in this book, since a radio pulse, like any electromagnetic wave, propagates at a fixed and known speed. Resolution — how finely two closely spaced targets could be told apart, and how tightly a radar's beam could be focused rather than sprayed broadly across the sky — scales with wavelength: a shorter wavelength lets a given antenna produce a narrower, more precisely aimed beam, and lets the same antenna shrink enough to fit on an aircraft or a ship's mast rather than requiring the sprawling arrays Britain's earlier Chain Home network had used. That was exactly the problem Randall and Boot's cavity magnetron solved, generating far more power at centimeter wavelengths than any prewar tube technology could manage, and GEC's Wembley laboratories spent the rest of the war turning that laboratory result into a sealed, mass-producible valve fielded by the thousand on both sides of the Atlantic. None of it was built with amateur radio in mind. All of it was building, in engineering terms amateur radio would not fully absorb for another decade or more, exactly the high-frequency tube and circuit technique — and exactly the cohort of technicians trained to use it — that a silent, patient hobby would eventually inherit.

### How It Works

**Amplifier classes, and why Class-C is the workhorse.** Every vacuum-tube RF amplifier operates by letting a small grid voltage control a much larger plate current, but *how much* of each drive cycle the tube is allowed to conduct — its "class" of operation — is a designer's choice, set by the DC bias voltage applied to the grid, and that choice trades efficiency against fidelity in a completely predictable way. Bias the tube to conduct through the tube's entire input cycle, 360 degrees of it, and the stage is Class A: the plate current never stops flowing, the output waveform is a faithful, undistorted copy of the input, and efficiency is poor, because the tube dissipates substantial power as heat even when it is amplifying nothing at all. Bias the tube instead to sit right at cutoff, so it conducts for almost exactly half of each cycle — roughly 180 degrees — and the stage is Class B, more efficient than Class A but no longer capable, by itself, of reproducing a full sine wave without help from a second, out-of-phase tube working the other half-cycle. Push the bias further still, past cutoff, so the tube conducts for *less* than half of each cycle — only during the brief interval when the input drive is near its peak and strong enough to overcome the extra bias holding the tube off — and the stage is Class C.

**FACT:** An amplifier biased beyond cutoff, conducting under half a cycle, offering high efficiency but only for constant-envelope modes.

{{fig:classc}}

Picture the grid drive as an ordinary sine wave and the bias point set well below the tube's normal cutoff voltage. For most of that sine wave's swing, the grid stays too negative for any plate current to flow at all — the tube is simply off. Only near the very peak of each positive half-cycle does the drive swing far enough to pull the grid above cutoff, and for that brief window the tube conducts hard, dumping a short, sharp pulse of plate current before cutting off again as the drive falls back. The result, plotted against time, is not a smooth sinusoidal plate current at all; it is a train of narrow current spikes, one per RF cycle, exactly as {{fig:classc}} shows. That pulse train is, by any ordinary measure, a badly distorted amplifier — and that distortion is precisely the point. Efficiency in any amplifier stage comes down to how much of the tube's dissipation is wasted as heat versus delivered as useful RF power, and heat is worst exactly when plate current and plate voltage are simultaneously large — current flowing while the tube is still dropping a lot of voltage across itself. A Class-C stage arranges, by design, for plate current to flow only in the brief moments when plate voltage has swung down near its minimum, so the tube is never simultaneously passing a lot of current and dropping a lot of voltage. Little instantaneous power is wasted inside the tube itself, and a well-designed Class-C stage can convert 60 to 80 percent or more of its DC input power into RF output — figures a Class A stage, conducting continuously regardless of drive level, cannot approach.

The catch is exactly what that narrow current-pulse waveform implies: a Class-C stage cannot faithfully reproduce an amplitude-varying signal, because its output bears almost no resemblance, cycle by cycle, to a clean sine wave at all — only the tuned tank on its output, ringing at the drive frequency between pulses like a bell struck once per cycle, restores something resembling a sine wave, and it restores it at essentially one fixed amplitude for a given drive level, indifferent to fine amplitude detail the pulses themselves don't preserve. That is no obstacle at all for a CW transmitter, which needs only to be on or off, full amplitude either way, precisely what Class-C delivers efficiently. It works for wideband FM for the identical reason: FM's information rides entirely in the carrier's frequency, not its amplitude, so an amplifier that mangles amplitude fidelity but preserves frequency perfectly is exactly what an FM transmitter's final stage wants. What Class-C by itself cannot do is amplify an already-amplitude-modulated AM signal without destroying the modulation — which is exactly why the previous chapter's *plate-modulated* transmitters put the audio into the DC supply feeding a Class-C final rather than trying to run modulated RF through the stage directly, letting the final stay in its efficient, nonlinear sweet spot while the amplitude information rode in on the one supply the Class-C stage's efficiency didn't touch.

> **Worked example:** Suppose a wartime-era 807 final runs at a DC plate voltage of $E = 750\ \text{V}$ and a DC plate current of $I = 0.30\ \text{A}$ — comfortably inside the era's 1000-watt input ceiling. The DC input power is simply
> $$P_{DC} = E \times I = 750 \times 0.30 = 225\ \text{W}$$
> At a representative Class-C efficiency of $\eta = 70\%$, the RF power actually delivered to the antenna feed system is
> $$P_{RF} = \eta \times P_{DC} = 0.70 \times 225 \approx 158\ \text{W}$$
> and the remaining $225 - 158 = 67\ \text{W}$ is dissipated as heat in the tube's plate — well within an 807's rated plate dissipation, and a very different, far more comfortable number than the roughly 110–135 W of dissipation the same input power would produce running the stage in inefficient Class A at 40–50 percent efficiency instead. The efficiency figure is not a rounding convenience; it is the entire reason a small, inexpensive tube like the 807 could deliver a genuinely useful signal at the legal power limit without melting.

One structural reason beam tetrodes like the 807 displaced triode finals so completely in this era's designs is worth pausing on, because it is the same idea the previous chapter's receiving pentode already exploited, simply moved to the transmitting side of the station. A power triode's plate and control grid face each other across a small but unavoidable interelectrode capacitance, and at radio frequencies enough of the amplified plate signal can feed back through that stray capacitance, in phase, to turn a Class-C amplifier into an unwanted oscillator — precisely the parasitic self-oscillation problem the previous chapter's neutralization circuits existed to cancel, typically with a small adjustable capacitor deliberately feeding an equal and opposite signal back from the opposite phase of the plate tank. A beam tetrode interposes a grounded screen grid between control grid and plate, exactly as a receiving pentode does, and that screen sharply reduces the same troublesome capacitance in a power-amplifier stage. Many 807-based Class-C finals ran with little or no neutralizing network at all — one more reason the tube displaced older triode-final designs so thoroughly, and one more example of a receiving-tube idea migrating, essentially unchanged, into the transmitting half of the circuit.

**The pi-network: matching the tube to the world outside it.** A Class-C tube's plate does not want to look directly into an antenna system. At the plate voltages and currents typical of this era's transmitters, the tube's own internal characteristics demand it be loaded with an impedance of several thousand ohms to deliver its rated power efficiently, while the antenna feed systems hams actually built — long-wire and dipole antennas fed with open-wire line, later coaxial-fed arrangements — typically presented something far lower, tens to a few hundred ohms. Earlier designs bridged that gap with a link-coupled tank: a separate, small coupling coil, loosely wound alongside the plate tank's main coil, picked off just enough energy by mutual induction to drive a feedline at a workable impedance. It worked, but it demanded an extra coil, extra tuning steps, and gave the builder only loose control over exactly how much impedance transformation was happening — plus, because the plate tank alone did comparatively little to attenuate the harmonic-rich pulse train Class-C operation inherently produces, link-coupled rigs were notorious sources of harmonic radiation and the interference problems that came with it.

**FACT:** A π-shaped LC output-matching network that matches a wide impedance range while filtering harmonics.

{{fig:pi-network}}

The pi-network solved both problems with one topology, and its name is a description, not a brand: drawn on a schematic, its shunt capacitor–series inductor–shunt capacitor arrangement traces the shape of the Greek letter π, as {{fig:pi-network}} shows. The first shunt capacitor, right at the tube's plate, resonates with the network's inductor to present the tube with the high load impedance its plate wants to see. The series inductor and the second shunt capacitor, at the network's antenna-side terminal, then transform that same resonant behavior down to the much lower impedance the feedline presents — all without a separate coupling coil, using components the builder already needed for basic tank resonance in the first place. Because the whole assembly is fundamentally a resonant structure built from the same $f_0 = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{LC})$ relationship that governs every tuned circuit in this book, it favors the fundamental frequency the operator has tuned it to and discriminates hard against the harmonics riding along in a Class-C stage's pulsed plate current — the pi-network is, among other things, a low-pass filter, and a considerably more effective one than a bare link-coupled tank had ever been. Operating one became a small ritual every ham of the era learned by feel: tune the input capacitor until the plate current *dips* — a sharp minimum that marks the tank resonating at the drive frequency — then bring up the output capacitor to *load* the stage, watching the plate current climb back up as power actually leaves the tube for the antenna, then touch up the dip again as loading shifts it slightly, back and forth until both settings agree. The loaded Q of the resulting network — how sharply, in the language of $Q = f_0/BW_{3dB}$, the circuit favors its tuned frequency over its neighbors — set a real design tradeoff: a network tuned for high loaded Q filtered harmonics more aggressively but tolerated a narrower range of antenna impedances and wasted more circulating power as resistive loss in the coil, while a lower-Q setting matched a wider range of real-world antennas at the cost of passing more harmonic energy through to the feedline. By the end of this era the pi-network, not the link-coupled tank, was what a well-built Handbook-derived transmitter's output stage looked like.

**Crystal filters: borrowing the quartz crystal's Q for selectivity, not just stability.** The previous chapter's Pierce oscillator had already put the quartz crystal's extraordinarily sharp mechanical resonance to work holding a transmitter precisely on frequency. This era extended the same underlying physical fact — an AT-cut quartz crystal can behave, electrically, like an LC resonator of astonishingly high Q, commonly in the thousands where an ordinary well-built LC tank might reach the low hundreds at best — to a different job entirely: sharpening a receiver's selectivity rather than a transmitter's stability.

**FACT:** A quartz-based bandpass filter in the IF strip giving very sharp, high-Q selectivity.

The typical circuit, appearing across the era's receiver designs, wired a single crystal into one arm of a bridge circuit inserted into the IF strip — commonly still in the 175–500 kHz range the previous decade had established as standard — with a small "phasing" capacitor in the opposing arm, adjustable by the operator, whose job was to cancel out the crystal holder's own unwanted parallel capacitance and, in doing so, let the operator sweep a sharp rejection notch to either side of the crystal's narrow passband. In practice this gave the operator two independent controls worth understanding separately: a *selectivity* setting, broadly widening or narrowing the passband the crystal defined, and a *phasing* control that placed a deep, tunable null exactly on top of a single interfering signal or heterodyne squeal without touching the wanted signal sitting just to one side of it. An ordinary two-stage, transformer-coupled IF strip of this period, without a crystal filter, typically behaved as a moderate-Q resonant system passing several kilohertz — comfortable for AM phone, where the modulation itself needs several kilohertz of room, but frustratingly wide for CW, where the entire information content is a single tone switched on and off. Add a well-adjusted crystal filter to the same IF strip and the effective passband could narrow to a few hundred hertz or less — not the raw crystal's full multi-thousand Q realized in the filter's practical loaded bandwidth, because the bridge circuit's own loading and the phasing adjustment traded some of that intrinsic sharpness away in exchange for a usable, tunable filter shape, but still a dramatic, night-and-day improvement over an uncomplicated IF strip. For a CW operator working a crowded band — and by this era, with tens of thousands of licensed stations sharing a handful of narrow allocations, every popular CW subband was crowded on a good evening — that narrowing was the difference between pulling one weak, precisely tuned signal cleanly out of the noise and simply not copying it at all through a wash of adjacent signals.

> **Worked example:** Put a number on the "several kilohertz versus a few hundred hertz" claim above using $Q = f_0/BW_{3dB}$. Take an IF of $f_0 = 450\ \text{kHz}$, inside the 175–500 kHz range this book's previous chapter established as standard, run through an ordinary two-stage transformer-coupled IF strip with a modest loaded Q of about 65. Rearranging for bandwidth gives
> $$BW_{3dB} = f_0/Q = 450{,}000/65 \approx 6{,}900\ \text{Hz}$$
> — a bit under 7 kHz, comfortable room for AM phone but frustrating for CW. Insert a crystal filter with a realistic *loaded* Q of around 2,000 — far below the raw crystal's intrinsic Q, which can run into the thousands or tens of thousands, but a fair figure once the bridge circuit's own loading and the phasing adjustment are accounted for — and the same relation gives
> $$BW_{3dB} = 450{,}000/2{,}000 = 225\ \text{Hz}$$
> a passband barely wider than a single hand-keyed CW note. That roughly thirtyfold narrowing, purchased with one quartz crystal and a phasing capacitor added to an IF strip that otherwise looked exactly like the previous decade's, is the entire technical case for the crystal filter, expressed in the same $Q = f_0/BW_{3dB}$ relationship this book has used since the spark era's low-Q tanks first explained why interference was inevitable at 200 meters.

**The Yagi–Uda beam: gain, directivity, and why VHF made it practical.** A simple half-wave dipole radiates roughly equally in every direction around its axis — useful when an operator has no idea where a correspondent might be, wasteful when the goal is concentrating limited transmitter power toward one particular direction, or rejecting an interfering signal arriving from another. The solution, invented over a decade earlier by Shintaro Uda and Hidetsugu Yagi at Tohoku Imperial University and first described in English in 1928, is a parasitic array — a directional beam built from parasitic reflector and director elements alongside a driven element: a single fed ("driven") element flanked by one or more unfed elements that pick up energy purely by mutual induction from the driven element's own field.

{{fig:yagi}}

The trick lies entirely in the parasitic elements' physical length. An element cut slightly *longer* than resonant behaves, to the RF field around it, as an inductive reactance, and the current induced in it lags the driven element's current in a way that, combined with its position roughly a fifth of a wavelength behind the driven element, reinforces the driven element's radiation in the forward direction while partially cancelling it to the rear — this element is the *reflector*. An element cut slightly *shorter* than resonant behaves as a capacitive reactance instead, inducing a current that leads rather than lags, and positioned in front of the driven element it reinforces the forward field still further while helping cancel the rearward one — this is the *director*, and a practical beam can carry more than one of them stacked along the boom for still tighter focus. No direct electrical connection joins reflector, driven element, and director; the entire directional effect comes from mutual coupling and the careful mistuning of two elements that never see the transmitter's power directly at all.

{{fig:yagi-pattern}}

The payoff of concentrating radiated energy this way is measured two ways, and period constructors cared about both. *Gain* compares the field strength a beam delivers in its favored direction against what a plain dipole, fed with the same power, would deliver in its best direction — a modest three-element Yagi of this era's construction typically delivered several decibels of gain over a dipole, meaningful when every decibel came at the cost of building and aiming a rotatable array rather than simply stringing more wire. *Front-to-back ratio* — how many decibels weaker the array's rearward lobe is compared to its forward lobe, exactly what {{fig:yagi-pattern}}'s large forward lobe and small rear lobe illustrate — mattered just as much operationally, because a beam that suppressed an unwanted signal or interference source arriving from behind was often as valuable as one that boosted a wanted signal ahead.

> **Worked example:** Why did the Yagi–Uda beam become a practical amateur project on five meters years before anyone seriously built a rotatable multi-element array on 80 or 40 meters? The answer is pure arithmetic on element size. Using $\lambda(m) = 300/f(\text{MHz})$ at the heart of the 5-meter band, $f = 56\ \text{MHz}$ gives
> $$\lambda = 300/56 \approx 5.36\ \text{m} \approx 17.6\ \text{ft}$$
> so a half-wave driven element measures only about $\lambda/2 \approx 8.8\ \text{ft}$ — short enough, together with a reflector and director each only a little longer or shorter, to fit comfortably on a boom a determined ham could mount on a mast and, with a hand-cranked rotator, actually turn toward a correspondent. Run the same calculation at 3.5 MHz, deep in the 80-meter band the previous two chapters have already covered, and $\lambda = 300/3.5 \approx 85.7\ \text{m}$, putting a single half-wave element alone at roughly 141 feet — before any thought is given to reflectors, directors, or a structure capable of rotating the whole assembly. A five-meter Yagi was a weekend project for an ambitious ham with a stepladder; an 80-meter Yagi, at this era's state of the art, was simply not attempted at all. VHF's small wavelengths, so often described purely as a limitation on range, were exactly what made directional gain antennas practical amateur projects for the first time.

**HF skip versus VHF line-of-sight: two different atmospheres.** Chapter 2 established why shortwave signals travel so far: the ionosphere, a region of the upper atmosphere ionized chiefly by solar ultraviolet radiation, refracts HF radio waves enough to bend them back toward the earth well beyond the horizon, letting a modest station work correspondents thousands of miles away by sky wave. That refraction is frequency-dependent, and it does not extend indefinitely upward in frequency. As frequency rises through the HF range and into the VHF territory this chapter's hams were just beginning to explore, the same ionospheric layers that reliably return an 80- or 40-meter signal to earth become, under normal conditions, essentially transparent — a 56 MHz signal aimed upward typically passes straight through the ionosphere and out into space rather than bending back down. (Prewar operators occasionally logged startling exceptions — unusually intense, patchy ionization, later understood and named Sporadic-E, could on rare days support brief VHF skip contacts hundreds of miles beyond anything line-of-sight theory predicted — but through this era those openings were treated as freak occurrences to be enjoyed and puzzled over, not as a reliable propagation mode a station could plan around; the systematic theory of how the troposphere itself, not the ionosphere, sometimes extends VHF range would not be worked out until well after this book's next chapter begins.)

What actually governed a five-meter or 112 MHz contact in this era, then, was something close to ordinary optical line of sight, with one small but useful correction: the lower atmosphere's density gradient bends radio waves very slightly downward as they travel, following the curvature of the earth just a bit more than a straight optical ray would, an effect conventionally captured by treating the earth as if its radius were four-thirds its actual value. Folding that correction into a simple, well-established engineering estimate gives the distance to an individual station's radio horizon as
$$d \approx \sqrt{2h}$$
with $d$ in miles and $h$ the antenna's height above the surrounding terrain in feet. Two stations working each other share the sum of their two horizons, so a modest home antenna at 50 feet, paired with a correspondent's similarly modest 30-foot mast, could expect a maximum working distance on the order of $\sqrt{2\times50} + \sqrt{2\times30} \approx 10.0 + 7.7 \approx 17.7$ miles under ordinary conditions — a genuinely different game from an HF station's routine coast-to-coast and transoceanic sky-wave contacts, and the direct physical reason VHF operators of this era prized height, and prized a Yagi's concentrated forward gain, far more than raw transmitter power. A five-meter station could not out-shout the curvature of the earth. It could only climb higher, aim better, and wait for the rare, unexplained night the ionosphere itself decided to cooperate.

The difference between the two atmospheres was not only where a signal could travel; it was also how quiet the receiver sounded getting there. Atmospheric noise — the crackle and crash of distant lightning, itself propagated to the receiver by the same ionospheric mechanism that carries the wanted skip signal — dominates the HF noise floor and falls off sharply as frequency climbs; by the time a receiver is tuned to 56 MHz, most of that noise has vanished, and what remains is chiefly the noise generated inside the receiver's own front-end tube, a quieter and more consistent floor than anything an 80-meter operator ever heard on a static-crashing summer evening. Five-meter and two-and-a-half-meter work, when the geometry cooperated, had a hush to it that shortwave never offered — one more reason the era's VHF pioneers described the experience less as a weaker version of HF operating and more as an entirely different kind of listening, on an entirely different piece of atmosphere.

### The State of the Art in 1940

The 17th edition of *The Radio Amateur's Handbook*, published in November 1939 and dated 1940, and its 18th edition a year later, describe a hobby at a genuine technical peak — the last peak before an order from Washington shut the whole enterprise down for the duration. A well-equipped station built from either edition's pages centered on a crystal-controlled MOPA transmitter, its stable low-power oscillator stage isolated from a Class-C final amplifier — an 807, by this point in the era's most popular single choice — running up near the legal 1000-watt DC input ceiling and feeding the antenna through a pi-network output tank rather than the link-coupled arrangements the previous decade had favored, the network doing double duty as both matching network and harmonic filter in a hobby increasingly conscious of television-interference and neighbor complaints. On receive, a superheterodyne with a well-designed IF strip — commonly an outboard-coil design like National's HRO, prized for its precise dial calibration and, in its better configurations, an optional crystal filter for CW work — let an operator dig a weak, carefully tuned signal out of a crowded CW subband with a selectivity no simple LC IF stage could match. Antennas ranged from the era's familiar center-fed and end-fed HF wire arrangements up to, for the more adventurous, a homebrew three-element Yagi mounted on a mast the builder could turn by hand or with a simple rotator, aimed across a suburban rooftop toward whatever direction the evening's five-meter activity seemed to be coming from.

The receiver market by 1940 had grown sophisticated enough to support real competition built specifically around the selectivity problem the crystal filter solved. Hallicrafters announced its SX-28 "Super Skyrider" in July 1940, the product of a dozen staff engineers working through more than six hundred field reports gathered from amateur, commercial, and government users, and advertised a new, highly efficient crystal-filter circuit as one of the set's headline features the following month — direct evidence that by this point in the era, selectivity, not raw sensitivity, was what a serious operator shopped for. National's HRO, already five years old by 1940 but still widely regarded as the benchmark instrument, held its own against the newer competition on the strength of its precision-geared tuning dial and its own crystal-filter option, and receivers from both companies, alongside smaller makers, filled the advertising pages of the 17th and 18th edition Handbooks next to the era's now-standard construction articles for building an equivalent superheterodyne from scratch, crystal filter included, for a builder unwilling or unable to buy one ready-made.

Getting on the air still meant sitting for one of the FCC's Class A, B, or C examinations, and the DX Century Club, not yet three years old, gave the era's best-equipped and most patient operators a concrete, internationally minded goal to chase across the treaty bands of 1927 — 80, 40, 20, and 10 meters — while a smaller, technically adventurous minority split their attention toward 56 MHz and the acorn-tube converters and Yagi beams that band demanded. The League's own calendar reflected both halves of that split personality: alongside the DX chasing and traffic handling that had defined amateur radio since Hiram Percy Maxim's day, an annual Field Day exercise, already an established tradition by this point, sent clubs and individuals out to operate from tents and truck beds on emergency power, a peacetime rehearsal for exactly the kind of improvised, portable, public-service operating that WERS would soon put to a very different purpose. Roughly 60,000 Americans held amateur licenses by the time this peak was reached, a population built up over two decades of steady growth from the Depression-era counts the previous chapter recorded, operating under a license structure, a power ceiling, and a set of core circuit techniques — Class-C, the pi-network, the crystal filter, the superheterodyne beneath it all — that would, in their essentials, still be recognizable in an amateur station built a generation later. It was, by any technical measure, the mature, confident state of the art the 1930s had been building toward since the superheterodyne first displaced the TRF receiver. It had perhaps two years left to run.

***

The order that ended it arrived by teletype and by radio bulletin on 8 December 1941, and it did not distinguish between a beginner's single-tube regenerative receiver and the most elaborate crystal-filtered, pi-network-equipped station in the country: every one of them went silent at once, and stayed silent for the duration. What replaced amateur radio on the nation's VHF spectrum for the next three years was not amateur radio at all but WERS — FM, municipally licensed, narrowly bounded — a single thread of the prewar VHF culture Ross Hull had championed, kept deliberately alive inside a wartime shell built for civil defense rather than for DX chasing or contest scores. Around that narrow thread ran a far larger and better-funded version of the same VHF, UHF, and FM technique amateur radio had only begun to taste before the order came down — built and fielded, this time, by the hundred thousand, for a military that needed it in every theater, and taught to a generation of servicemen, many of them prewar hams, many more not.

Somewhere in a padlocked hamshack, an 807 sat cold in its socket beside a pi-network coil nobody had touched since the first week of December 1941. It would sit there through 1942, through 1943, through 1944 — through magnetrons and centimetric radar and hundreds of thousands of military VHF and FM sets built for purposes the prewar hobby had never imagined — until an order from the same Commission that had silenced it said otherwise.
