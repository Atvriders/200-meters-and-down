## Ch5 — Postwar, 1945–1960

### (a) Dated milestones, people, laws

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **Dec 16, 1947** | John Bardeen and Walter Brattain achieve the first working point-contact transistor at Bell Telephone Laboratories, Murray Hill, NJ, under William Shockley's group; germanium slab, two closely spaced gold-foil contacts, ~100× current gain. Publicly announced/demonstrated to lab management Dec 23, 1947; Nobel Prize in Physics awarded to Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley in 1956. |
| **June 1948** | Don Norgaard, W2KUJ (General Electric engineer), publishes "A New Approach to Single Sideband" in *QST*, popularizing the **phasing method** for amateur SSB generation. (The phasing method itself was patented by Ralph Hartley of Western Electric in 1928; Norgaard's contribution was making it practical/cheap for hams.) |
| **July 1948 – March 1954** | *QST* runs Byron Goodman W1DX's monthly column "On the Air with Single Sideband," driving grassroots SSB adoption — the "sideband wars" era in which AM diehards and SSB converts publicly sparred in *QST* letters and on-air. |
| **By April 1950** | *QST* reports hams using the phasing method outnumber filter-method users roughly 2:1, largely because crystal/mechanical filters were far more expensive than phasing hardware. |
| **Nov 1950** | Norgaard's design reaches the market as the "SSB, Jr." — a commercially produced phasing-type sideband transmitter. |
| **By April 1953** | At least ~300 active SSB stations on the air in the US (per ARRL). |
| **June–Aug 1953** | *QST* runs a three-part series by Walter Van B. Roberts, W2CHO, "Magnetostriction Devices and Mechanical Filters for Radio Frequencies" — key vehicle for popularizing the Collins-style **mechanical filter method**. |
| **July 1, 1951** | FCC amateur license restructuring takes effect: old Class A/B/C system replaced by six classes — **Novice, Technician, Conditional, General, Advanced, Amateur Extra**. Novice and Technician licenses are issued for the first time on this date. Novice: 1-year, non-renewable, 5 wpm code + written exam, distinctive 2×3 "WN" call signs. Technician: 5-year license, full privileges above 220 MHz (some sources: full General privileges on 50 MHz + all bands above 220 MHz; on 2m originally limited to 145–147 MHz). General = renamed Class B (13 wpm). Conditional = renamed Class C (mail-in exam eligibility). Advanced = renamed Class A. Amateur Extra = new top class, 20 wpm + 2 years holding a lower license. |
| **Early 1953** | FCC reverses part of the plan: full amateur (HF) privileges restored to all holders of General, Conditional, Advanced, and Extra licenses, softening the incentive-style stratification the 1951 order had begun to introduce. (This 1953 walk-back is a precursor to the more famous, more contentious 1968 Incentive Licensing fight — see Ch6.) |
| **1955** | Collins Radio releases the 75A-4 receiver / KWS-1 transmitter pair, engineered around SSB as well as AM/CW. |
| **1957** | Collins Radio ships the **KWM-1**, generally credited as the first purpose-built amateur SSB/CW **transceiver** (combined transmitter+receiver sharing frequency-control and filter hardware) — 14–30 MHz in 100 kHz segments, ~100 W PEP output, VOX. Same year, SSB is formally adopted by USAF Strategic Air Command for B-52 HF communications, underscoring SSB's bandwidth/power efficiency over AM for military long-haul use. |

### (b) Core technology facts

- **SSB generation, filter method vs. phasing method.** Both methods derive single-sideband, suppressed-carrier signal from a balanced modulator; they differ in how the unwanted sideband is removed.
  - *Filter method*: a balanced modulator produces a double-sideband suppressed-carrier (DSBSC) signal at a fixed low IF; a sharp **bandpass filter** (crystal lattice or Collins-style mechanical/magnetostrictive filter, typically a few kHz wide centered near 455 kHz–9 MHz IF) removes one sideband. Requires an expensive, precision filter but gives excellent, consistent sideband suppression (typically 40–60 dB) independent of audio frequency.
  - *Phasing method*: two balanced modulators fed with RF carrier and audio, each shifted 90° in phase (audio via an RC/LC phase-shift network, RF via a quadrature splitter); summing/subtracting the two modulator outputs cancels one sideband by phase interference rather than filtering. Cheaper (no crystal filter) but sideband suppression depends on how precisely the 90° phase relationships are held across the whole audio band — practical suppression typically only 20–30 dB without careful trimming, which is why the filter method eventually displaced phasing rigs as parts costs fell.
- **Balanced modulator.** A circuit (commonly two diodes or two tubes/transistors in push-pull, i.e. a "balanced" or "ring" modulator) that multiplies RF carrier and audio so that the output contains the two sidebands (sum and difference frequencies) but cancels out the carrier itself by symmetry — the basis of DSBSC generation for both SSB methods above.
- **Product detector.** The SSB/CW receive-side counterpart to the balanced modulator: it mixes the incoming suppressed-carrier IF signal with a locally generated carrier (the beat-frequency oscillator, BFO) to recover the original audio, as opposed to the simple envelope (diode) detector used for AM, which cannot properly demodulate a carrier-suppressed signal.
- **BJT common-emitter amplifier.** The workhorse small-signal RF/audio amplifier topology enabled by the 1947 Bell Labs transistor and its 1950s commercialization (junction transistors superseding the original point-contact type by the mid-1950s): input applied base-emitter, output taken collector-emitter, emitter common to both loops — provides both voltage and current gain (moderate-to-high power gain), inverted output phase, moderate input impedance. Transistors' small size, low voltage/power operation, and lack of a heater made them attractive for amateur gear from the late 1950s on, though tube-based HF transmitters remained dominant for high-power stages well into the 1960s–70s because contemporary transistors couldn't yet handle comparable RF power/voltage.

### (b) Core technology facts

- **FM (frequency modulation) fundamentals.**
  - *Deviation*: the amount the carrier frequency swings above/below center in response to modulating audio. US amateur VHF/UHF FM traditionally used **±5 kHz peak deviation** ("wideband FM," on 20 kHz channel spacing); post-2013 "narrowbanding" pushed many bands to **±2.5 kHz** deviation on 12.5 kHz spacing, though the older wideband standard persisted informally on 2m simplex/repeater use for years.
  - *Carson's Rule*: estimates the occupied bandwidth of an FM signal containing ~98% of its power: **B ≈ 2(Δf + fm)**, where Δf = peak deviation and fm = highest modulating (audio) frequency. Example for standard ham wideband FM: Δf = 5 kHz, fm = 3 kHz (typical voice bandwidth) → B ≈ 2(5+3) = **16 kHz**, consistent with the historical 20 kHz VHF channel spacing that included guard band.
  - *Capture effect*: unlike AM, an FM receiver strongly favors the stronger of two co-channel signals — once one signal is roughly 2–3 dB stronger than another on the same frequency, the receiver output is almost entirely that stronger signal rather than a mix of both (a direct consequence of the limiter/discriminator stage stripping amplitude variations before frequency detection).
  - *Discriminator*: the FM demodulator stage that converts instantaneous frequency deviation back into an audio voltage — classically a Foster-Seeley or ratio detector (tuned LC circuits producing a phase-shifted signal that, when compared to the original, yields an amplitude proportional to frequency); later largely supplanted by PLL-based demodulators and, from the 1990s on, DSP.
- **Repeater architecture.** A repeater receives on one frequency and simultaneously retransmits (with a fixed, band-specific offset) on another, extending range for low-power mobile/handheld stations by relaying via a favorably sited (usually elevated) station. Standard **2m offset is ±600 kHz** (input 600 kHz below output, or vice versa, by band-plan convention — e.g., input 146.250 MHz / output 146.850 MHz). **CTCSS** (sub-audible tone, ~67–254 Hz range) riding on the transmitted audio is used as an access-control/anti-triggering scheme so a repeater only responds to signals carrying its designated tone.
- **Satellite transponders and Doppler.** Early amateur "linear transponders" (OSCAR 6/7 Mode A/B) retransmit a whole slice of uplink spectrum (tens of kHz wide) shifted to a different downlink band, allowing many simultaneous SSB/CW QSOs through one bird, unlike a single-channel FM repeater. Because LEO satellites move at orbital velocities on the order of ~7.5 km/s relative to a ground station (with the along-track component of that velocity — the "range rate" — reaching several km/s near horizon passes), operators must continuously retune for **Doppler shift**: Δf ≈ f₀·(v/c), where v is the instantaneous line-of-sight (range-rate) component of the satellite's velocity relative to the ground station and c is the speed of light. For a 2m uplink (~146 MHz) this can be on the order of several kHz of shift across a pass; the effect is proportionally larger on higher transponder frequencies (e.g., 70cm/435 MHz), which is why satellite operators of that era learned to "tune the downlink, not the uplink" during a pass.

## Ch7 — Microprocessor, 1977–1990

### (a) Dated milestones, people, laws

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **Late 1970s–1980s** | Synthesized (PLL-controlled), microprocessor-managed amateur transceivers become mainstream, replacing crystal-bank/VFO-only designs — this is a broad technology-diffusion trend rather than a single dated event; no single "first synthesized ham rig" date is well-attested across sources in this pass, so treat as a period generalization rather than a pin. |
| **1979** | **World Administrative Radio Conference (WARC-79)** creates new international amateur allocations at 30m (10.100–10.150 MHz), 17m, and 12m — the "WARC bands." |
| **Oct 28, 1982** | FCC formally authorizes US amateur use of the **30-meter band** (10.100–10.150 MHz), the first WARC-79 band actually opened to US hams — roughly 3 years after the international conference, reflecting the lag between an international allocation and domestic implementing rules. (17m and 12m access for US hams followed later in the 1980s; exact US-implementation dates for 17m/12m were not independently pinned in this research pass — **[UNVERIFIED]** for those two specific dates.) |
| **Sept 1978** | Peter Martinez, G3PLX (UK), develops **AMTOR** (Amateur Teleprinting Over Radio, derived from the commercial/maritime SITOR standard) and makes the first amateur AMTOR contact, with G3YYD on 2m, using a homemade Motorola 6800-based microcomputer running assembler code. |
| **1980** | FCC grants a Special Temporary Authority (STA) to four US hams — William Meyn K4PA, Charles Roettcher K3FLS, Marvin Leibowitz W3KET, Walter Kaelin KB6BT — to operate AMTOR, ahead of full US rule-making. |
| **Oct 1981** | A Tucson IEEE Computer Society chapter meeting produces the discussion that leads a week later to a small group founding what becomes **TAPR** (Tucson Amateur Packet Radio Corporation), aimed at building an affordable Terminal Node Controller (TNC) for hams. |
| **Nov 1983** | TAPR ships the first **TNC** in kit form, the **TNC-1** (soon followed by the far more widely deployed **TNC-2** design) — the hardware that made AX.25 packet radio practical and affordable for ordinary hams, catalyzing rapid adoption of packet BBSs and digipeater networks through the mid-to-late 1980s. |

### (b) Core technology facts

- **PLL frequency synthesis.** Replaces banks of crystals / manually tracked LC VFOs with a **phase-locked loop**: a voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO) generates the working RF frequency; a programmable frequency **divider** (÷N counter, often microprocessor-controlled) divides the VCO output down; a **phase detector** compares that divided signal against a stable reference (crystal) oscillator and generates an error voltage that steers the VCO until the two are phase-locked. Changing the divider ratio (N) under microprocessor control lets a single reference crystal generate any of thousands of precise, stable operating channels/frequencies — the technology that made frequency-agile, memory-channel, microprocessor-controlled ham radios (and later, computer-controlled/CAT-interface rigs) practical from the late 1970s onward.
- **AX.25 packet protocol.** An amateur-adapted derivative of the commercial/ITU **X.25** link-layer protocol, standardized for ham use in the early-1980s TAPR era.
  - *Framing*: HDLC-style frames (flag bytes, address field, control field, PID, information field, FCS).
  - *Addressing*: uses amateur **callsign + SSID** (Secondary Station Identifier, 0–15) in place of X.25's numeric network addresses, allowing one callsign to represent multiple logical stations/ports (e.g., MYCALL-1 for a BBS port, MYCALL-7 for a node).
  - *Error detection*: a 16-bit **CRC** (CRC-CCITT) appended as the Frame Check Sequence to detect bit errors.
  - *Error recovery*: **ARQ** (Automatic Repeat reQuest) — connected-mode AX.25 acknowledges received frames and retransmits unacknowledged ones, similar in spirit to AMTOR's ARQ but adapted to a packet/frame model rather than character-pair handshaking.
  - *Physical/modulation layer*: on VHF, packet almost universally used **1200-baud AFSK** compatible with the **Bell 202** modem standard (mark/space tones, commonly 1200 Hz/2200 Hz), sent over standard FM voice channels via a TNC; HF packet instead commonly used slower ~300-baud Bell-103-style AFSK suited to the narrower, noisier HF channel. **Digipeaters** (digital repeaters) relayed AX.25 frames hop-by-hop toward a destination using a repeater path listed in the frame's address field, extending range without needing a dedicated network infrastructure.
- **AMTOR** technically bridges Ch6/Ch7: it's a teleprinter (RTTY-descended) mode using 5-bit Baudot-derived characters with 4-of-7 error-detecting coding, run in either FEC (broadcast, forward-error-correction) mode or ARQ (two-station, listen-and-repeat) mode — the amateur adaptation of the maritime commercial SITOR standard, itself standardized by ITU-R Recommendation 476-1.

## Ch8 — Digital/SDR, 1991–Present

### (a) Dated milestones, people, laws

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **Feb 14, 1991** | FCC eliminates the Morse code requirement for the **Technician** class license — the "no-code Technician." (Technicians who had also passed the 5 wpm code test going forward were informally called "Technician Plus," retaining Novice HF privileges; the FCC stopped issuing new Tech Plus licenses in 2000, folding the distinction into plain Technician on renewal/modification.) |
| **Dec 1998** | Peter Martinez, **G3PLX** — the same engineer behind 1978's AMTOR — introduces **PSK31** to the amateur community. In 1999 he releases soundcard-based software, letting any ham with a PC and a sound card get on PSK31 without dedicated DSP hardware, driving rapid worldwide adoption. |
| **2001** | **D-STAR** published by the Japan Amateur Radio League (JARL), the result of a digital-voice/data research program funded by Japan's Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications starting in 1998 — the first digital-voice standard designed specifically for amateur radio. |
| **Apr 2004** | Icom begins shipping D-STAR-capable hardware, starting with the IC-2200H 2m mobile — first commercial D-STAR radio. |
| **2003** | **FlexRadio Systems** introduces the **SDR-1000**, generally credited as the first true, commercially available, fully software-defined amateur HF/VHF transceiver (all DSP/mode functions done in host-PC software via the open-source PowerSDR/GPL codebase; the hardware itself converts RF to/from an 11 kHz-bandwidth quadrature (I/Q) audio-frequency signal, with essentially no front-panel controls beyond power). |
| **2003 (WRC-03)** | **World Radiocommunication Conference 2003** amends ITU Radio Regulations Article 25 (specifically provision 25.5) to make Morse code proficiency testing **optional at each national administration's discretion** rather than an international treaty requirement — removing the international mandate that had underpinned Morse testing since early 20th-century radio regulation. |
| **Feb 23, 2007** | Following WRC-03 and subsequent petitions, the **FCC formally ends all Morse code testing** for US amateur licenses (Report and Order published in the Federal Register Jan 24, 2007, effective Feb 23, 2007); General and Amateur Extra candidates no longer need to demonstrate code proficiency, and existing Technicians are simultaneously granted the former "Technician Plus" HF privileges. |
| **~2010–2012** | **RTL-SDR** discovered/popularized: Eric Fry first notices (Mar 2010) that the Realtek RTL2832U chip used in cheap ($20) DVB-T USB TV-tuner dongles can output raw 8-bit I/Q baseband samples; Antti Palosaari independently confirms this (Feb 2012); Steve Markgraf and the Osmocom project release the first open-source `rtl-sdr` driver, turning a mass-market TV dongle into a general-purpose ~24 MHz–1.7 GHz wideband SDR receiver and massively lowering the cost of entry to SDR experimentation. |
| **1997** | **IRLP** (Internet Radio Linking Project) launched by Dave Cameron, VE7LTD, in Canada — first three nodes installed Nov 1997 — pioneering VoIP-based repeater linking over the internet ("keeping the radio in amateur radio" by requiring an actual RF link at each end). |
| **2002** | **EchoLink**, designed by Jonathan Taylor K1RFD, launches as a successor/competitor to the earlier "iLINK" system, adding PC-softphone-to-repeater linking alongside repeater-to-repeater linking. |
| **2013** | **Yaesu System Fusion** introduced, using proprietary **C4FM** (4-level FSK) digital voice — the third major amateur digital-voice ecosystem alongside D-STAR (2001/2004) and amateur-adapted **DMR** (a commercial land-mobile-radio digital standard developed by Motorola and adopted into ham use around the same period; exact "first amateur DMR" date not independently pinned in this pass — **[UNVERIFIED]**). |
| **Jun 29, 2017** | **FT8** released as part of the WSJT-X software suite by Joe Taylor, **K1JT** (Nobel laureate in Physics, 1993, for the binary pulsar), and Steve Franke, **K9AN** (with Bill Somerville, G4WJS, also credited on WSJT-X development). "FT8" = Franke–Taylor design, 8-FSK modulation. Within about two years FT8 becomes the single most-used digital mode on the amateur bands by spot volume (per PSK Reporter and similar networks). |

### (b) Core technology facts

- **SDR/DSP fundamentals.**
  - *I/Q sampling*: rather than demodulating in analog hardware, an SDR digitizes two mixer outputs 90° apart in phase — the In-phase (I) and Quadrature (Q) baseband components — which together preserve both the amplitude and phase (hence frequency/sign of offset) of the original RF signal, letting all subsequent demodulation, filtering, and mode-decoding happen in software/DSP rather than analog circuitry.
  - *Direct conversion*: an SDR architecture (used e.g. by SDR-1000 and most SoftRock-class kits) that mixes the incoming RF straight down to baseband (0 Hz center) in one step using a local oscillator at the target frequency, rather than through one or more analog intermediate frequencies (IFs) as in a superheterodyne receiver — simpler hardware, but historically more sensitive to DC-offset and LO-leakage artifacts that DSP calibration must correct.
  - *FFT waterfall*: applying a Fast Fourier Transform to successive blocks of sampled baseband/IF data produces an instantaneous spectrum; stacking successive spectra vertically over time produces the now-ubiquitous **waterfall display**, letting an operator visually locate weak or drifting signals across a wide swath of spectrum simultaneously rather than searching serially by ear.
- **PSK31** (BPSK): **31.25 baud** binary phase-shift keying, occupying only ~31 Hz of bandwidth per signal — allowing many simultaneous QSOs in the space one SSB voice signal would need. Characters are encoded with **Varicode** (a variable-length code, conceptually similar to Morse in that common letters get shorter codes), rather than fixed-length ASCII/Baudot, which keeps average throughput reasonable despite the very low baud rate, and — crucially — Varicode's inter-character gaps are represented by a run of zero phase-shifts, so word/character boundaries are self-synchronizing without a separate framing/sync scheme.
- **FT8**: an **8-FSK** (8-tone frequency-shift keying) mode — each of the 79 transmitted symbols carries 3 bits (2³ = 8 tones), with a total occupied bandwidth of about 8 × 6.25 Hz ≈ 50 Hz. The payload (77 information bits + 14-bit CRC = 91 bits) is protected by a **(174,91) LDPC** (low-density parity-check) forward error correction code, producing a 174-bit codeword — powerful FEC plus synchronized, computer-timed 15-second transmit/receive cycles let WSJT-X reliably decode signals reported at roughly **−20 to −24 dB SNR** in a 2500 Hz bandwidth (sources vary in this range depending on whether "a priori"/AP decoding assistance is counted), i.e. copyable well below the noise floor where a human ear could hear nothing at all — the headline claim behind FT8's rapid adoption for weak-signal and marginal-propagation work.
- **Digital voice vocoders.** D-STAR, DMR, and System Fusion all digitize voice with a low-bit-rate **vocoder** (D-STAR and early DMR implementations commonly used the proprietary AMBE/AMBE+2 codec at a few kbit/s) before wrapping it in each system's own framing/FEC and modulating it (D-STAR: GMSK; DMR: 4-level FSK in TDMA 2-slot structure; Fusion: C4FM 4-level FSK) — trading the graceful/analog degradation of FM (weak signal = more noise, but still intelligible) for a comparatively sharp "cliff edge" where a marginal digital signal either decodes cleanly or drops out/garbles, in exchange for markedly better performance (and multiplexing capability, e.g. DMR's 2 voice slots on one 12.5 kHz channel) at moderate-to-good signal levels.
