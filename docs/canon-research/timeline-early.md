# Pinned Timeline: Amateur Radio Eras, 1864–1945

Sourcing: cross-checked against ARRL (arrl.org), Wikipedia, IEEE/ETHW (Engineering and Technology History Wiki), and period-adjacent secondary sources (earlyradiohistory.us, IEEE UFFC history, Columbia University Armstrong archive) via live web search, 2026-07-21. Every disputed or unconfirmed date is flagged inline with **[FLAG]**. Numeric values (wavelengths, frequencies, patent numbers) are given wherever a reliable source stated them.

---

## Prologue: The Physics Before Radio (1864–1900)

### Milestones
- **8 Dec 1864** — James Clerk Maxwell presents "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field" to the Royal Society (published in *Philosophical Transactions*, approved for publication 15 Jun 1865). First unified mathematical treatment of electricity, magnetism, and light as manifestations of one electromagnetic field; predicts wave propagation at a speed matching the known speed of light.
- **1873** — Maxwell publishes *A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism*, the canonical, complete statement of what became known as Maxwell's equations.
- **1887–1888** — Heinrich Hertz, at Karlsruhe, builds a spark-excited dipole transmitter (two 30 cm zinc spheres) and a resonant loop receiver (7.5 cm diameter, its own spark gap) and experimentally generates and detects electromagnetic waves, confirming Maxwell's prediction. Over 1887–88 he also demonstrates reflection, refraction, polarization, and finite propagation speed — the first fully experimental proof of EM waves.
- **Late 1894 / 1895** — Guglielmo Marconi begins wireless telegraphy experiments at Villa Griffone, his family's estate at Pontecchio, near Bologna, Italy, combining a Hertzian spark oscillator with a Branly-type coherer detector.
- **Summer 1895** — Marconi, having added a grounded antenna (a major innovation beyond Hertz's balanced dipole), extends his range to roughly 1.5 miles (~2–2.4 km), including transmission over an intervening hill — proof that wireless could work well beyond line-of-sight lab conditions.
- **12 Dec 1901** — Marconi and assistant George Kemp, at Signal Hill, St. John's, Newfoundland, report hearing the Morse letter "S" (three dots), sent from Marconi's Poldhu, Cornwall station ~3,500 km (2,000 mi) away, using a kite-suspended wire antenna and telephone receiver. Widely reported as the first transatlantic wireless signal. **[FLAG]** — this claim was disputed even at the time (contemporary propagation theory could not explain reception at that range/frequency) and by later historians, who note no independent third-party confirmation and the possibility the "signal" was atmospheric noise. Marconi's first *rigorously documented, independently verifiable* transatlantic transmission is generally dated to later tests (Glace Bay, Nova Scotia ↔ Poldhu, 1902). Treat 12 Dec 1901 as "claimed/traditionally cited," not ironclad.

### Tech to teach
- **c = f · λ** — the wave-speed relation is the single load-bearing formula of the whole book: wavelength in meters and frequency are reciprocally linked through the (constant) speed of light. Keep this equation visible whenever the prose switches between "meters" language (period convention) and "kHz/MHz" language (modern convention).
- **Resonance and the LC tank circuit** — an inductor and capacitor exchanging energy at a natural (resonant) frequency is the basic building block of every tuned circuit that follows, from Hertz's dipole to a 1945 transmitter's tank coil.
- **Hertz's apparatus was, functionally, a damped LC oscillator** radiating via a dipole, received by a second resonant (tuned) loop — establishing the tuning/resonance principle a full generation before it become an engineering discipline.
- **Coherer detector** (Édouard Branly) — a tube of metal filings that clump together (drop in resistance) under RF excitation; a crude, non-linear, binary (on/off) detector that must be mechanically "tapped" to reset between signals. No amplification, no continuous-signal fidelity — fine for on/off Morse keying, useless for anything more refined.
- **Marconi's grounded antenna / earth return** — a key practical departure from Hertz's symmetric lab dipole, and the ancestor of the ground-plane monopole antenna concept used throughout amateur radio.
- **No modulation yet** — everything in this era is on/off keying (presence or absence of a damped-wave carrier over time); there is no concept yet of impressing an audio waveform on a carrier.

## Chapter 1: The Spark Era (1900–1917)

### Milestones
- **1901–1912** — An unlicensed, unregulated amateur wireless boom sweeps the US; interference disputes escalate between amateurs, the Navy, and commercial operators (including allegations of amateurs sending fake distress calls and forged naval orders). **[FLAG]** — no reliable numeric estimate of the pre-1912 unlicensed amateur population could be verified; only the post-Act figure (below) is solid.
- **14–15 Apr 1912** — RMS *Titanic* strikes an iceberg at 11:40 p.m. (14th) and sinks at 2:20 a.m. (15th); wireless operators Jack Phillips and Harold Bride send CQD, then also SOS (the first documented US use of SOS by name recognition, per most sources); spectrum congestion/amateur interference is blamed — rightly or not — for garbling nearby rescue communications and becomes the political trigger for federal regulation (a bill was already pending before the disaster; *Titanic* accelerated its passage).
- **13 Aug 1912** — The Radio Act of 1912 ("An Act to Regulate Radio Communication") is signed by President Taft; takes effect 13 Dec 1912. It mandates federal licensing of all radio operators and stations and restricts amateur stations to wavelengths **below 200 meters** (i.e., frequencies **above 1500 kHz**) — a band 1912-era engineers widely believed was useless for long-distance work, expecting amateurs to abandon radio once confined there. (Individual amateurs could still apply for special licenses on longer, 200–600 m wavelengths for relay work.)
- **6 Apr 1914** — The American Radio Relay League is founded in Hartford, Connecticut, by Hiram Percy Maxim (callsign 1AW) and Clarence D. Tuska, secretary of the Hartford Radio Club. By September 1914 the roster already lists over 230 stations.
- **6 Apr 1917** — The US declares war on Germany; the Department of Commerce immediately orders **all** amateur transmitting and receiving stations shut down and antennas dismantled — a total ban, for the duration of US involvement in WWI. (A pointed coincidence: this falls exactly three years, to the day, after ARRL's founding.)

### Tech to teach
- **Spark-gap transmitter / LC "tank" circuit** — a charged capacitor dumps its energy through a spark gap into a tuned LC circuit, radiating a train of exponentially decaying ("damped") oscillations, not a continuous wave.
- **Damped-wave spectrum is inherently broad/dirty** — each spark pulse's decaying oscillation occupies significant bandwidth compared to a pure sine wave; this — not just operator misbehavior — is a root technical cause of the era's chronic mutual interference.
- **Detectors**: the coherer gives way to passive rectifying **crystal detectors** (galena "cat's-whisker," commercialized from ~1906 by Greenleaf Whittier Pickard) — more sensitive and reliable than the coherer, but still passive, with no gain.
- **Q (quality factor)** of a resonant circuit determines selectivity (how sharply it discriminates one frequency from a neighbor); the era's spark circuits were fundamentally low-Q, another root cause of interference and the technical logic behind eventually mandating narrower-bandwidth CW.
- **Ground-wave propagation** dominates at the very long wavelengths (thousands of meters) favored by commercial/military stations of the day; the received wisdom of the time held that shorter waves (200 m and below) were commercially "useless" for distance — a belief the amateurs confined there would spectacularly overturn in the 1920s.
- **Numeric anchor**: 200 m ⇔ 1500 kHz (1.5 MHz), via λ(m) = 300 / f(MHz). Keep this conversion consistent whenever the prose invokes "the 200-meter law."

## Chapter 2: The Shortwave Revolution (1919–1927)

### Milestones
- **~1 Oct 1919** — Amateur transmitting privileges begin to be restored once the Navy relinquishes its wartime control of radio, following the Armistice. **[FLAG]** — sources vary on the exact granularity: some cite 1 Oct 1919 as the date transmitting resumed outright; others describe an October 1919 Post Office announcement restoring *receiving* licenses first, with full transmitting privileges following in November 1919. Treat "~October 1919" as the safe, sourced approximation; do not commit to a single hard calendar date without a primary-source citation.
- **6 Oct 1914** — Edwin H. Armstrong is granted US Patent 1,113,149 for the regenerative ("feedback") circuit (conceived as a Columbia undergraduate in 1912). Priority became one of the most bitter disputes in radio-patent history: Lee De Forest patented a similar circuit in 1916, and after decades of litigation the US Supreme Court ruled in De Forest's favor in 1934 — a legal outcome most engineers of the day, and since, regarded as technically wrong (Armstrong is still generally credited as the true inventor in engineering histories).
- **Aug–Sep 1918 (invented) / 8 Jun 1920 (patented)** — Armstrong, serving with the US Army Signal Corps in France, develops the superheterodyne receiver (application filed 8 Feb 1919; Patent 1,342,885 issued 8 Jun 1920).
- **Dec 1921** — ARRL's Transatlantic Tests: receiver expert Paul Godley (2ZE) travels to Ardrossan, Scotland with a purpose-built receiver to listen for US amateur signals on shortwave. The first US station heard is 1BCG (Greenwich, CT); its transmission was logged at 2152 UTC. **[FLAG]** — sources give the calendar date of this first reception inconsistently as either 11 Dec or 12 Dec 1921 (an artifact of UTC/GMT vs. local-time bookkeeping across the transatlantic test literature); use "December 1921" in prose and verify the exact day against a primary QST/ARRL source before printing a specific date in a pull-quote. Godley ultimately logged signals from more than two dozen US stations, proving that shortwave signals could cross the Atlantic on amateur-scale power — a direct rebuttal of the 1912-era assumption that the 200-meter band was worthless.
- **27 Nov 1923** — First two-way transatlantic amateur contact: Léon Deloy (French station 8AB) works Fred Schnell (1MO) and John Reinartz (1XAM/1XAL), both in Connecticut, on a specially authorized wavelength of **110 meters**, CW; the Schnell/Deloy QSO ran nearly two hours, with Reinartz and Deloy making contact separately the same night.
- **23 Feb 1927** — The Radio Act of 1927 is signed by President Coolidge, creating the Federal Radio Commission (FRC) — five commissioners over five zones — with licensing authority and a "public interest, convenience, or necessity" standard. Driven primarily by the chaos of the 1920s broadcasting boom, which the 1912 Act's framework could not handle.
- **4 Oct 1927** — The International Radiotelegraph Conference (Washington, DC) formally allocates amateur bands on a worldwide, treaty basis, including the wavelengths that become the 80/75, 40, 20, and 10 meter bands, plus international callsign-prefix blocks.

### Tech to teach
- **The triode vacuum tube** (Lee De Forest's Audion, 1906) is the enabling active device for this entire era — the first practical amplifying/oscillating element.
- **Regeneration** — feeding a portion of the amplified plate-circuit signal back to the grid in phase boosts both gain and selectivity, and, pushed further, drives the tube into self-oscillation (making it usable as either a highly sensitive receiver stage or a CW transmitter/oscillator).
- **CW (continuous wave) vs. damped spark** — CW is a pure, un-decaying sine-wave carrier, on/off keyed; it occupies dramatically narrower bandwidth than spark and, critically, is inaudible on its own — it must be heterodyned against a locally generated tone to be heard, which is why...
- **Heterodyne principle / BFO (beat-frequency oscillator)** — mixing the incoming CW carrier with a local oscillator signal produces an audible beat note at the difference frequency; the conceptual root (Reginald Fessenden, ~1901) of both CW reception and, more importantly...
- **The superheterodyne receiver** — the incoming RF signal is mixed with a local oscillator (LO) to produce a fixed intermediate frequency (IF), which is then amplified and filtered before final detection. This decouples selectivity/gain from the (varying) tuned signal frequency, avoiding the instability of trying to get high gain directly at RF — the architectural leap that makes the rest of receiver history possible.
- **MOPA (master-oscillator power-amplifier)** — isolating a low-power, frequency-stable oscillator stage from the power-amplifier stage that actually drives the antenna, preventing the transmitted frequency from "pulling" under load or antenna-coupling changes (a chronic problem with simple self-excited oscillators).
- **Hartley oscillator** (Ralph Hartley, patented 1915) and **Colpitts oscillator** (Edwin Colpitts, ~1918) — the two canonical LC feedback-oscillator topologies (tapped-inductor feedback vs. tapped-capacitor feedback) that underlie most 1920s amateur transmitters.
- **Ionospheric skip and the skip zone** — shortwave signals refract back to earth off an ionized upper-atmosphere layer (hypothesized as the Kennelly–Heaviside layer from 1902, experimentally confirmed by Edward Appleton, 1924–1926), letting them travel far beyond line-of-sight/ground-wave range; between the ground wave's practical limit and the first sky-wave return lies a **skip zone** with no reception at all. This is the technical explanation for the era's central irony: the "useless" sub-200-meter band mandated in 1912 turned out to be the best band on earth for global DX.

## Chapter 3: The Golden Age of Tubes (1928–1936)

### Milestones
- **1927–1929** — HF amateur bands settle into the structure still recognizable today: 160, 80/75, 40, 20, and 10 meters, following the Oct 1927 international conference; a 5-meter band (56–64 MHz) had already been opened domestically on 10 Oct 1924 (Third National Radio Conference) and was allocated internationally (56–60 MHz) at the same Oct 1927 conference.
- **1923** — George Washington Pierce (Harvard physics professor) develops the Pierce crystal oscillator, replacing the inductor of a Colpitts circuit with a quartz crystal for far higher frequency stability (much higher effective Q than an LC tank). QST publishes its first crystal-controlled-transmitter construction schematic in **July 1924**; four more schematics follow in 1925, nine in 1926, nine more in 1927 — a rapid mid/late-1920s uptake among hams that, per IEEE UFFC's own history, helped seed the entire US commercial quartz-crystal industry. **[FLAG]** — no verifiable date or regulation was found mandating crystal control for amateur transmitters; treat crystal control in this era as *rapidly-adopted best practice driven by frequency-stability rules and peer pressure*, not a hard legal cutover, unless a specific FRC order can be sourced.
- **1929 → 1936** — US licensed amateur count grows from 16,829 to 46,850 despite the Depression.
- **19 Jun 1934** — The Communications Act of 1934 is signed at President Franklin D. Roosevelt's request, creating the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), which absorbs and supersedes the FRC (1927) and consolidates wire (telephone/telegraph) and wireless regulation under one body; it incorporates much of the 1927 Act, including the "public interest, convenience, or necessity" standard.

### Tech to teach
- **Superheterodyne becomes the dominant receiver architecture** of this era, displacing simpler TRF (tuned-radio-frequency) designs for both broadcast and amateur equipment.
- **Image frequency** — the superhet's structural weakness: a spurious response at (LO ± IF) on the opposite side of the local oscillator from the wanted signal, which a poorly selective front end will let straight through; managing image rejection (front-end tuning, IF choice) is a defining design problem of the period.
- **IF (intermediate frequency)** — the fixed frequency, after the mixer stage, where most of a superhet's gain and selectivity is concentrated; typical era IFs for consumer/amateur sets were commonly in roughly the 175–500 kHz range (values rose in later decades).
- **AGC (automatic gain control)** — feedback from the detector/IF stage that automatically reduces receiver gain on strong signals; commercialized in the late 1920s and standard in "modern" superhets by the mid-1930s, smoothing out fading and preventing strong-signal overload/blasting.
- **AM (amplitude modulation) via plate modulation** — an audio signal varies the high-voltage plate supply of the RF power-amplifier stage; the result is a carrier plus two sidebands, with total occupied bandwidth ≈ 2× the highest audio frequency being transmitted (e.g., ~6 kHz total for 3 kHz audio) — directly relevant to how tightly this era's bands could be packed with phone stations.
- **Pentode tubes** (commercialized ~1927–1930, pioneered by Philips/Tellegen) add a screen grid and suppressor grid beyond the triode, giving much higher gain and greatly reducing (though not eliminating) the need for neutralization.
- **Neutralization** — a deliberate feedback-cancelling circuit (typically a small capacitive bridge) required in triode (and, to a lesser extent, tetrode) RF power amplifiers to cancel the tube's internal plate-to-grid capacitance and prevent parasitic self-oscillation; a routine, non-optional part of building an RF power stage in this era.
- **Pierce crystal oscillator** — the quartz crystal replaces the inductor in a Colpitts-type feedback oscillator, giving dramatically higher frequency stability than any LC tank and becoming the era's gold standard for a stable, "on-frequency" ham transmitter.

## Chapter 4: Eve of War (1937–1945)

### Milestones
- **26 Dec 1933** — Edwin Armstrong is granted four foundational wideband-FM patents (filed 1930–1933) simultaneously.
- **5 Nov 1935** — Armstrong gives his first public demonstration of wideband FM broadcasting, before an audience of radio engineers (IRE) in New York City, dramatically showing reduced static/noise versus AM.
- **1936–1937** — Engineers from the Yankee Network (New England) attend an Armstrong FM demonstration and begin planning a regional FM relay system (envisioning a 50 kW station atop Mount Asnebumskit near Paxton, MA, plus 5 kW relay stations on Mount Washington, NH and Mount Mansfield, VT).
- **7 Dec 1941** — The attack on Pearl Harbor.
- **8 Dec 1941 (per some sources) / 11 Dec 1941 (per FCC-adjacent sources)** — The FCC orders (Order No. 87, per several secondary sources) the immediate suspension of **all** amateur radio operation in the continental US and its territories/possessions "for the duration," with exceptions only for specifically authorized national-defense-related operation. **[FLAG]** — the exact issuance date (8 Dec vs. 11 Dec 1941) is inconsistent across the secondary sources checked; the order number ("87") likewise appears in only some sources and should be confirmed against a primary FCC/Federal Register citation before being stated as fact in the manuscript.
- **Jun 1942** — The War Emergency Radio Service (WERS) is established by the FCC at ARRL's urging, as the only legal outlet for licensed-amateur participation for the rest of the war. WERS operated on VHF only — **112–116 MHz and 219–225 MHz** (i.e., roughly the "2½-meter" band) — using FM, not AM. Licenses were issued to communities/civil-defense organizations, not to individuals, and only licensed amateurs were eligible to operate; by the end of 1944 roughly 5,000 transmitters were active under about 250 community licenses.

### Tech to teach
- **Class-C amplifier operation** — the RF power-amplifier stage is biased well beyond cutoff, conducting for less than half (well under 180°) of each RF cycle; high efficiency (roughly 60–80%+) at the cost of gross nonlinearity, making it ideal for constant-envelope modes (CW, FM) and requiring extra care when used for AM plate modulation. The workhorse PA class of the era's transmitters.
- **Pi-network (π-network) output matching** — an L-into-pi tank configuration between the PA plate and the antenna feedline, able to match a wide range of antenna impedances while also providing harmonic filtering; this period sees it displace simpler link-coupled tank circuits as the standard ham-transmitter output stage.
- **Crystal (IF) filters** — quartz-crystal-based bandpass filters inserted into a superhet's IF strip for very sharp, high-Q selectivity; a significant late-1930s/wartime-era advance for weak-signal CW work, and a direct forerunner of postwar mechanical and crystal-ladder filters.
- **Yagi (Yagi–Uda) beam antennas** — parasitic director/reflector element arrays, invented in Japan (Shintaro Uda, 1926, with Hidetsugu Yagi; first English-language description in Yagi's 1928 *Proc. IRE* paper "Beam Transmissions of Ultra Short Waves"). Real amateur uptake of Yagi beams accelerates in this period, especially as the shorter VHF wavelengths (5 m / 2½ m) make a directional array of practical physical size.
- **HF skip vs. VHF line-of-sight** — the late-1930s/wartime push into VHF (the 5-meter, 56–60 MHz band, and the WERS-era ~2½-meter/112 MHz band) forces a fundamentally different propagation model on operators: near-line-of-sight VHF coverage rather than the long-haul ionospheric skip that defines HF. (Tropospheric scattering/ducting as a formal explanation for anomalous VHF propagation is understood mostly in the postwar period — avoid projecting that framework backward onto pre-1945 operators.)
- **FM (frequency modulation) fundamentals** — information is carried by varying the carrier's *frequency* (not its amplitude) in proportion to the modulating signal. Armstrong's wideband implementation trades occupied bandwidth for dramatically improved noise/static immunity and exhibits capture-effect/noise-quieting behavior fundamentally unlike AM. **[FLAG — scope caution]**: through 1945, FM is overwhelmingly a *broadcast*-era technology (Armstrong's demonstrations, the Yankee Network) plus the specific case of WERS's VHF FM traffic; do not portray general amateur-band FM adoption as an accomplished fact before 1945 — that is substantially a postwar development.

## Cross-cutting numeric anchors (for consistency across chapters)
- **c = f · λ**, with c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s (the working ham approximation) or 299,792,458 m/s (exact).
- **λ(m) = 300 / f(MHz)** — the quick-conversion form used throughout: 200 m ⇔ 1.5 MHz; 110 m ⇔ ~2.73 MHz; 80 m ⇔ 3.75 MHz (band-center approx.); 40 m ⇔ 7.5 MHz; 20 m ⇔ 15 MHz; 10 m ⇔ 30 MHz; 5 m ⇔ 60 MHz; 2½ m ⇔ 120 MHz (close to the WERS 112–116 MHz allocation).
- Patent numbers cited above (Armstrong regeneration: US 1,113,149; Armstrong superheterodyne: US 1,342,885) are worth spot-checking against the USPTO/Google Patents record before print if used as pull-quotes.
