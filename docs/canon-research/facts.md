# Canon Research: Fact-Check Findings

Four flagged questions resolved via ARRL, FCC, and secondary ham-history sources. Dates below are the best-supported values found; where sources genuinely conflict, both readings are given with the more strongly corroborated one flagged as primary.

---

## 1. Restoration of US amateur TRANSMITTING after the WWI blackout

**Resolved value: October 1, 1919.**

Receiving/listening privileges were restored first, effective April 15, 1919, per a Navy Department order ("effective April 15, 1919, all restrictions are removed on the use of radio receiving stations"), while transmitting stations remained banned "as soon as the President proclaims that a state of peace exists." Transmitting privileges were restored separately, later that year: "Although the transmitting ban on amateur stations wasn't lifted until October 1, 1919, restrictions on private and amateur listening ended on April 15th of that year." A supplement to the October 1919 *QST* issue announced "BAN OFF." One secondary source (ARRL centennial article) instead gives "November 1919" for when "amateur radio operating privileges were not restored," which appears to be a looser/rounded reference to the same fall-1919 reopening rather than a competing precise date — the October 1, 1919 date is the one carried by the more detailed primary-adjacent account and is the generally cited figure in amateur-radio histories.

- Source: [Amateur Radio After World War One (1919–1925), earlyradiohistory.us](https://earlyradiohistory.us/sec015.htm) — "the transmitting ban on amateur stations wasn't lifted until October 1, 1919"
- Source: [Amateur Radio Restored (1919), earlyradiohistory.us](https://earlyradiohistory.us/1919ama.htm) — Navy Dept. order restoring receiving April 15, 1919, transmitting still banned pending peace proclamation
- Cross-reference (looser date): [ARRL — Centennial of Amateur Radio Blackout for World War I](https://www.arrl.org/news/centennial-of-amateur-radio-blackout-for-world-war-i-occurs-on-april-6) — "restored until November 1919"

**Confidence:** High for October 1, 1919 as the specific date carried in the more detailed sources; note the minor October-vs-November ambiguity in secondary ARRL material above.

## 2. US availability dates of the three WARC-79 bands (30 m, 17 m, 12 m)

WARC-79 (Geneva) created worldwide allocations for these three bands, entering into force internationally January 1, 1982. US amateur access came later and on three separate dates:

- **30 meters (10.100–10.150 MHz): October 28, 1982.** FCC approved the band for US amateur use on this date. *(Note: US 30 m access is secondary — amateurs there operate on a shared, non-interference basis with fixed stations and are limited to CW/digital modes, unlike the other WARC bands.)*
- **12 meters (24.890–24.990 MHz): 1985.** Multiple sources consistently give the year 1985; **the exact month/day could not be confirmed** from the sources available in this pass — flagged as unresolved to day-level precision.
- **17 meters (18.068–18.168 MHz): January 1989**, via FCC Docket 88-467. The Report and Order (FCC 89-18) was **adopted January 27, 1989** and **released January 31, 1989**; secondary sources describe amateur access as granted "January 30, 1989." Use "released January 31, 1989" as the citable FCC-document date; the operative day-of-grant is given inconsistently across secondary sources (Jan 30 vs Jan 31) and was not independently confirmed against the primary FCC text in this pass.

- Source: [WARC bands, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WARC_bands) — band designations, 1979 WARC origin, "opened for use in the early 1980s"
- Source: [FCC — Amendment of the rules to permit use of the 17 meter band by the amateur radio services](https://www.fcc.gov/document/amendment-rules-permit-use-17-meter-band-amateur) — Docket 88-467
- Source aggregating the three dates (secondary, not independently verified against FCC primary text for 30 m/12 m): search-aggregated ham-history summaries citing "FCC approved the 30-meter band ... on October 28, 1982, followed by the 12-meter band ... in 1985 and the 17-meter band ... on January 31, 1989"

**Confidence:** High for 30 m (Oct 28, 1982) and for 17 m being January 1989 (exact day ambiguous between Jan 30/31). **Low/unconfirmed for the 12 m exact date** — only the year 1985 could be corroborated.

## 3. "Incentive licensing" — origin, docket, effective date, and controversy

**Resolved value, with an important nuance the flat "FCC-initiated, not an ARRL program" framing oversimplifies:**

- It was implemented via formal FCC rulemaking: **Docket No. 15928**, "Amendment of the Amateur Service Rules to Provide for Incentive Licensing and Distinctive Call Signs, Report and Order," released **August 29, 1967** (FCC 67-978), published in the Federal Register September 1, 1967, and reported at 9 FCC 2d 814. Only the FCC has legal authority to set license-class privileges, so the binding regulatory act was unambiguously the FCC's.
- The rule changes were **phased in over roughly two years**; several sources (including ac6v.com's amateur radio history timeline) explicitly cite **"Effective Nov. 22, 1968"** for the core subband reallocation between General/Conditional and Advanced/Extra classes. Other sources place the first phase (reopening the Advanced class to new applicants) at November 22, 1967, with full phase-in completing around November 22, 1969. November 22, 1968 is well-attested as *a* — and likely *the* most-cited — effective date within that phased schedule, corresponding to the point most amateurs experienced the bulk of the privilege changes.
- **Origin is genuinely contested, not clean-cut.** ARRL first proposed a version of "incentive licensing" itself, in an October 1963 FCC filing (reinstating the Advanced class and phasing out General-class HF phone privileges), and continued to support some form of it through the FCC's own 1965 rulemaking proposal. The FCC's adopted 1967 rule differed from ARRL's original proposal, and the two are often described (e.g., by Wikipedia's "Amateur radio licensing in the United States" article) as having "developed" the program together starting in 1964. So: **the regulatory mechanism was purely an FCC action (Docket 15928)** — ARRL had no unilateral rulemaking power — **but ARRL was not a bystander**; it proposed the underlying concept and advocated for it, which is precisely why much of the community's anger during and after the fact was directed at ARRL leadership as well as at the FCC. Describing it as simply "FCC-initiated, not an ARRL program" understates ARRL's role; describing it as "an ARRL program" overstates ARRL's authority, since the ARRL cannot issue or change licensing rules.
- **Contentiousness is well confirmed.** It "effectively [stopped] growth of Amateur Radio and caus[ed] a lot of bad feelings among the amateur community," with resentment described as persisting "over a half century" later in some quarters.

- Source: [A History of Amateur Radio License Changes, Eastern Massachusetts ARRL](https://ema.arrl.org/a-history-of-amateur-radio-license-changes/) — docket citation (9 FCC 2d 814 (1968)), phased effect, ARRL proposal origin, community backlash
- Source: [ac6v.com — Amateur Radio History](https://ac6v.com/history.php) — "Effective Nov. 22, 1968 Incentive Licensing plan," 1967 FCC announcement, two-year phase-out of General/Conditional HF phone privileges
- Source: [Amateur radio licensing in the United States, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amateur_radio_licensing_in_the_United_States) — "In 1964, the FCC and the American Radio Relay League (ARRL) developed a program known as 'Incentive Licensing' ... It did not take effect until 1968"; Advanced class reopened "by exam, on November 22, 1967"
- Source: [Incentive Licensing: 1969, OneTubeRadio.com](https://onetuberadio.com/2019/11/23/incentive-licensing-1969/) — ongoing resentment, first phase in November 1968

**Confidence:** High that Docket 15928 is the correct FCC docket and that the program was contentious. High that November 22, 1968 is a real, well-cited effective date within the phased rollout — but flagged that it is one date within a multi-year phase-in (1967–1969), not a single clean "effective date," and that the "not an ARRL program" framing needs the origin nuance above rather than a flat confirmation.

## 4. IARU founding

**Resolved value: April 17, 1925, Paris — Hiram Percy Maxim (1AW/u1AW) elected first International President.**

The International Amateur Radio Union was formed when delegates of 23 nations met at the Faculté des Sciences in Paris for the First International Amateur Radio Congress; the constitution was adopted April 17, 1925, with the congress running April 14–18 and a closing plenary on April 18 (ARRL's own account: "The International Amateur Radio Union, the dream of years, came into existence on April 17, 1925, when the delegates of twenty-three nations met at the Faculte des Sciences in Paris" and "Our A.R.R.L. president, Mr. Hiram Percy Maxim, u1AW, was elected International President."). Preliminary planning had taken place a year earlier, in March 1924, when Maxim met in Paris with radio amateurs from France, Great Britain, Belgium, Switzerland, Italy, Spain, Luxembourg, Canada, and the USA to lay groundwork for an international organization. "April 1925" as a general reference and "April 17, 1925" as the precise founding date are both accurate — April 18 is separately marked as World Amateur Radio Day, commemorating the date the IARU's constitution took effect / the congress concluded.

- Source: [ARRL — World Amateur Radio Day: International Amateur Radio Union Formed 100 Years Ago](http://www.arrl.org/news/view/world-amateur-radio-day-international-amateur-radio-union-formed-100-years-ago) — "came into existence on April 17, 1925 ... Faculte des Sciences in Paris"; Maxim "elected International President"
- Source: [International Amateur Radio Union, Engineering and Technology History Wiki (ETHW)](https://ethw.org/International_Amateur_Radio_Union) — corroborating account of the 1925 Paris congress and Maxim's role (page could not be re-fetched in this pass due to a 403; content triangulated via search-result summary only)

**Confidence:** High — multiple ARRL-sourced accounts agree on April 17, 1925, Paris, and Maxim as first president.

## Summary of unresolved items

- **12-meter WARC band:** exact US availability date not confirmed beyond the year **1985** (month/day unresolved).
- **Incentive licensing "effective date":** November 22, 1968 is well-attested but is one point in a multi-year (1967–1969) phased rollout, not a single unambiguous effective date; the "not an ARRL program" framing is an oversimplification — ARRL proposed the concept in 1963 and helped develop it with the FCC from 1964, even though only the FCC could actually promulgate the binding rule (Docket 15928, R&O released Aug 29, 1967).
- **17-meter exact grant day:** sources split between January 30 and January 31, 1989 (R&O adopted Jan 27, released Jan 31, 1989); not independently resolved against FCC primary text.
